Add The Dollar Auction misfit (Shubik's all-pay escalation). A prize of known worth is auctioned under one added rule: the runner-up, not only the winner, forfeits their last bid. So whoever is second faces losing their stake for nothing, and bidding just past the leader, to win the prize and escape the forfeit, is the smaller loss, a sound forward-looking choice at every price. Because there is always a one who is second, two reasonable bidders climb past the prize toward ruin: the winner overpays, the loser pays for nothing, and the auctioneer, who offered a transparent game and bid nothing, collects both stakes. No swindle and no fallacy: each raise is correct, and the all-pay rule leaves no floor beneath it, so being right at every step is exactly what ruins them, and the only winning move is not to enter. Warranted on Shubik (1971), Teger (Too Much Invested to Quit, 1980), O'Neill (1986, the game-theoretic solution), Leininger (1989), and Maynard Smith (1974, the war of attrition, the all-pay kin); cut explicitly against Good Money After Bad (the sunk-cost fallacy, backward-looking and by one actor, where this escalation is forward-looking and rational and needs two) and The Winning Bid (the winner's curse from a misjudged common value, where here the prize's value is known exactly and the overpayment is the rule's doing, not an estimation error). Sixth target of the warrant-mine slate.
