# The Lowest Bar: Reference

The research warrant for [The Lowest Bar](play_the_lowest_bar.md).
The dynamic is the race to the bottom in regulatory competition: Cary's account of states competing to charter
corporations by lowering the protections owed to shareholders, Winter's race-to-the-top counter, Romano's
empirical debate over which race is run, and Tiebout's mobility engine, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the race to the bottom as a **mobile-base structure**: a base that can move sits where the standard costs
it least, so each jurisdiction lowers its own standard to attract or keep the base and the franchise revenue it
brings, a rival that holds a higher standard loses the base and must match or undercut, and the standard ratchets
down toward the lowest, a floor no jurisdiction chose and none can lift alone. The play models the documented
mechanics, the standard that is at once a cost to the mobile base and a protection to the immobile party, the
mobility that turns the standard from a rule a jurisdiction sets into a bid it must win, the franchise revenue
that makes lowering the bar worth money, the descent in which each cut reads as competitiveness while the sum is a
level no one wanted, and the harm externalised onto the third parties the standard protected and who cannot follow
the base out, as the load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of any jurisdiction's bad faith. The
governing finding of the sources is the play's governing law: where a mobile base chooses its jurisdiction and its
revenue follows, no jurisdiction can hold a standard higher than its rivals without losing the base, so a standard
whose cost the base would rather not bear is competed downward, each lowering locally rational and the descent
authored by no one.

The direction of the race, however, is not fixed, and the honest boundary is carried as carefully as the effect:
the race runs **down** only where the mobile chooser's interest diverges from the party who bears the standard's
cost. Where the chooser itself internalises the harm of a lax standard, the same competition can raise the bar, a
race to the **top**, because a jurisdiction that offers a laxer standard then offers the chooser a worse deal, not
a better one (Winter; Romano on the empirical dispute over which race Delaware actually runs). The race to the
bottom is therefore an externality condition and not a universal law of competition: it bites specifically where
the lowered standard the chooser prefers externalises its harm onto immobile third parties. The structural test
still holds within that condition: the descent runs regardless of which officials set the standards, driven by the
mobility of the base and the competition for it, not by any jurisdiction's malice.

It is distinct from a tragedy-of-the-commons production, and the two are close enough that the cut must be exact:
there many users draw down a single shared depletable stock, each taking a unit that subtracts from a common pool,
and the loss is the pool exhausted; here nothing shared is depleted, each jurisdiction lowers its **own** standard
to attract a mobile base, and the harm lands not on a common stock but on immobile third parties who bear a
protection removed. It is distinct from a winner's-curse or auction production, where bidders overpay to win a
prize and the winner is the one who most overvalued it: there the competition is buyers bidding a price **up** and
the winner overpays, here the competition is sellers of standards bidding their standards **down** and the winner
keeps the mobile base by offering it the least protection, the harm falling not on the winner but on parties
outside the transaction entirely.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast any jurisdiction as
  corrupt or any base as a bad actor, because the source finding is precisely that no one errs: the base moves
  lawfully to the terms that cost it least, each jurisdiction lowers rationally to keep a base that would otherwise
  leave, and each lowering is a defensive answer to a rival's. The descent is authored by no one and emerges from
  the mobility of the base and the competition for it, so the misfit is that a jurisdiction which keeps a higher
  standard loses the base and the revenue to one that keeps it lower, and no jurisdiction can hold the bar high
  while the base can find it low elsewhere.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a costless correction. The one move that stops the
  descent is a common floor, a minimum below which no jurisdiction may cut, and it means every jurisdiction
  forgoing the base it could win by quietly cutting below the line, holding a floor that never enforces itself and
  that any party can profit by breaking. The reckoning is a standard held only by paying, continuously, the price
  of not undercutting a rival to win the base back, against every standing incentive to defect, not a free
  correction.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never claimed as
  the house's. The staging is the house's; the race to the bottom in chartering and the franchise-tax incentive
  are Cary's, the race-to-the-top counter where the mobile party bears the cost is Winter's, the empirical dispute
  over which race is run is Romano's, and the mobility engine of jurisdictional competition is Tiebout's. The
  honest boundary, that the direction of the race turns on who bears the standard's cost and that the descent is an
  externality condition rather than a law of competition, is carried as carefully as the effect itself.
