# Play: The Lowest Bar

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

The Lowest Bar.
A jurisdiction sets a standard, and it is a reasonable thing to set: how much a company must disclose, how a crew must be treated, how much capital a firm must hold against its promises, the protection the standard exists to give to someone who is counting on it. And under that standard sits a base: a charter, a flag, a firm, a pool of capital, and the base can move, it is free to register wherever it likes, and it will sit where the standard costs it least. And here is the thing about a base that can move: it does not have to accept the standard where it is, it can carry itself and the revenue it pays to whichever jurisdiction asks the least of it, so a jurisdiction that wants the base, and wants the franchise revenue the base brings with it, has a plain and lawful way to attract it, which is to lower the standard the base would rather not meet. And lowering it works: the registrations come, the fees come, the jurisdiction's revenue rises, and by every number the jurisdiction watches, this reads as a win, competitiveness, a place that is good for business, open, attractive, so the jurisdiction lowers, and it is right to, because the base is mobile and the revenue is real and the lowering brings both. And the rival across the border sees the base leave, or sees it about to, and the rival had held a higher standard, and now the rival must choose: keep the standard and lose the base to the laxer neighbour, or match the neighbour and keep the base. And matching is the rational move, because a standard no base will sit under protects no one anyway, so the rival lowers too, and then the first jurisdiction lowers again to stay ahead, and each lowering is a local win and each is answered by the next, and the standard ratchets down, not because any jurisdiction wants it low but because none can hold it high while the base can move to whoever holds it lower. And the standard protected someone: the shareholders the disclosure rule guarded, the crew the safety rule guarded, the public the capital rule guarded, and these are the ones who cannot move, who cannot follow the base out, who hold the shares or crew the ship or live in the country while the base carries its revenue elsewhere and leaves them the lowered standard. So the harm of the descent falls not on the base that chose the jurisdiction, and not on the jurisdiction that lowered the standard, both of whom gain, but on the immobile third party the standard existed to protect, who never chose the jurisdiction and cannot leave it. Nobody was a villain. No jurisdiction acted in bad faith, each lowering was a rational answer to a rival's, each base moved lawfully to better terms, and every step read as competitiveness. It is only that a base free to move sits where the standard costs it least, and a jurisdiction that keeps a higher standard loses the base and the revenue to one that keeps it lower, so no jurisdiction can hold the bar high while the base can find it low elsewhere, and the floor the descent settles at is the one no one chose and none can lift alone, the standard everyone would rather was higher and the competition will not let stay there.

## Arc

The bend is this: a base that can move sits where the standard costs it least, so each jurisdiction lowers its own standard to attract or keep the base and its revenue, a rival that holds a higher standard loses the base and must match or undercut, and the standard ratchets down toward the lowest, a floor no one chose, the harm falling on the immobile third party the standard protected.
It runs on a base that is free to move.
A mobile base is not bound to the standard where it sits: a charter, a flag, a firm, a pool of capital can register wherever it likes and will sit where the standard asks the least of it, so the choice of jurisdiction is itself a choice of standard (Tiebout, on mobility and jurisdictional competition, the mobile party sorting to the jurisdiction whose terms suit it best). The mobility is lawful and ordinary, and it is what makes the standard a thing the jurisdictions must compete over rather than simply set.
And each jurisdiction competes for the base because the base brings revenue. A jurisdiction that holds the base collects the franchise fees the base pays, so attracting or keeping the base is worth money, and the plain lawful way to attract a base that dislikes a standard is to lower the standard, so lowering becomes the instrument of the competition (Cary, on Delaware selling charters by lowering the protections owed to shareholders, the franchise-tax incentive that rewards the state for pleasing the mobile chooser). The lowering is not corruption but the ordinary pursuit of a lawful revenue the mobile base controls.
And each lowering reads as a win. The jurisdiction that lowers sees registrations and revenue rise, and by every number it watches this is success, competitiveness, a place good for business, so the downward step wears the face of a victory and never of a loss, and the metric that would show the cost is not the one the jurisdiction is holding (the descent booked as attractiveness, each cut to the standard counted as a gain).
And a rival that holds a higher standard must match or lose the base. Once one jurisdiction lowers, the neighbour that keeps a higher standard watches the base leave for the laxer terms, and a standard no base will sit under protects no one, so the rival lowers too, and the first lowers again to stay ahead, and the standard ratchets down not because any jurisdiction wants it low but because none can hold it high while the base can move to whoever holds it lower (Cary, on the race to the bottom, no state able to keep a higher standard unilaterally against the competition for charters).
And the harm falls on those who cannot move. The standard protected someone: the shareholders a disclosure rule guarded, the crew a safety rule guarded, the public a capital rule guarded, and these are immobile, they hold the shares or crew the ship or live in the country and cannot follow the base out, so the base carries away its revenue and leaves them the lowered standard, the cost of the descent externalised onto the party the standard existed to protect (the externality borne by the immobile third party whose interest diverges from the mobile chooser's).
None of it is anyone's bad faith, and the direction of the race turns on who bears the cost. Each jurisdiction lowers rationally, each lowering is answered rationally, and no villain drives the descent: the mobility of the base and the competition for it drive it. And where the mobile chooser itself bears the cost of a lax standard, the same competition can raise the standard instead, a race to the top, so the descent is not a law of competition but a condition of it, biting only where the chooser's interest diverges from the immobile third party's and the standard the chooser prefers externalises its harm (Winter, on state law and shareholder protection, competition raising standards where the mobile party internalises the cost of laxity; Romano, on the empirical debate over which race a competition for charters actually runs).
Hold a common floor and the descent can be stopped: a minimum standard agreed across the jurisdictions, a harmonisation below which none may cut, so the base cannot be drawn by going lower and the immobile third party keeps the protection: but a floor is costly to agree and defection-prone to keep, because any jurisdiction that quietly cuts below it draws the base and the revenue back, so each carries a standing incentive to defect and the floor holds only while every jurisdiction pays, continuously, the price of not undercutting a neighbour it could win the base from, against the very competition that built the descent.

**[The Match](plot_the_match.md)**

Closes when the rival has cut to keep the base and the first jurisdiction has cut again to stay ahead, each lowering a rational answer to the last, and the bar a notch lower than before with no one having chosen it low.

**[The Descent](plot_the_descent.md)**
Cue: each cut is answered by the next across the market of laws. The lowerings compound, and the standard ratchets down toward the lowest on offer, no jurisdiction able to hold it high while the base can find it low elsewhere. Closes when the standard has settled at the bottom, a floor no one chose and none can lift alone, the harm resting on the immobile third parties the standard had protected and no villain anywhere in the descent.

**[The Floor](plot_the_floor.md)**
Cue: someone would stop the descent with a common minimum. The Bound would have the jurisdictions agree a floor below which none may cut, against each jurisdiction's standing power to win the base back by quietly cutting below it. Closes when the floor is agreed and held and the base can no longer be drawn by going lower ... or a jurisdiction defects to draw the base back, the harmonisation unravels, and the descent resumes toward the lowest bar.

## Stakes

Whether a set of jurisdictions can hold a standard, when a base can move to whichever holds it lowest and the revenue follows the base.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the standard lowered, which each jurisdiction is right to cut to keep a base that would otherwise leave, and away from the floor held, which asks every jurisdiction to forgo the base it could win by cutting below. The production cannot make the standard hold on its own, because a base that can move sits where the standard costs it least, and a jurisdiction that keeps a higher standard loses the base and the revenue to one that keeps it lower, so the descent is competition working as competition does over a mobile base, not anyone's malice: each jurisdiction lowers rationally and each lowering is answered. It can only show that the base is mobile and the revenue is real and each cut reads as a win, that the harm falls on the shareholders and crews and public who cannot follow the base out, and that the one move that stops the descent, a common floor no jurisdiction may cut below, must be held by paying, continuously, the price of not undercutting a rival to win the base back, against every standing incentive to defect, so the market of laws is competitive and each jurisdiction is rational and the floor they would all prefer is the one thing the competition will not let them keep.
