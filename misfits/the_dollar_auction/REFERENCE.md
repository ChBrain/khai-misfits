# The Dollar Auction: Reference

The research warrant for [The Dollar Auction](play_the_dollar_auction.md).
The dynamic is the dollar auction, an all-pay escalation: Shubik's paradox, the experimental study of entrapment,
the game-theoretic solution, the refined analysis of the escalation, and the war-of-attrition kin that shares the
all-pay structure, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the dollar auction as an **all-pay escalation structure**: a prize of known worth is auctioned under a rule
that the runner-up, not only the winner, pays their last bid, so whoever is second faces losing their stake for
nothing and bidding just past the leader is the smaller loss, a sound forward-looking choice, and because there is
always a one who is second the two bidders climb past the prize toward ruin. The play models the documented mechanics,
the known-value prize, the all-pay rule whose forfeit makes the next bid rational, the escalation with no floor, the
lock that only tightens as the bids pass the prize, and the shared ruin that pays the auctioneer from both stakes, as
the load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of a fool or a swindler. The governing finding of the source
is the play's governing law: where the second-highest bidder forfeits their last bid, every next bid by the one who
is behind is a rational choice of the lesser loss, so a transparent rule met by two reasonable bidders escalates past
the prize and splits the ruin between them, no misjudgement and no fallacy required.

The rationality the play stages is step-wise: each bidder weighs only the next move, forfeit the last bid now or
raise now and take the prize, and by that local reckoning the raise is always the smaller loss with no floor beneath
it. Fuller reasoning points elsewhere: given commonly known budgets, backward induction forecloses the war, a single
deterring opening bid ending it before it starts (O'Neill), or the players resolving to a draw (Leininger), so the
ruin is the road step-wise rationality takes and equilibrium rationality does not, which is why the one escape is
never to enter. Teger's real bidders are pulled backward too, by the wish to recoup a stake already at risk, and the
play brackets that entrapment to isolate the structural driver that needs none of it: the all-pay rule alone makes
the local raise rational.

It is distinct from the house's Good Money After Bad, and the distinction is the whole point of the cut: Good Money
After Bad stages the sunk-cost fallacy, where a single actor throws more after a loss because of what is already
spent, a backward-looking error that the sunk cost should have been ignored to avoid; here each bid is forward-looking
and correct, the smaller loss chosen not because of what was spent but because the all-pay rule makes the next bid
worth its cost, and the trap needs two parties and no fallacy required, so where Good Money After Bad is irrational
persistence, the dollar auction is step-wise rational escalation. It is distinct too from The Winning Bid, where the winner's
curse has the winner overpay because, among honest estimates of an uncertain common value, theirs ran highest and
won the prize: here the prize's value is known exactly, a dollar is a dollar, and no one estimates it at all, the
overpayment driven entirely by the all-pay structure and not by a value that could only be guessed, so the two
paradoxes overpay for opposite reasons, one from a value known only by estimate and the other from a rule that
punishes coming second.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the auctioneer as a
  swindler or the bidders as fools, because the source finding is precisely that no swindle and no folly is needed:
  the auctioneer offers a transparent game with its rule stated plainly, the bidder who enters for a dollar going
  cheap is answering a real bargain, and the bidder who bids again is choosing the smaller loss with open eyes. The
  ruin is authored by no deception and no error and emerges from a transparent rule met by two sound sequences of
  choices, so the misfit is that a clear all-pay structure, offered honestly and played rationally, climbs two
  reasonable people past the prize and pays the house from both their losses, no con and no blunder required. That
  the auctioneer profits is a fact of the structure, not a trick worked on the bidders, who could read the rule as
  easily as they read the prize.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a bidder stopping the spiral from within. The one move that
  would spare them is to precommit not to enter, since once in, every exit forfeits more than the last bid and the
  reason to bid one more only strengthens as the numbers climb, but the bait is a dollar for a nickel and the entry
  looks free, so refusing it asks a bidder to walk away from an apparent gift on the strength of foreseeing a trap not
  yet sprung. The reckoning is a spiral escaped only before it starts, not one any bidder can climb back down, so the
  cure for an all-pay escalation is a refusal at the threshold that the bait is built to make look foolish, and the
  one winning move is not to play.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never claimed as the
  house's, and its reach is not overstated. The staging is the house's; the dollar-auction paradox is Shubik's, the
  experimental entrapment is Teger's, the game-theoretic solution under finite budgets is O'Neill's, the refined
  analysis of when the escalation runs is Leininger's, and the war of attrition that shares the all-pay structure is
  Maynard Smith's, each carried as carefully as the finding itself: the misfit stages the all-pay rule that makes each
  bid rational and credits that the escalation is neither a sunk-cost fallacy nor a misjudged value but a sound step
  the rule leaves no floor beneath.
