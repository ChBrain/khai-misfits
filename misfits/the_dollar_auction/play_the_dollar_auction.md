# Play: The Dollar Auction

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

The Dollar Auction.
An auctioneer holds up a dollar and offers it to the highest bidder, with one rule stated plainly: the second-highest bidder also pays their last bid, and gets nothing. Someone bids a nickel, because a dollar for a nickel is free money. Someone bids a dime. Now the nickel-bidder is about to lose a nickel for nothing, so they bid fifteen cents: better to pay fifteen and win a dollar than pay five and win nothing. And it climbs, each raise the same small sum of arithmetic. At ninety cents against ninety-five, the ninety-cent bidder bids a dollar and five: better to pay a dollar-five for a dollar, losing a nickel, than pay ninety cents for nothing, losing it all. Past the dollar the logic does not change. At a dollar-fifty against a dollar-fifty-five, bidding a dollar-sixty to lose sixty cents still beats forfeiting a dollar-fifty for nothing. So two reasonable people bid a dollar up past two, past five, the auctioneer pocketing both stakes. Nobody misjudged the dollar: everyone knows it is worth exactly one. Nobody is throwing good money after bad: that is a fallacy, and this is its opposite, a forward-looking choice of the smaller loss at every turn. The trap is the rule. Because the runner-up forfeits their last bid, being second always makes one more bid worth its cost, and there is no bid at which that stops being true, so the sound step has no floor and the only escape is the one move that looks like leaving money on the table: never to bid at all.

## Arc

The bend is this: when the runner-up in an auction forfeits their last bid, every next bid by the one who is second is, weighed one move ahead, a rational choice of the smaller loss, and since there is always a one who is second, two reasonable bidders climb past the prize toward ruin, a spiral driven by no misjudgement of the prize and no sunk-cost fallacy, only the sound step-wise choice the all-pay rule leaves no floor beneath, though a bidder who reasoned the whole game through would never begin it.
It runs on a prize of known worth and an all-pay rule.
The prize is a dollar, its value plain to everyone, and the rule is stated openly: the highest bidder wins it and the second-highest pays their last bid for nothing (Shubik). The structure is transparent, and the trap is in the structure, not in any deception.
And the bait draws entry.
A dollar going for a nickel is free money, so a bidder enters for the easy prize, and a second enters to take it or to deny it, each stepping in for a small stake and a large-looking gain (Teger). The entry looks costless, and it is the only costly move there is.
And being second makes the next bid rational.
Whoever is second faces losing their last bid for nothing, so bidding just above the leader, to win the prize and escape the forfeit, is the smaller loss, and each raise is a sound forward-looking choice, not a throwing of good money after bad (Shubik; O'Neill). The one who is behind always has reason to bid, and there is always one who is behind.
And the lock closes past the prize.
Once the bids near the dollar, quitting means forfeiting a stake as large as the prize or larger, so the reason to bid one more only strengthens as the numbers climb, and past the dollar it does not weaken, each bidder choosing to lose a little more to win rather than lose it all for nothing (O'Neill; Leininger). The escalation has no natural stop, the same all-pay logic biology knows as the war of attrition (Maynard Smith).
And the ruin is shared, and the house is paid.
The climb ends only when one bidder runs out of nerve or budget and quits, forfeiting their last bid, while the winner takes a dollar for far more than a dollar, so both bidders lose and the auctioneer collects both stakes, a mutual ruin authored by two sound sequences of choices (Shubik). The winner overpaid and the loser paid for nothing, and neither made a mistake along the way.
None of it is folly. The auctioneer offering a transparent game, the bidder choosing the smaller loss at every step: each is doing the reasonable thing, and the ruin emerges from the sum, no swindle and no fallacy, which is exactly why two clever people are as easily trapped as two foolish ones.
Precommit not to enter and the spiral never starts: but the bait is a dollar for a nickel and the entry looks free, and once in, every exit costs more than the last bid, so the reckoning is a trap escaped only by the foresight to refuse a gift, not a spiral anyone can climb back down, and the one winning move is not to play.

## Stakes

Whether two reasonable people can be handed a structure that turns each right choice into a shared ruin, or whether being right at every step is enough to keep them safe.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the escalation, which the one who is second is right to continue because one more bid is the smaller loss, and away from the stop, which no bidder can choose without forfeiting more than the one behind them. The production cannot make a bidder quit while ahead of the forfeit, because at every price the next bid still beats eating the last one, so bidding on is the sound play and refusing to stop the sound response: the leader defending the prize and the chaser escaping the forfeit are each only doing the arithmetic. It can only show that no one is a fool, that a transparent all-pay rule met by two rational bidders climbs past the prize and pays the house from both their losses, and that the one move that would spare them, never to enter, is the hardest because the bait is a gift and the entry looks free, so the ruin holds not by anyone's misjudgement but by the sound step that the rule leaves no floor beneath.
