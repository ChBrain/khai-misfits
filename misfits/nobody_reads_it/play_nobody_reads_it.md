# Play: Nobody Reads It

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

Nobody Reads It.
A competitive market is supposed to hold the fine print in line the way it holds the price in line. If a seller writes a harsh warranty or a one-sided clause, buyers will notice and take their custom elsewhere, so competition should discipline the terms as surely as it disciplines the price, and no buyer needs to fear the boilerplate. That is the reassuring picture, and it has a real mechanism under it: the market does not need every buyer to read, only enough. If an informed minority reads and compares the terms, and the seller cannot tell at the counter which buyer read and which did not, the seller must offer decent terms to everyone in order to keep the readers, and the many who never read ride free on the few who do (Schwartz and Wilde). The catch is in the size of that minority. Reading a standard form, understanding its clauses, and comparing them across sellers has a real cost, and on any single purchase that cost is larger than the expected saving, since the bad term is rare and the reading is dear, so a rational buyer skips it (Stigler). Each buyer is right to skip, and the skipping is the trap: as the readers thin, the seller's incentive to hold the terms thins with them, and below some margin the discipline lifts, because keeping decent terms to retain a handful of readers costs the seller more than the readers are worth. Then the non-salient terms, the ones no one reads and no one prices, drift toward the drafter, one clause at a time, not because the drafter is grasping but because a rival who writes a harsher term and shaves the price captures the buyers who see only the price, so the generous clause loses and the market clears on the meaner one. Nobody cooked anything. Each buyer skipped the terms because reading did not pay, each seller wrote toward itself because competition on the visible price rewards it and the buried terms cost nothing to worsen, and the fine print, unread and unpoliced, ratchets one way. The terms are competitive only where enough people read, and nobody reads, so the terms are not competitive, and the market that keeps the price honest lets the print go bad in the dark it is never read in.

## Arc

The bend is this: the discipline that keeps standard-form terms competitive requires an informed minority large enough to make policing worth the seller's while, and because reading the terms costs more than it saves, that minority stays too thin, so the non-salient terms drift toward the drafter unchecked (Schwartz and Wilde).
It runs on a reading that does not pay.
Because acquiring and comparing the terms is costly and the bad clause is rare, the expected benefit of reading a single form falls short of its cost, so the rational buyer searches the salient price and skips the buried terms, informed on what is cheap to know and uninformed on what is dear (Stigler). The skipping is sound, and it is what thins the margin of readers.
And a thin margin does not discipline.
Because the seller cannot separate the reader from the non-reader at the counter and must post one form to all, decent terms are worth offering only while the readers retained are worth more than the terms concede, so below a threshold of informed demand the seller stops policing its own print, and the informed and uninformed coexist with the terms tilted toward the seller (Salop and Stiglitz). The equilibrium is stable, and it leaves the fine print unheld.
And unheld terms drift toward the drafter.
Because the standard form is offered take-it-or-leave-it and its clauses are dictated rather than dickered, a seller who worsens a non-salient term and trims the visible price beats a rival who keeps the term generous and prices it in, since buyers choose on the price they read and not the clause they do not, so competition itself moves the terms toward whoever writes them (Kessler). The drift is one-way, and each notch is a rational response to a market that reads only the price.
And the free ride collapses onto everyone.
Because one form clears to all, the reader who did the work is bound by the same degraded clause as the non-reader who rode free, so the thinning of the margin does not merely fail the lazy: it removes the floor from under the diligent too, the careful buyer and the careless one signing the identical term, the discipline gone for both (Schwartz and Wilde). The uniformity is the point, and it is why no private diligence recovers what the missing readers would have held.
And the obvious remedy does not read for anyone.
Because mandating disclosure makes the terms available but not attended, and the cost that kept them unread survives their being posted, forcing the clause into the open leaves the informed minority as thin as before, so the discipline is not manufactured by making the print visible when the print was never the barrier: the reading was (Bakos, Marotta-Wurgler and Trossen). The availability is real and the attention is not, because a term one is free to read and still will not read is disclosed and still unpoliced.
None of it is a fix. Each buyer skipping a reading that does not pay, each seller writing toward the price its buyers actually read, the one form clearing to reader and non-reader alike, and the mandate that discloses what no one attends: each is sound on its own, and the degraded term emerges from the sum, no bad faith and no deception required. Arrest the drift, and the arrest is dear: it takes an informed minority thick enough to make policing pay, which means making the reading worth the buyer's while against a cost that made skipping rational in the first place, or a rule that writes the terms directly rather than trusting a discipline that has no readers to run on. So the one move that would hold the fine print is to manufacture the attention the market cannot, at a cost the market's own economy explains away, and short of it the terms are competitive in name and drifting in fact, honest on the price and bad in the print, kept in line exactly where they are read and let go exactly where they are not.

## Triggers

**[The Skim](plot_the_skim.md)**
A form at the counter and a queue behind. The Signer reads the price, runs an eye past the terms, and signs, because stopping to read and understand the clauses would cost more time and effort than the rare bad term is likely to take. Closes when the term is accepted unread, the search cost saved and the clause taken on trust, one more buyer added to the uninformed many.

**[The Nudge](plot_the_nudge.md)**
Cue: a term no one reads. The Drafter moves a non-salient clause a notch toward itself and trims the visible price to match, since a rival who does the same will undercut a form that keeps the clause generous, and no buyer choosing on price will see the difference. Closes when the term slides one notch toward the drafter, unresisted because unread, the competition on price having worsened the print.

**[The Lone Reader](plot_the_lone_reader.md)**
Cue: one buyer reads the terms. The Shopper compares the clauses across sellers, finds them poor, and would take custom elsewhere, but the readers are too few to be worth keeping, so the seller loses nothing by ignoring the one who noticed. Closes when the informed reader's discipline fails to bite, the margin below the threshold that would move the term, the reader right and unheeded.

**[The Mandate](plot_the_mandate.md)**
Cue: someone would fix it by forcing the terms into the open. A disclosure rule posts the clause plainly and makes it free to read, on the theory that a visible term is a policed term. The Shopper is no more numerous for it, because the cost that kept the print unread was never its availability. Closes when the mandate makes the clause accessible and still almost no one reads it, the informed margin unmanufactured and the drift unarrested, or the dearer correction that would thicken the margin left unattempted.

## Stakes

Whether a competitive market disciplines the terms it disciplines the price, or holds the price alone and lets the fine print drift toward the drafter, competitive in name and one-sided in fact wherever too few read to police it.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the degraded term, which the Signer is right to skip a reading that does not pay, and the Drafter right to write toward the price its buyers actually read, and away from the discipline that would hold the print, which needs more readers than the search cost leaves. The production cannot make the drift a fraud, because each buyer skips soundly and each seller responds soundly to a market that reads only the price and one form clears to all, so signing without reading and writing toward the drafter and posting a single term are each only sensible acts. It can only show that a discipline resting on an informed minority fails when the minority is priced too thin, that the free ride the many take on the few collapses onto everyone when the few grow too few, so the careful buyer is bound by the same drifted clause as the careless one, and that the obvious remedy, making the terms visible, does not manufacture the reading the discipline needs, since a clause one is free to read and still will not read is disclosed and still unpoliced, so the print goes bad not by anyone's deception but by the ordinary good sense of everyone who skips a reading that costs more than it saves.
