# Piece: The Regressive Load

## Taxonomy

[Too Much to Ask](play_too_much_to_ask.md). The incidence of the burden: weighing most on the poor, the sick, the elderly, and the already stretched, so the friction screens out exactly the neediest the programme was built for.

## Owner

- Project: khai-misfits

## Place

Borne everywhere the steps are met, at [the kitchen table](place_the_kitchen_table.md) and [the counter](place_the_counter.md) alike, and read only at [the rolls](place_the_rolls.md), where whose claim went unmade at last comes to a shape.

## Load Bearing

Without it the burden would fall evenly, a flat tax on time and patience that any applicant, rich or poor, paid the same and could plan around.
With it the same set of steps costs each applicant differently: a document is a filing-cabinet errand to one household and a day's unpaid leave to another, a wait is an inconvenience to one and the difference between eating and not to another, and time, health, and ease with forms are exactly what the poor, the sick, the elderly, and the stretched have least of. The Regressive Load is load bearing because it is the incidence itself (Sunstein): the burden weighing most on those with the least to spend on it, so the piece carries the distribution that turns an equal ask into an unequal exclusion, the neediest paying the identical toll at a price they alone cannot afford.

## Apparent

Fairness. The same form, the same wait, the same verification, asked alike of every applicant regardless of who they are.
Seen at the surface, the Regressive Load looks like the opposite of a bias, a rule applied without exception or favour, and the truth that identical demands are not identical costs, that the very people the programme exists for are the ones with the least time, health, and ease to meet them, stays hidden behind how scrupulously equal the paperwork looks on the page that does not ask who is filling it in.

## Yearbook
