# Too Much to Ask: Reference

The research warrant for **Too Much to Ask** (play and company not yet staged;
this warrant is drafted first, to prove the science and the distinction before authoring).
The dynamic is the administrative-burden account of access: Moynihan, Herd, and Harbage's learning,
compliance, and psychological costs, Herd and Moynihan's burden framework, and Sunstein's sludge, all
cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging administrative burden as an **access-friction structure**: a programme built to serve people,
each of whose verification steps, forms, and waiting periods is added for a defensible reason, fraud
prevention or eligibility accuracy or stewardship of funds, yet whose steps together impose a learning
cost, a compliance cost, and a psychological cost on the applicant that accumulate until the very people
the programme was meant to serve are deterred from claiming it, so take-up falls below eligibility with
no one intending to exclude anyone. The governing law is not that a gate was built to keep people out,
but that **a claimant-side friction, each increment defensible, deters the take-up it never meant to
block, and falls hardest on those with the least to spend on it**, so the programme screens out the
neediest it was built for. The play models the documented mechanics: the three costs of a citizen-state
interaction, learning what one is entitled to and how, complying with the forms and documents and waits,
and bearing the stigma, stress, and loss of standing (Moynihan, Herd, and Harbage), the accumulation of
these small costs into a large effect on whether an eligible person applies at all (Herd and Moynihan),
and the regressive incidence by which the burden weighs most on the poor, the sick, the elderly, and the
already stretched, exactly the population the programme exists for (Sunstein), as the load-bearing
vectors of the structure, not as a tale of a bureaucrat building a barrier on purpose.

It is distinct from the house's The Paper Trail, where a provider documents defensively to be blameless,
process optimised for the sign-off that protects the person while the aim fails: there the burden is
provider-side and protective, and the failure is a professional going through motions to cover
themselves, here the burden is claimant-side and deterrent, and the failure is the opposite, the
qualified applicant going unserved because claiming costs too much. It is distinct from Nobody Reads It,
where a mandated disclosure is available but unread, the information never consumed: here the problem is
not that a document goes unread but that the accreted cost of the whole interaction deters the eligible
from entering it at all, an access friction and not an ignored notice. And it is distinct from The
Tyranny of Small Decisions, where many uncoordinated choices sum to an aggregate no one chose: here the
sum is not an unwilled aggregate but a selection, a friction that screens the intended beneficiaries by
their capacity to bear it, the harm distributional and falling on the neediest, not a merely unmanaged
total. The house stages only the earnest version, the accretion of well-meant steps, and refuses the
deliberate-sludge barrier the literature also records.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast a
  bureaucrat building a barrier on purpose, because the source finding it stages is the earnest case:
  each verification step, form, and waiting period is added for a defensible reason, to prevent fraud,
  to check eligibility, to steward public funds (Herd and Moynihan), and the deterrence is not the aim
  but the accreted side effect of the learning, compliance, and psychological costs (Moynihan, Herd, and
  Harbage). The exclusion is authored by no one and emerges from the structure, a claimant-side friction
  summing past what the neediest can bear, so the misfit is that a programme built to serve, adding only
  well-meant steps, screens out the very people it was built for. The house explicitly leaves aside the
  deliberate-sludge barrier the literature also records (Sunstein), staging the trap and not the weapon.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a costless correction. The moves that lower
  the burden, auto-enrolment, pre-filled forms, dropped verifications, help to navigate the steps, do
  not come free: each defensible step eased is a measure of the fraud, error, or misallocation it guarded
  against let back in, and reaching people rather than filtering them spends real administrative effort
  and money. The reckoning is access bought against the very risks the steps were raised to control, a
  burden lowered by accepting more of what it guarded, not a free correction.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never
  claimed as the house's. The staging is the house's; the three costs are Moynihan's, Herd's, and
  Harbage's, the burden framework and the take-up gap are Herd's and Moynihan's, and sludge and its
  regressive incidence are Sunstein's, and the condition the trap actually requires, an earnest,
  claimant-side friction that deters the eligible and falls hardest on the neediest, is carried as
  carefully as the finding itself.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per element. The play and its company are not yet authored; these are the
proposed load-bearing pieces the warrant commits the staging to carry, one per vector, no filler.

- **The Step**: carries the single requirement (Herd and Moynihan), the verification, form, or waiting
  period added for a defensible reason and, on its own, entirely reasonable.
- **The Learning Cost**: carries the cost of finding out (Moynihan, Herd, and Harbage), knowing that one
  is entitled and what the rules and demands are, the first barrier before any form is filled.
- **The Compliance Cost**: carries the cost of complying (Moynihan, Herd, and Harbage), the forms,
  documents, appointments, and waits the applicant must produce and endure.
- **The Psychological Cost**: carries the cost of enduring (Moynihan, Herd, and Harbage), the stigma,
  stress, frustration, and loss of standing of asking the state for help.
- **The Take-Up Gap**: carries the accrued sum (Herd and Moynihan), the eligible who never claim, take-up
  fallen below eligibility, the exclusion that lives only in aggregate.
- **The Regressive Load**: carries the incidence (Sunstein), the burden weighing most on the poor, sick,
  elderly, and stretched, so the friction screens out exactly the neediest the programme was built for.
