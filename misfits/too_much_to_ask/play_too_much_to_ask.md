# Play: Too Much to Ask

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

Too Much to Ask.
The programme is built to help. It exists to get a benefit, a credit, a course of care to the people who qualify for it, and it takes its duty seriously, so it checks. It asks the applicant to prove they are eligible, and that is fair: public money should reach the right people and not the wrong ones. It asks for a form, and a document, and a second document to confirm the first, and a wait while the office verifies, and a renewal every so often to be sure nothing has changed, and each of these is added for a reason anyone would grant: to stop fraud, to catch error, to be accountable for the funds. And on its own, no single step is unreasonable. But the applicant does not face one step, they face all of them at once, and each carries a cost: the cost of finding out they qualify and what the rules demand, the cost of gathering the papers and making the appointments and sitting in the wait, and the cost, harder to see, of the stigma and the stress and the small humiliation of proving to the state that you are poor enough, sick enough, deserving enough to be helped. And these costs fall most heavily on exactly the people the programme is for, the ones with the least time, the least health, the least ease with forms, the fewest hours to spend in a queue, so it is the neediest who are most likely to give up somewhere in the middle, to not apply at all, to let the renewal lapse, and the benefit goes unclaimed. And the terrible thing is that no one built a gate: every step was added in good faith for a defensible reason, and no one anywhere decided to keep these people out, and yet the sum of the well-meant steps is a programme that screens out the very people it was created to serve, take-up falling below eligibility not by anyone's design but by the mere weight of a hundred reasonable asks, each fair, all of them together simply too much to ask of the ones who needed it most.

## Arc

The bend is this: a claimant-side friction, each increment defensible, deters the take-up it never meant to block, and falls hardest on those with the least to spend on it, so a programme built to serve screens out the neediest it was built for.
It runs on a burden borne by the applicant.
Dealing with the state costs the citizen three things: a learning cost, to find out one is entitled and what the rules and demands are, a compliance cost, the forms and documents and appointments and waits, and a psychological cost, the stigma and stress and loss of standing in asking for help (Moynihan, Herd, and Harbage). Each is real, and each is borne not by the programme but by the person trying to claim.
And each step is defensible. A verification is added to stop fraud, a document to check eligibility, a wait to be sure, a renewal to keep the rolls accurate, and every one of these is a reasonable thing to ask, added in good faith by an administrator doing their duty (Herd and Moynihan). No step is a barrier by intent, and none, alone, is unreasonable.
And the costs accumulate. The applicant faces the steps together, not singly, and the small, isolated costs sum into a large effect on whether an eligible person applies at all, so take-up falls below eligibility, the benefit going unclaimed not because anyone was refused but because claiming cost more than the claimant could spend (Herd and Moynihan).
And the burden is regressive. It weighs most on the poor, the sick, the elderly, the least literate, the already stretched, exactly the people the programme exists for, because they have the least time, health, and ease to spend on the cost of claiming, so the friction screens hardest the ones who needed it most (Sunstein).
None of it is a barrier built on purpose. Every step was added for a defensible reason and no one decided to exclude anyone: the deterrence is not the aim but the accreted side effect of a hundred reasonable asks, and the exclusion emerges from the sum. What was built to serve the neediest turns them away by the weight of serving carefully.
Lower the burden, auto-enrol the eligible, pre-fill the forms, drop a verification, help the applicant through the steps, and the benefit can reach the people it is for: but every defensible step eased is a measure of the fraud, error, or misallocation it guarded against let back in, and reaching people rather than filtering them spends real administrative effort and money, so the escape is bought by accepting more of exactly what the steps were raised to control, access weighed against the risks the checks were there to catch.

## Triggers

**[The Requirement](plot_the_requirement.md)**
The Administrator adds a step for a defensible reason. A verification against fraud, a document to check eligibility, a wait to be sure, each one reasonable and added in good faith, the Auditor's risk the warrant for it. Closes when the step is in place, fair on its own and defensible to anyone, the interaction one requirement longer.

**[The Ordeal](plot_the_ordeal.md)**
Cue: the Applicant faces the steps together. At the kitchen table and the counter they bear the cost of finding out they qualify, the cost of the forms and documents and waits, and the cost of the stigma of asking, all at once. Closes when the applicant is worn by the accreted learning, compliance, and psychological costs, the benefit not yet claimed and the effort mounting.

**[The Abandonment](plot_the_abandonment.md)**
Cue: the cost exceeds what the applicant can spend. Worn down somewhere in the middle, short of time or health or ease with the forms, the Applicant gives up, does not finish, does not apply, lets the renewal lapse. Closes when an eligible person has been deterred, the benefit unclaimed, no one having refused them and the door never formally shut.

**[The Reckoning](plot_the_reckoning.md)**
Cue: the deterrals sum across the rolls. Day after day the eligible give up and the least-resourced give up most, so take-up falls below eligibility and the gap is regressive, widest exactly among the people the programme was for. Closes when a programme built to serve has turned away the neediest it was built for, the exclusion visible only in the aggregate and authored by no one.

**[The Easing](plot_the_easing.md)**
Cue: the Reformer would lower the burden. Auto-enrolling the eligible, pre-filling the forms, dropping a verification, helping the applicant through, against the Auditor's warning that each eased step lets back the fraud or error it guarded. Closes when the burden is lowered and the benefit reaches its people ... or the risk the steps controlled is judged too dear to admit, and the steps, each defensible, stay.

## Stakes

Whether a programme can reach the people it was built for, when every step it asks is fair.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the steps, each added for a defensible reason, and away from the applicant, worn down by the accreted cost of claiming and, if they are among the neediest, worn down soonest. The production cannot make the programme reach its people by any single correction, because no single step is the fault: the exclusion is the sum of a hundred reasonable asks, and the deterrence is the side effect of serving carefully, not a gate anyone built. It can only show that every verification is defensible and added in good faith, that the burden falls on the claimant and hardest on the neediest, and that the one move that reaches the people the programme is for, lowering the burden and easing the steps, must be made by letting back a measure of the fraud, error, and misallocation the steps were raised to catch, and by spending real effort to reach people rather than filter them, so the diligence is real and the steps are fair and the programme built to serve the neediest turns them away by the weight of asking, each ask reasonable and all of them together too much to ask of the ones who needed it most.
