# Play: Moral Licensing

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

Moral Licensing.
You do a good thing, and it is real, and it proves you are good. Then the proof is banked: a credit against your name, a balance in your favour, and having earned it you can spend it. So a later lapse, often nowhere near the good deed, is waved through, because a good person is allowed this one, has earned this one ... and the good deed turns out not to have raised your conduct but to have bought permission to lower it, the virtue and the vice netting, quietly, to nothing.

## Arc

The bend is this: the good deed does not add to the good, it buys a licence to do less of it.
It runs on a balance kept, not a good maximised.
The self is held to a moral level, not driven to a moral peak, so conduct is regulated toward a set point: fall below it and you correct upward, rise above it and you have slack to spend. A good deed is a rise above, so it banks credit, and the credit is not saved but drawn on, licensing a lapse that brings the balance back down to level.
None of it is hypocrisy, not consciously. The good deed is genuine, the credit is really earned, and the balancing is not a trick but the ordinary machinery of a moral self-image, which keeps books and feels entitled to spend a surplus. The lapse feels not like a betrayal of the good but like its reward, a good person letting themselves off, this once, on the strength of a virtue that was true.
Hold the standard regardless of the credit, refuse to let a good deed buy a pass, treat the self as a work in progress and not a balance to spend, and the good can add instead of netting: but a bar that never credits virtue saps the will to be virtuous, since if it never counts, why do it, and the vigilance that denies itself every licence curdles into a joyless self-watching, and some of what looks like a licensed lapse is a rest that was honestly earned, which no ledger can cleanly tell from the other.

## Triggers

**[The Good Turn](plot_the_good_turn.md)**
A genuine good deed is done, and it proves the doer good. The Doer earns it honestly and the Ledger records it, banking the act as a credit against the doer's name, a balance in their favour. Closes when the good deed has become a moral credit, held and spendable.

**[The Banking](plot_the_banking.md)**
Cue: the credit is not saved but readied to spend. The Ledger keeps the moral self at a set level rather than a peak, and the License draws on the surplus, turning a rise above the line into a pass to fall back to it. Closes when the credit has become a licence, permission issued against a virtue that was real.

**[The Cashing](plot_the_cashing.md)**
Cue: the pass is spent. The License grants the lapse, often far from the good deed's domain, and the Lapse takes it, a good person letting themselves off this once. Closes when the lapse has been waved through on the strength of the credit, and felt like a reward rather than a fall.

**[The Wash](plot_the_wash.md)**
Cue: the account is totalled. The Doer's real virtue and the Lapse it funded net to nothing, so the good deed is revealed not to have raised the conduct but to have bought permission to lower it. Closes when the good has washed out against the licence it bought, and the balance sits back at level.

**[The Reckoning](plot_the_reckoning.md)**
Cue: someone moves to stop the good deed buying a pass. The Consistent would hold the standard regardless of credit, against the Motivator's warning that virtue uncounted goes undone, that vigilance curdles, and that an earned rest cannot be cleanly told from a licensed lapse. Closes when the good adds instead of netting ... or the will to be good is sapped, or a true rest is denied as though it were a lapse.

## Stakes

Whether a good deed ever adds to the good, or only ever buys permission to do less of it.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the balance, which a good deed lifts above the line and a lapse brings back down, and away from the good maximised, which the set point was never keeping. The production cannot make a moral self-image stop keeping books, because the same accounting that credits a true good deed is the accounting that lets it be spent. It can only show that no one is a conscious hypocrite, that the good deed is real and the credit earned and the balancing the ordinary machinery of feeling good about oneself, and that the one move that makes virtue add instead of net, holding the standard regardless of the credit, saps the will it depends on, curdles into joyless self-watching, and cannot tell the licensed lapse from the rest that was honestly earned.
