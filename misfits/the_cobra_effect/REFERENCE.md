# The Cobra Effect: Reference

The research warrant for [The Cobra Effect](play_the_cobra_effect.md). The name is the
economists' term for a perverse incentive; the mechanics the play runs on are grounded in
the incentive-design and unintended-consequences literature, and in a documented historical
case, cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the perverse incentive as a **systemic system**: a reward set in good faith to reduce
a harm, which increases it, because a price paid per unit removed is, to anyone who can make
the unit, a price to produce it. The play models the documented mechanics, the reward aimed at
a countable token rather than the uncountable goal, the manufacture of the target to harvest
the reward, the metric that reads production as progress, and the release of the manufactured
stock when the reward is withdrawn, as the load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale
of cheats. The governing finding of the source literature is the play's governing law: an
incentive attached to a proxy that can be produced calls that proxy into being, so the reward
does not draw the harm down, it prices it up, and its correction strands the supply it created,
leaving the problem worse than the start.

## Origin

The phenomenon in practice, and the research spine beneath it.

| ----------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Siebert**             | "Der Kobra-Effekt: Wie man Irrwege der Wirtschaftspolitik vermeidet" (2001)                        | The naming: the colonial-Delhi anecdote of a cobra bounty that led to cobra breeding and, once withdrawn, a larger wild population. | **Kerr**                | "On the Folly of Rewarding A, While Hoping for B", _Academy of Management Journal_ (1975)          | The canonical statement: reward systems reliably produce the behaviour they pay for, which is often not the behaviour intended. | **Merton**              | "The Unanticipated Consequences of Purposive Social Action", _American Sociological Review_ (1936) | The **perverse consequence**: purposive action can produce the opposite of its aim, the sociological root of an intervention that reverses. The Backfire.                  |
| **Campbell / Goodhart** | Campbell's Law (1979); Goodhart's Law (1975)                                                       | Why a countable proxy is paid in place of the goal, and how using it for decisions corrupts it: the measurement gap the reward is aimed across. | **Practitioner**        | "perverse incentive", "cobra effect", bounty and quota design in policy and management             | The naming in use: incentives that manufacture the outcome they were meant to prevent, and the caution to reward outcomes rather than tokens.                              |

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast any
  seat as a cheat, because the source finding is precisely that no one need cheat: the sponsor means
  well, the farmer responds rationally to the price on offer, and the office pays honestly on real
  proof. The misfit is that a reward on a producible token is an offer to produce it.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a wise correction arriving in time. Pulling
  the bounty is the right call and is also the trigger, because it strands the manufactured stock and
  looses it into the street, so the reckoning leaves the problem worse, not a clear-eyed fix restoring
  it.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never
  claimed as the house's. The staging is the house's; the findings are theirs. The Delhi anecdote is
  credited as the term's popular origin, not asserted as verified history; the documented case is Hanoi.
