# Early and Uncounted: Reference

The research warrant for [Early and Uncounted](play_early_and_uncounted.md).
The dynamic is antagonistic pleiotropy: George Williams's finding that a gene with a beneficial early-life effect and a
harmful late-life one is favoured by selection because selection's force declines with age, Peter Medawar's founding
insight that a cohort thinned by extrinsic mortality exposes late-acting effects to weaker selection than early-acting
ones, W. D. Hamilton's formal derivation of that declining force, and Michael Rose and Brian Charlesworth's laboratory
confirmation of the trade-off, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging antagonistic pleiotropy as an **early-counted, late-uncounted structure**: a gene with two age-specific effects
is favoured for an early one felt by nearly the whole cohort and is not selected against for a late one, because the
cohort has thinned by then to a remnant too small to move the tally, however severe the cost to those it falls on; and
because many separate genes strike this same trade across the genome and across evolutionary time, their sum is the
visible pattern of organismal decline with age. The play models the documented mechanics, the cohort's thinning by
causes the trait never set, the full weight an early effect earns simply by how many carriers it reaches, the thin
weight a late effect earns by how few are left to have it, and the accrual of many such favoured trades into a single
visible pattern, as the load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of a careless gene or a failing body. The
governing finding of the source is the play's governing law: senescence is not adaptive in itself and not a design flaw
either, but the correlated cost of pleiotropic genes selected for their early benefit, a by-product of a selective force
that weakens with age (Williams). It is a tendency under stated conditions and not an iron rule, holding wherever
extrinsic mortality thins a cohort with age and a gene's effects are age-specific and opposed in sign, and it varies
with the shape of that thinning: where extrinsic mortality is lower and more of a cohort survives into later years, the
tally reaches further and senescence is predicted, and observed, to run slower, so the play stages a tendency set by a
population's mortality schedule, not a fate fixed alike for every organism.

It is distinct from the house's Fit to Fail, which stages a trait crossing a viability threshold into collapse: there
the population fails outright once a boundary is breached, and the engine is a threshold crossed. Here there is no
threshold and no collapse: a trait's late cost is paid gradually, by a thinning remnant, across an accumulating set of
genes, and the outcome is decline, not extinction, no boundary required. It is distinct too from the house's Seed Corn,
whose engine is a deliberate trade of present resource for future gain made by an agent under pressure: here nothing is
deliberated and nothing is traded by anyone. No agent weighs the early gain against the late cost, because the two are
never brought before the same ledger: the early effect is counted while the cohort is thick and the late effect is
counted, thinly, only once the cohort has thinned, on its own unrelated schedule, so the asymmetry is a fact about when
a fair count can reach, not a choice about what to spend now against what to save for later. Kirkwood's disposable-soma
theory, which casts the same senescence in terms of a body allocating limited resources between reproduction and its
own repair, is a distinct and credited account of a related question and is held off this play entire: it explains why
a trait might be built to divert resources away from maintenance, an allocation story, while this play stages why a gene
with two opposed effects is retained at all, a selection-force story, and the two are not blended here.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast a careless gene or a
  negligent tally, because the source finding is precisely that no error and no malice is needed: the trait does only
  what a gene with two ages does (Williams), the cohort thins for reasons entirely its own (Medawar), and the tally
  weighs exactly what is present at every age it is asked to weigh (Hamilton). So the misfit is that a gene favoured for
  a real, widely-felt early gain carries a real, unweighed late cost, no misfire and no malice required, each part of
  the count correct and the asymmetry their sum, the trait most rewarded early the one most nearly unaccountable late.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a body saved by a clean fix. The one lever that would let
  the tally reach the late cost is a cohort that survives further into later years before the count is taken, but which
  hazards thin a cohort, and how far, is not a choice available to the trait, the cohort, or the tally: it is set by
  extrinsic circumstances belonging to none of the parties inside the mechanism. Where a population's mortality
  schedule happens to thin more slowly, senescence is observed to run slower too (Williams; Rose and Charlesworth), but
  no persona here commands that schedule, so the remedy is an ecological fact the mechanism cannot reach for, never a
  lever any of its own parts can pull.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never claimed as the
  house's, and its reach is not overstated. The staging is the house's; the finding that a pleiotropic gene is favoured
  for its early effect and unbilled for its late one is Williams's, the extrinsic-mortality thinning that gives the
  finding its mechanism is Medawar's, the formal declining force of selection is Hamilton's, and the laboratory
  confirmation of the trade-off is Rose and Charlesworth's, each carried as carefully as the finding itself: the misfit
  stages a fair count that never reaches the years a cohort has already thinned out of it, and credits that no gene
  erred and no tally was negligent, holding it a tendency under stated conditions and not a law that fixes every
  organism's decline alike.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per element.

- **[The Toll](piece_the_toll.md)**: carries the finding whole (Williams), the visible decline with age built from many
  genes each favoured early and uncounted late.
- **[The Early Weight](piece_the_early_weight.md)**: carries the declining force of selection at its heavy end
  (Hamilton), the rule that weighs an early effect by the nearly whole cohort present to have it.
- **[The Thin Count](piece_the_thin_count.md)**: carries the declining force of selection at its thin end (Hamilton)
  applied to a cohort already narrowed by extrinsic mortality (Medawar), the same rule returning almost nothing once
  almost no one is left to weigh.
- **[The Accrual](piece_the_accrual.md)**: carries the empirical confirmation (Rose and Charlesworth), the trade-off
  demonstrated directly in a living population and the sum, across many genes, that the theory predicts.
- **[The Remnant](position_the_remnant.md)** and **[Thinning](process_thinning.md)**: carry the extrinsic-mortality
  thinning whole (Medawar), the cohort narrowing with age for reasons no gene set.
- **[The Tally](position_the_tally.md)** and **[Weighing](process_weighing.md)**: carry the formal declining force of
  selection whole (Hamilton), the rule itself that produces both the heavy early count and the thin late one.
