# Play: Early and Uncounted

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

Early and Uncounted.
A trait arises with two faces: one early, one late. Early, while the cohort is still nearly whole, whatever the trait does is felt by nearly everyone who carries it, so if it raises survival or fertility at that age, the gain is large and selection favours it in full, exactly as a gain felt by many should be favoured. But the same trait's other face waits for later years, and by then the cohort has thinned: not because of the trait, but because accident and predation and disease have been thinning it all along, on their own schedule, indifferent to what any gene does. So when the late face of the trait finally shows itself, whatever it costs, it is a cost paid by whoever is left, and whoever is left is a remnant, and a cost paid by a remnant barely moves the tally no matter how severe it is to the few who pay it. The trait is not hiding anything: it does both things it was always going to do, in the open, and selection is not looking away: it weighs exactly what is there to weigh, in full, every time. But weighing by who is there, applied fairly and without exception, produces a strange asymmetry all its own: an early gain that helps most of the cohort outweighs, every time, a late cost that harms only a remnant, so a trait can win a place in the genome for what it does at twenty and never answer for what it does at eighty, not because the twenty-year gain matters more, but because there are so many more twenties to count than eighties. And it is never one trait. Across a genome, across generations, many separate genes make this same trade on this same asymmetry, each one favoured on its own early face and each one uncounted on its own late face, so the visible pattern of decline with age, weaker, slower, more brittle, less fertile, is not the work of any single flawed gene or failing organ: it is the accrued residue of many individually correct countings, a body's whole senescence built from nothing but a tally that, applied honestly at every step, never had a way to reach the years that thinned out from under it.

## Arc

The bend is this: selection favours a trait for an early effect felt by nearly the whole cohort and cannot weigh the same trait's late effect against the cost it carries, because so few of the cohort survive to pay it, so senescence is not one flaw but the summed residue of many independently favoured, unbilled trades (Williams).
It runs on a cohort that thins for its own reasons.
Because predation, accident, and disease keep removing individuals from a population as it ages, regardless of any particular gene, the cohort that starts whole is, by any given later age, a remnant, thinned by hazards the trait never caused and cannot be blamed for (Medawar). The thinning is prior to the trait and indifferent to it, and it is what makes every later age sparsely attended.
And an early effect is counted in full.
Because nearly the whole cohort is still present at early ages, whatever a trait does there is felt by nearly everyone who carries it, so the tally weighs it in full and a real early gain is a strongly favoured one, the size of the cohort the size of the count (Hamilton). The weight is earned honestly, and it is what makes an early-favouring trait a winning one.
And a late effect is barely counted at all.
Because only a remnant is left alive by the time a trait's later face shows itself, the same fair rule, weigh by who is there, now returns almost nothing, so a cost that would be severe to any one of the remnant moves the whole-population tally hardly at all, and the trait is not selected against for a harm the tally was never positioned to see in force (Hamilton). The rule has not changed: only the number left for it to weigh has.
And no single trait is the whole story.
Because the same asymmetry, a heavy early count and a thin late one, applies to every gene with two faces at two ages, many genes across the genome make the identical trade across evolutionary time, each one favoured on its own early gain and each one passing its own late cost uncounted, so their sum, not any one of them, is the visible pattern of decline with age (Williams; Rose and Charlesworth). The residue is cumulative, and no one trait carries more than its own small, uncounted share.
And the tally never had a way to reach further.
Because weighing by who is present is the only rule the tally has ever had, and the thinning of the cohort is not something the tally can see around, there is no version of fair, honest, full counting that would have caught these late costs without the cohort itself surviving further into life to be counted at, so the trait is not an error slipping past a check: it is what a correct check produces when applied to a population that thins (Hamilton). The absence of a wider reach is not a failure of the rule: it is the rule, working.
None of it is a fix. The tally weighs exactly what is in front of it, the cohort thins for reasons that have nothing to do with any one trait, and each trait does only what it was always going to do at both of its ages, so a real early gain is rewarded in full and a real late cost is barely billed, no misfire and no malice required. And there is no lever inside the mechanism to pull: no trait can choose to be weighed differently, no tally can choose to weigh a remnant as though it were the whole, and the only thing that would change the count, more of the cohort surviving into later years, is not a choice available to any gene or any tally, but a fact about the hazards the cohort happens to face, set by circumstances belonging to none of them. Short of that, a trait favoured early and uncounted late remains favoured and uncounted, and the sum of many such trades is a body's decline with age, built entirely of countings that were each, on their own terms, done right.

## Triggers

**[The Claim](plot_the_claim.md)**
The Trait's early face shows itself while the cohort is still nearly whole. The Count weighs the gain against the whole cohort present to feel it, since a benefit felt by nearly everyone is a benefit fairly favoured in full. Closes when the Trait is strongly favoured, correctly, for an early effect it did nothing to hide and the Count did nothing to overweigh.

**[The Thinning](plot_the_thinning.md)**
Cue: time passes, and the Cohort is worn down. Predation, accident, and disease remove individuals from the population as it ages, on their own schedule and regardless of the Trait, so that by any later age only a remnant of the original cohort remains. Closes when the Cohort stands thinned, not by the Trait's doing, into the sparse remainder that any later count will have to weigh.

**[The Reckoning](plot_the_reckoning.md)**
Cue: the Trait's late face shows itself, at last, in the thinned Cohort. The Count applies the same rule it always has, weigh by who is present, and returns almost nothing, however severe the cost is to the remnant who carry it. Closes when the Trait passes unselected against, not from any oversight but because the fair rule, honestly applied, has almost no one left to weigh it against.

**[The Toll](plot_the_toll.md)**
Cue: someone looks at the whole span, not one trait but many. Across the genome and across generations, gene after gene has made the identical trade on the identical asymmetry, and their sum stands as the visible pattern of a body's decline with age. Closes when the toll is named as accrual, not damage: no single trait broken, no single check failed, only many individually correct countings summed into senescence, and the one thing that would have changed the count, a cohort surviving further before the reckoning came, set by hazards none of the parties inside the mechanism command.

## Stakes

Whether a trait rewarded for its early effect and never billed for its late one is a flaw in the tally or the tally's honest output, and whether a body's decline with age is a design that failed or the summed residue of countings that never failed at all.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward a trait correctly favoured and a cost correctly uncounted, and away from any check that could have weighed the late face as fully as the early one. The production cannot make the trait a mistake, because it does only what its two ages call for, and it cannot make the tally negligent, because it weighs exactly what is present to weigh at every age it is asked to weigh, so the asymmetry is not a bug in the rule or a fault in the gene: it is what a fair count produces once the cohort it counts begins, for its own unrelated reasons, to thin. It can only show that a trait can win a permanent place in a genome for a gain felt by many and keep that place regardless of a cost felt only by a few, that this is not one trait's story but the pattern of every gene that carries two faces at two ages, and that the one thing which would change the count, a cohort surviving further into the years it currently loses to other causes, belongs to none of the parties inside the mechanism: not the Trait, which cannot choose when to act; not the Cohort, which thins for reasons it does not set; and not the Count, which has never had any rule but weighing who is there. Senescence, on this reading, is not a punishment and not a design: it is what remains once every trait's early face has been paid for in full and every late face has been paid for out of a remnant too thin to move the count.
