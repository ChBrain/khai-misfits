# Pitch: A Cost Too Thin to Count

## Taxonomy

[Early and Uncounted](play_early_and_uncounted.md). The single hook: a trait's early effect is felt by nearly the whole cohort and its late effect by only the remnant that survives to have it, so selection can favour the trait in full for what it does young and never weigh what it does old, and the visible decline of age is the sum of many such favoured, uncounted trades.

## Owner

- Project: khai-misfits

## Tenor

A trait earns its place by what it does.
The tenor is fair accounting: a gene that helps a great many individuals survive or reproduce is a gene that should spread, and one that helps hardly anyone should not, so weighing a trait's effect by how many carriers actually have it is simply what an honest tally does, the size of the benefit and the size of the count the same number.

## Undertow

The count can only weigh who is left to be counted.
Underneath, a cohort thins with age for reasons no gene set, predation, accident, disease, so that by any later age only a remnant remains, and the same honest rule, weigh the effect by the carriers who have it, now returns almost nothing for a cost felt by that remnant, however severe it is to them, the thinness of the count doing all the work the trait's own severity cannot undo.

## Nerve

A trait can be favoured in full and answer for nothing.
The nerve is that the identical rule, applied without exception at every age, produces a wide asymmetry between a young gain and an old cost, so a trait need not hide its later face to escape selection against it: the cohort has already thinned it out of the reckoning before the trait ever has to.

## Echo

What lingers is that no single trait made this happen.
Across a genome and across generations, every gene with an early face and a late one strikes the same bargain on the same asymmetry, and their sum, not any one gene's flaw, is the pattern of decline with age, a body's whole senescence built of nothing but tallies that were, at every step, done honestly.
