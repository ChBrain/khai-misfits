# Play: The Horizon Problem

## Estate

## Name

The Horizon Problem.
Someone asks for a plan, and it is a reasonable ask: plan the next five years, model the next three, project to the end of the lease, cost it out to where we can see. And a planner takes up an instrument, a model, a spreadsheet, a forecast, a roadmap, and builds. And here is the thing about an instrument: it has an edge. A five-year model runs to year five. A cash-flow projection runs to the end of its horizon. A roadmap runs to the last quarter it plots. Not because the planner is short-sighted, and not because anyone decided the future past the edge does not matter, but because the instrument has a reach, and the reach is finite, and everything inside the reach is counted and everything past it is not there. Within the plan, the beyond does not exist as a cost, a consequence, or a constraint. It is not weighed and then set aside. It is not entered and then discounted. It is simply outside what the instrument can hold, so it carries no number, casts no shadow, and moves nothing in the model. And the planner is attentive, and far-sighted, and works in good faith to the edge, and the plan that comes out is sound: every cost it can reach is reckoned, the model balances, the horizon is where the ask fixed it. And nothing in the plan flags what it left out. There is no line that reads "and past year five, this." The exclusion does not announce itself, because the instrument does not know it has an edge, it only has one, and a cost that is structurally absent leaves no gap where it was, only a plan that reads whole. So the plan is approved, because it is complete to its horizon and its horizon is the one that was asked for, and it goes into effect, and the years inside the edge run as costed. And then someone arrives past the edge. A successor, a next tenure, the year after the model stopped, and meets the future the instrument excluded: the maintenance the five-year build never carried, the cliff past the end of the projection, the debt that came due in year six of a five-year plan. And it lands on them uncosted, because it was never weighed against anything, never traded, never entered, so there was no moment where anyone chose to spend it and no reckoning that could have caught it: it was outside the instrument, and now it is here. Nobody was short-sighted. Nobody spent the future to buy the present. The ask was reasonable, the instrument was sound, the planner was attentive, and the horizon was necessary, because you cannot plan to infinity and a plan without an edge is not a plan. It is only that an instrument with a finite reach excludes what is past its reach, structurally and without deliberation, and nothing in the plan marks the exclusion, so the beyond casts no shadow inside the plan and raises no flag, and the future the instrument could not reach arrives, uncosted, on whoever is standing there when it does.

## Arc

The bend is this: every planning instrument has a finite edge, and what lies past the edge does not exist within the plan as a cost or a constraint, not weighed and dismissed but structurally absent, so nothing flags the exclusion and the plan reads whole, and the uncosted future lands on whoever arrives past the edge.
It runs on an instrument that has a reach.
A model, a projection, a roadmap, a forecast: every planning instrument covers a finite span, and a plan that reached to infinity would not be usable, so the edge is not a defect but a property of the tool, the terminal horizon a usable model must have (the finite-horizon literature, dynamic programming's terminal horizon, Simon on the bounded premises a decision is actually taken within). The reach is necessary, and this is what makes it a trap and not a fault, because there is no instrument without an edge, and the edge is what makes the instrument work.
And the edge draws a boundary that excludes, undeliberately. Where the instrument stops, a boundary falls, and everything past it is outside the plan's account, not marginal but absent, and the boundary judgement that placed the edge is usually never examined as a judgement at all (Ulrich, on critical systems heuristics and boundary critique, every planning definition drawing a boundary that sweeps stakeholders and consequences out of view). The beyond is what the boundary sweeps outside (Churchman, on the system and its environment, what falls beyond the line the system draws). The exclusion is real and it is not chosen.
And the beyond carries no cost and casts no shadow. Inside the plan, what is past the edge has no number, so it constrains nothing, trades against nothing, and moves nothing in the model, and a cost that is structurally absent does not appear as a lack, because there is no slot it failed to fill. The future past the horizon is not deprioritised, not deferred, not judged and found less urgent: it is simply not in the instrument, so it exerts no pull on the plan at all.
And nothing flags the exclusion. There is no line in the plan that names what the horizon left out, because the instrument does not represent its own boundary as a cost, so the plan reads complete and sound and its completeness is real to its own edge (Ulrich, on the boundary judgement that goes unexamined, the framing whose margins are never brought back into view). The silence is the whole difficulty: the plan does not lie about the beyond, it says nothing about it, and saying nothing reads, to anyone checking, exactly like there being nothing to say.
And the excluded future lands on whoever arrives past the edge. The years inside run as costed, and then a successor arrives in the beyond and meets the future the instrument never carried, uncosted, untraded, and now present, and there is no one to charge for it because no one spent it (the boundary of the effect: the horizon is necessary, so the exclusion is required and undeliberate, and the loss falls where the plan could not see). The whole phenomenon is the landing: the structurally absent future, arriving with its bill, on the one standing where the plan ran out.
None of it is short-sightedness or a spent future. The ask was reasonable, the instrument sound, the planner attentive to the edge, and the horizon necessary, so no one erred and the future past the edge was not traded away but never in the account. What makes an instrument usable, that it has a finite reach, is exactly what makes it exclude the beyond, and a boundary the tool draws without deliberation is a boundary nothing in the plan is built to flag.
Look past the edge and carry the beyond as a cost: extend the horizon, stress the plan against the years past its reach, name what the instrument excludes and enter it as a constraint, and the uncosted future can be seen before it lands: but this means costing a future the instrument was built to bracket off, spending present effort and present numbers on years the model was not made to hold, defending a line item for a beyond that carries no shadow to justify it, and treating a plan that reads complete as incomplete precisely where it looks soundest, so the beyond is made visible only by paying, continuously, to look past an edge the instrument gives no reason to doubt.
