# The Horizon Problem: Reference

The research warrant for [The Horizon Problem](play_the_horizon_problem.md).
The dynamic is the unexamined boundary of a planning instrument: Ulrich's critical systems heuristics and
boundary critique, Midgley's systemic intervention, Churchman's system and environment, and the finite-horizon
literature on the terminal edge a usable model must have, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the horizon problem as a **bounded-instrument structure**: every planning instrument has a finite reach,
and what lies past that edge does not exist within the plan as a cost, a consequence, or a constraint, not weighed
and dismissed but structurally absent, so it carries no number, casts no shadow, and moves nothing in the model,
and nothing in the plan flags the exclusion, so the plan reads whole and the uncosted future lands on whoever
arrives past the edge. The play models the documented mechanics, the instrument whose finite reach is a property
of the tool and not the mind that holds it, the boundary that falls where the instrument stops and sweeps the
future past it out of the account, the beyond that is structurally absent rather than deprioritised, the boundary
judgement that goes unexamined so that nothing flags the exclusion, and the arriving future that comes due on the
successor with no one to charge because no one spent it, as the load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a
tale of short-sightedness or a spent future. The governing finding of the sources is the play's governing law:
every system or planning definition draws a boundary that includes some consequences and excludes others, the
boundary judgement is usually unexamined, and the exclusion it makes is not a fault to be corrected but a
condition of a usable framing, so a perfectly attentive, far-sighted planner using a finite instrument still
produces a plan with no costs past its edge.

The emphasis is exact and must be held: the future is not ignored, not deprioritised, and not judged and found less
urgent. It is outside the instrument. A cost weighed and set aside leaves a trace; a cost structurally absent
leaves no gap where it was, so the plan reads complete precisely where it stops, and the silence about the beyond
reads exactly like there being nothing to say. The horizon is necessary, because a plan without an edge is not a
plan and no instrument reaches to infinity, so the exclusion is required and undeliberate, and the scout who looks
past the edge is the costed remedy, not a hero: making the beyond visible as a cost means costing years the
instrument was built to bracket off, defending a line item for a future that casts no shadow to justify it, and
treating a sound plan as incomplete where it looks soundest.

The cut from neighbours must be exact, because several house structures sit near the edge and each turns on a
different engine. It is distinct from **Seed Corn**, where the future is knowingly spent, a deliberate
present-for-future trade in which someone chooses to consume tomorrow's capacity today: here the future is not
traded at all but structurally absent from the model, never entered and so never spent. It is distinct from **Time
Inconsistency**, where a plan optimal today is not optimal to keep tomorrow, a preference-reversal across time: here
there is no reversal and no changed preference, only a horizon the instrument could not reach. It is distinct from
**The Tyranny of the Urgent**, where the urgent crowds out the important in attention, an attention problem in which
the important is present and deprioritised: here the future is not deprioritised but outside the instrument
altogether. It is distinct from **The Law of the Instrument**, where the tool shapes the problem you see, a framing
of what counts as the task: here the tool's temporal edge excludes what is past it, a boundary and not a framing.
And it is distinct from **The Streetlight Effect**, where search goes where it is easy to look, a matter of ease:
here it is not ease but the model's boundary that excludes the beyond, which no amount of diligence within the edge
can reach.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the planner as
  short-sighted or the sponsor as careless, because the source finding is precisely that no one errs: the ask is
  reasonable and sizes the plan to the decision, the planner is attentive and far-sighted and counts every cost the
  instrument can reach, and the horizon is necessary because no instrument reaches to infinity. The exclusion is
  authored by no one and emerges from the finite reach of the tool, so the misfit is that an instrument usable
  because it has an edge is an instrument that excludes what is past its edge, and a boundary the tool draws without
  deliberation is a boundary nothing in the plan is built to flag.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a costless correction. The one move that carries the
  future is to look past the edge, extend the horizon, stress the plan against the years past its reach, and enter
  the beyond as a constraint, and it means costing years the instrument was built to bracket off, spending present
  effort and present numbers on a future that casts no shadow to justify it, defending a line item nothing in the
  plan flags, and treating a plan that reads complete as incomplete precisely where it looks soundest. The reckoning
  is a beyond made visible only by paying, continuously, the cost of doubting a plan that reads whole, not a free
  correction.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never claimed as
  the house's. The staging is the house's; boundary critique and the unexamined boundary judgement are Ulrich's,
  the marginalising framing is Midgley's, the system and its environment are Churchman's, and the necessary terminal
  horizon of a usable model is the finite-horizon literature's and Simon's on the bounded premises of a decision.
  The honest boundary, that the horizon is necessary and the future is not traded but structurally absent, is
  carried as carefully as the finding itself.
