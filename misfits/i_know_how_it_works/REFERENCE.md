# I Know How It Works: Reference

The research warrant for **I Know How It Works** (play and company not yet staged;
this warrant is drafted first, to prove the science and the distinction before authoring).
The dynamic is the illusion of explanatory depth: Rozenblit and Keil's finding that felt understanding
far outruns the mechanism actually held, with Fernbach and colleagues' political case in which the
illusion supports extreme positions a demand for mechanism, not reasons, undoes, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the illusion of explanatory depth as a **felt-understanding-without-the-mechanism structure**: a
person who feels they understand how something works, a device, a natural process, a policy, in real
mechanistic detail, whose felt understanding runs far deeper than the mechanism they can actually
produce, so the confidence holds unexamined and often anchors a strong or even extreme position, until
they are asked to trace the mechanism step by step, to generate the causal account and not merely to
give reasons, at which point the felt understanding collapses and the self-assessment drops. The
governing law is not that people are stupid or lying, but that **the sense of understanding a mechanism
runs far deeper than the mechanism actually held, an illusion specific to explanatory knowledge that
survives precisely because tracing the mechanism is almost never asked, so confident and even extreme
positions rest on an understanding that is not there and that only a demand to explain, not to justify,
exposes**. The play models the documented mechanics: the illusion of explanatory depth, felt
understanding far exceeding actual for how-it-works knowledge and far stronger there than for facts,
procedures, or narratives, and collapsing when the person tries to explain the mechanism step by step
(Rozenblit and Keil), and the political case, in which strong positions rest on that illusion and a
demand for a mechanistic explanation, not for reasons, both undermines the felt understanding and
moderates the position (Fernbach, Rogers, Fox, and Sloman), as the load-bearing vectors of the
structure, not as a tale of a fool or a liar.

It is distinct from the house's Second Nature, the curse of knowledge, where an expert who knows a thing
can no longer imagine not knowing it and so fails to convey it to a novice, a dyadic transfer failure
running between two people, one who holds the knowledge and one who does not: there the knowledge is
real and the failure is in passing it across, here there is no transfer and no second person, only one
mind's overestimate of its own causal grasp, a solo illusion about how much of a mechanism it actually
holds, exposed not by another's incomprehension but by its own attempt to explain. It is distinct from
Cheaper Not to Know, the house's rational ignorance, where the citizen knows they do not know and
rationally declines the costly learning for its near-zero return: there the ignorance is recognized and
chosen, an honest not-knowing, here the person does not know that they do not know, the ignorance
unrecognized and felt as its opposite, so the two are opposite failures of the same knowledge, a
knowing, chosen ignorance on the one hand and a confident, unaware ignorance that feels like
understanding on the other.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the person
  as a fool or a liar, because the source finding it stages is precisely that the illusion is universal:
  the felt understanding that outruns the mechanism is a general feature of cognition, present in the
  careful and the expert as much as the ignorant, and not a defect of the stupid (Rozenblit and Keil).
  The confident position rests on the felt understanding sincerely, no pose in it, and the illusion
  survives for a structural reason, that everyday reasoning and argument ask for reasons and
  justifications, which do not expose the gap, and almost never for the step-by-step mechanism, which
  does (Fernbach and colleagues). The unexamined conviction is authored by no one and emerges from the
  structure, a felt grasp that runs ahead of the actual and a discourse that never calls for the tracing
  that would reveal it, so the misfit is that a sincere and often careful person holds a confident view
  on an understanding they do not have and cannot see they lack.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a costless correction. The move that punctures
  the illusion, asking for the step-by-step mechanism, exists and works, but it is uncomfortable and
  rarely made, and it runs against the grain of how argument usually goes: giving reasons, the natural
  mode of persuasion, does not undermine the illusion and can entrench the position, and only a demand for
  the mechanism moderates it (Fernbach and colleagues). And the cure is carried no further than the
  evidence bears: the reliably replicated core is that the felt understanding falls when a person tries to
  explain the mechanism, while the downstream moderation of a political attitude is the more contested
  part of the finding, so the reckoning is a fix few will ask of others or of themselves, against a
  discourse built to trade reasons rather than trace mechanisms, and with even its effect on conviction
  held as a claim more fragile than the illusion it exposes.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never
  claimed as the house's. The staging is the house's; the illusion of explanatory depth and its
  specificity to explanatory knowledge are Rozenblit and Keil's, the political application and the
  mechanism-not-reasons puncture are Fernbach, Rogers, Fox, and Sloman's, and the condition the trap
  actually requires, a felt understanding of a mechanism far deeper than the mechanism held and exposed
  only by tracing it, is carried as carefully as the finding, including that the robust core is the
  illusion and its collapse under explanation while the moderation of attitudes downstream is a further,
  more contested claim. The scope is held to explanatory, how-it-works understanding, with policy as the
  field case, and not stretched to overconfidence in facts, skills, or predictions generally, which the
  illusion of explanatory depth is specifically not about.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per element. The play and its company are not yet authored; these are the
proposed load-bearing pieces the warrant commits the staging to carry, one per vector, no filler.

- **The Felt Grasp**: carries the subjective sense of understanding the mechanism (Rozenblit and Keil),
  confident and detailed-seeming, felt far deeper than the mechanism the person can actually produce.
- **The Shallows**: carries the actual thinness of the understanding (Rozenblit and Keil), a superficial
  grasp of what the thing does mistaken for a grasp of how it works, shallow exactly where it feels deep.
- **The Tracing**: carries the demand to explain the mechanism step by step (Rozenblit and Keil;
  Fernbach and colleagues), the causal account rather than the reasons, the one act that exposes the gap.
- **The Collapse**: carries the fall of the felt grasp when the tracing fails (Rozenblit and Keil), the
  self-rating dropping as the person meets the mechanism they cannot produce, and the conviction easing.
- **The Position**: carries the confident, often extreme stance held on the felt grasp (Fernbach and
  colleagues), a strong view resting on an understanding that is not there to hold it.
- **The Discourse**: carries the collective outcome (Fernbach and colleagues; Rozenblit and Keil), a
  polity arguing from unexamined conviction and polarising, each side sincere and no one lying.
