# Chesterton's Fence: Reference

The research warrant for [Chesterton's Fence](play_chestertons_fence.md). The title is Chesterton's
parable; the play runs on his rule of reform, the theory of tacit and inherited knowledge that
explains why a safeguard's reason goes unwritten, and the sociology of latent function that explains
why a working safeguard looks useless, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging Chesterton's Fence as a **systemic system**: a safeguard whose reason has been forgotten is
removed as useless, and the harm it silently prevented returns, not through recklessness but because
a working safeguard produces a non-event, so its cost is visible and its benefit is not. The play
models the documented mechanics, the safeguard with its reason unwritten, the forgotten purpose, the
invisible benefit that is a disaster averted, the sound reformer's case that no one can rebut because
no one can point to a harm that did not happen, the returning danger, and the discipline of
recovering the reason before removing the fence, as the load-bearing vectors of the structure, not
as a tale of folly. The governing finding of the source literature is the play's governing law: do
not tear down a fence until you know why it was put up, because the reasons for inherited safeguards
are often tacit, distributed, and unarticulated, and their benefits are latent, so the case against
them is always easiest to make while they are still working. It is the foil to the house's New
Normal: not a protective line eroded because each breach survived, but a protective fence amputated
because its reason was lost, two ways a forgotten safeguard fails, by drift and by the axe.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the
  reformer as a fool, because the source finding is precisely that the case to remove is sound: the
  fence's cost is real, its reason is genuinely unwritten and often genuinely lost, and no one can
  exhibit the harm it prevents, because a prevented harm is a non-event. The misfit is that a
  safeguard works by producing nothing, so it looks most useless exactly when it is most needed.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a rule of never touching anything. The
  remedy, learning the reason before removing the fence, is slow, is often defeated by a reason lost
  past recovery, and cuts both ways, because some fences truly have outlived their danger, so the same
  discipline that prevents the amputation can leave a system venerating walls whose purpose is dead.
  The reckoning is inquiry bought at the price of delay and of some fences kept too long, not
  vigilance winning free.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here,
  never claimed as the house's. The staging is the house's; the parable, the latent-function account,
  and the tacit-knowledge and inherited-reason arguments are theirs, and the counter-caution that a
  fence can be genuinely obsolete is carried as carefully as Chesterton's rule itself.
