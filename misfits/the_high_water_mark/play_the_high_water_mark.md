# Play: The High-Water Mark

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

The High-Water Mark.
A crisis arrives, a war or a slump or an emergency, and the spending rises to meet it, as it must. Taxes are borne that would not be borne in calm, programmes are stood up, commitments are made, and every one of them is the right answer to a real and present need. Then the crisis passes. The water recedes, but it does not go all the way back down: the programmes have beneficiaries now, the commitments have constituencies, the temporary measures have become the way things are done, and to lower the spending to where it was is no longer to decline a new expense but to take a standing one away from people who have built their lives around it. So the level settles above the old line, at a new plateau, and there it stays, marked on the wall like a flood's high-water line. When the next crisis comes it lifts the spending again, from the mark, not from the old floor, and leaves a higher mark when it recedes. Nobody was profligate. Each outlay answered a genuine need, each beneficiary is genuinely dependent, and the asymmetry that raising costs a little from everyone and cutting costs everything from someone is a plain fact of who notices and who organises. The floor climbs, crisis by crisis, and never comes back down, not because anyone willed a bigger state but because up is easy and down is dear, and a commitment, once someone stands on it, is a thing you can only take away by taking it away from them.

## Arc

The bend is this: spending rises to meet a crisis and does not fall when the crisis ends, so the temporary becomes the permanent and the floor ratchets up displacement by displacement, each rise sound and the accumulation unplanned (Peacock and Wiseman).
It runs on an asymmetry of ease.
Raising a commitment is easy: the need is real, the crisis is present, and the cost is spread thin across everyone, so the outlay is granted with little resistance (Buchanan and Wagner). Lowering it is dear, because by then the spending has a constituency, and the saving is diffuse while the loss is concentrated on the people who depend on it.
And the crisis lifts the level past the old line.
An emergency loosens what calm would not permit, taxes tolerated and programmes stood up that answer the moment exactly, so the spending crests to a height the ordinary years would never have reached, every measure of it the correct response to a real and present need (Peacock and Wiseman). The rise is sound, and it is steep.
And the level does not recede with the crisis.
When the emergency passes the spending does not fall back, because money once placed sticks where it landed rather than fungibly draining away, the programme now serving a purpose and a people it did not serve before, so the temporary measure beds in as the standing baseline and the plateau holds above the old floor (Hines and Thaler). What was raised to meet a crisis outlives it, and the recession of the water leaves the mark.
And a constituency forms to hold the mark.
Around every standing commitment gathers the group that depends on it, small and organised and vigilant where the taxpayer who funds it is large and diffuse and inattentive, so the beneficiaries defend their niche against any cut while no one of equal force champions the saving, and the commitment entrenches, cleared only by an upheaval as large as the one that raised it (Olson). The floor is held not by a plan but by the people standing on it.
And the marks accumulate into a climb.
Each crisis lifts the spending from the current mark, not the old floor, and leaves a higher mark when it recedes, so the plateaus stack one on another and the long line of public expenditure trends ever upward, the secular climb the sum of displacements that never gave their ground back (Wagner; Peacock and Wiseman). The ratchet is the shape of the history, up in steps and never down.
None of it is a blunder. The outlay that meets a real crisis, the beneficiary who comes to depend on a commitment they were invited to rely on, the taxpayer too dispersed to fight a cut that would save them a little: each is acting soundly, and the ever-rising floor emerges from the sum, no profligacy and no plan required. Let a commitment sunset when its crisis ends, hold the floor down against the pull of the standing programme, and the ratchet is broken (the sunset clause, the deliberate drawdown): but that means telling a real constituency that the thing they depend on is ending, bearing their concentrated resistance now for a diffuse saving no one will thank you for, and being the one who took it away, so the move that would lower the floor is a visible loss inflicted on identifiable people for an invisible gain to everyone, and the reckoning is a state that only ever grows, or a cut that costs the cutter everything and the beneficiary more.

**[The Constituency](plot_the_constituency.md)**
Cue: a standing commitment, and the group that lives on it. The Dependent gathers around the outlay, small and organised where the taxpayer is diffuse, and defends it against any cut. Closes when the commitment is entrenched, held by the people standing on it and no longer a thing that can simply be declined.

**[The Cut That Isn't](plot_the_cut_that_isnt.md)**
Cue: a reformer would lower the floor. The Cutter moves to end the commitment when its crisis is long over, and meets the concentrated resistance of those who depend on it against a diffuse saving no one champions. Closes when the cut is taken at the cost of the cutter and the constituency both, or declined for the ease of leaving the mark where it stands.

## Stakes

Whether a body can let its spending fall when the crisis that raised it has passed, or is left to climb by the plain asymmetry of a commitment easy to grant and dear to withdraw.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the mark that holds, which the Provider is right to fund in the crisis and the Dependent right to rely on once it stands and the taxpayer reasonable not to fight, and away from the drawdown that would lower the floor at a cost no one will bear. The production cannot make the rising floor a blunder, because each outlay is sound on the need it met and the lag between granting a commitment and being unable to remove it is a fact of who organises and who does not, so funding the crisis and defending the entitlement are each only answering the situation truly. It can only show that a level raised to meet an emergency outlives it, that the water recedes and the mark stays, and that the one move that would bring the floor down, sunsetting the commitment against its constituency, means inflicting a visible loss on identifiable people for an invisible gain to everyone, so the state climbs not by anyone's greed but by the ordinary good sense of every party who grants what a crisis needs and keeps what a calm still gives.
