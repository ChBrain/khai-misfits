# Play: The Dilbert Principle

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

The Dilbert Principle.
Someone on your team cannot do the work. Not for want of trying: they turn up, they mean well, the job simply overmatches them, and the output suffers for it. You cannot just sack them: it is slow, it is cruel, it invites a grievance, it wounds the team to watch, and it means admitting the hire was yours. So you do the kind and practical thing, the thing that solves your problem today: you move them up and to the side, into a role off the critical path, a title and a desk where they can do less harm to the work ... and it is a relief all round, the floor runs cleaner, the confrontation is spared, the person keeps their face. But every manager, facing the same hard sack, reaches for the same soft exit, and the place they all move the weakest to is the same: up. So the ranks that plan and direct and judge the able fill, one humane promotion at a time, with the very people who could not do the work, and the higher you look the less the job is understood, because rising was never the reward for doing it well but the escape hatch for doing it badly.

## Arc

The bend is this: promoting the one who cannot do the work is the kind move for one manager and, made by all, fills the leadership with the least able, so competence is sorted out of the seats that most need it.
It runs on a sack made costly against a promotion made cheap.
A worker overmatched by the job damages the output, and the manager must do something. The clean answer, to remove them, is the dear one: dismissal is slow and legally fraught, it costs severance and morale and the manager's own stomach for the confrontation, and it means owning a bad hire. The move up and out is the cheap one: no grievance, no scene, the person's dignity kept, and the damage lifted off the work that matters. For the single manager, weighing this seat and this person, the promotion is plainly the humane and rational choice.
And the cheap choice, made everywhere, sorts the organisation upside down. Because up is where the weakest are moved to spare the work, the management ranks accrete the overmatched, and a stratum built that way cannot easily do otherwise: leaders who did not master the work neither recognise mastery in others nor feel the lack of it in themselves, so they promote in their own image and shield their own kind, and the inversion locks. The seats that set direction, allocate the able, and judge who rises are staffed, tier over tier, by those the work defeated, so competence runs downhill and authority uphill, and the two part company exactly where the stakes are highest.
None of it is malice or folly. The overmatched worker is not lazy but simply misplaced, the manager who lifts rather than fires is being humane and prudent under real costs, and the promotion spares a cruelty and keeps a floor clean: no one schemes and no one is villainous, and the upside-down ranks are authored by no one and emerge from the sum of kind exits taken one at a time. What is a mercy to each worker and a relief to each manager is, in aggregate, a leadership that cannot lead.
Face it instead, have the hard conversation, coach the misplaced into a fit or manage them honestly out, build a way up that rewards the work and not escape from it, and the inversion can be held off: but the honest reckoning is the dear thing the whole trap exists to avoid, it asks the confrontation and the grievance and the admitted mistake the soft exit spared, and it must be pressed against a leadership that is itself the product of the trap and will not vote for the standard that would have stopped its own rise, so the competent ranks are bought only against every kindness and every cost the promotion was reached for to escape.

## Stakes

Whether an organisation can spare its overmatched the cruelty of the sack and still keep competence in the seats that lead.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the kind promotion, which each manager is right to reach for and all are led upside-down by reaching for, and away from the honest reckoning, which would keep competence and rank together and which is exactly the confrontation, the grievance, and the admitted mistake the soft exit was taken to escape. The production cannot make the upward exit fill the ranks with the able, because up is precisely where the overmatched are sent to spare the work, so the leadership accretes those the work defeated and the inversion is the mercy working, not failing. It can only show that no one is cruel or scheming, that the misplaced worker means well and the lifting manager is humane and the spared confrontation is a real kindness, and that the one move that keeps competence in power, the honest reckoning, must be made against every cost the promotion was reached for to avoid and against a leadership that is itself the trap's own product, so the kindness is real and the inversion is real and the higher you look the less the work is understood.
