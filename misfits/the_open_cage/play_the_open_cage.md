# Play: The Open Cage

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

The Open Cage.
For a while, nothing the animal does changes anything. It presses the lever and the shock comes anyway; it stops pressing and the shock comes anyway; whatever it tries, the outcome is the same, because the outcome has been unhooked from the trying. And it learns the truth of its situation, which is that here, effort and result have nothing to do with each other, so it stops trying, and lies down, and takes what comes. Then the situation changes. A door opens. Now there is a way out, a lever that works, an escape a single step away, and the animal does not take it, because it has learned that nothing it does matters, and that lesson does not know the door has opened. It does not test the lever. It does not try the door. It stays in a cage that is no longer locked, and the staying is not stupidity, it is the faithful memory of a time when trying truly was useless, carried past the moment trying would have worked. And people do this too: the worker whose every initiative was overridden until they stopped bringing any, and who keeps their head down now under a manager who would welcome one; the student failed so often they no longer open the book that would pass them; the one who learned, correctly, in a stretch of real powerlessness, that their actions did not count, and who cannot feel that the stretch has ended. And the cruel part is how it looks from outside. The one who has stopped trying looks lazy, looks unmotivated, looks like they lack the grit that others have, and the passivity that was taught by a spell of genuine no-control is read as a flaw in the person, so they are blamed for the very thing that was done to them, and the blame teaches them, once more, that nothing they do will change how they are seen. Nobody need be cruel. The stretch of powerlessness was often real and often no one's design; the manager who now would welcome initiative did not cause the years that killed it; the one who calls it laziness is only reading passivity the way passivity reads. It is only that a lesson true in one room is carried, faithfully, into a room where it is false, and the door stays open, and no one walks through.

## Arc

The bend is this: a stretch in which action is severed from outcome teaches the true lesson that effort does not pay, and the lesson outlives the stretch, so the actor goes on not trying once control returns and effort would now work, staying in a cage whose door has opened.
It runs on a lesson that was true.
When outcomes are made independent of actions, an actor that stops trying has learned something correct: in that situation, effort really did not change the result, so ceasing to spend it was the accurate response, not a failure of will (Seligman and Maier). The giving-up is a well-calibrated update to a real contingency, and this is what makes it stubborn, because it is not an error to be corrected but a truth to be unlearned.
And the lesson generalises and persists. The expectation formed, that nothing I do matters, does not stay pinned to the room that taught it: it spreads to new situations and outlives the conditions, so the actor carries the no-control expectation into a changed world where control has returned (Maier and Seligman; Hiroto and Seligman). The door opens and the expectation does not know it, and, expecting no control, the actor does not test for it, so the one act that would reveal the door is open is the act the expectation forbids.
And the passivity is misread as character. From outside, an actor who has stopped trying looks lazy, unmotivated, lacking in grit, so the observer attributes the passivity to a stable flaw in the person rather than to the stretch that taught it (Abramson, Seligman, and Teasdale). The misattribution deepens the trap: blamed for the giving-up, the actor learns again that nothing they do changes how they are seen, and the diagnosis of character becomes another uncontrollable outcome.
And it stays hard to reverse. Because the actor will not test for control, the expectation cannot be corrected by the actor alone: it takes an outside hand to arrange a graded, unmissable demonstration that action now works, against the actor's certainty that it does not, and even the capacity to expect control is itself something learned, an immunity the trapped actor was never given (Maier and Seligman, 2016). So the escape is not a thing the caged one can simply choose.
None of it is folly or fault. The stretch of powerlessness was real and often no one's design, the giving-up was the correct response to it, generalising a learned truth is what learning does, and reading passivity as passivity is the natural inference: no one need be cruel, and the trap emerges from a true lesson carried faithfully past its expiry. What kept the actor from wasting effort in a hopeless room keeps it from spending effort in a room where effort would now save it.
Arrange from outside a graded demonstration that control has returned, small enough to be unmissable and repeated until the expectation shifts, and credit the passivity to its cause rather than to character, and the actor can be brought to try again: but this means spending an outside effort the actor will not spend for itself, working against its settled certainty and the observer's verdict of laziness, and supplying an expectation of control that must be installed, not summoned, so the escape is bought by someone else opening the door and walking the caged one through it, again and again, until trying feels worth it once more.

## Triggers

**[The Severing](plot_the_severing.md)**
A stretch in which action is cut from outcome. The Overrider, by design or by chance, makes the Subject's effort count for nothing, the same result whatever it does. Closes when outcome and action have come apart on the Subject, effort and result shown to have nothing to do with each other.

**[The Surrender](plot_the_surrender.md)**
Cue: the lesson is true. The Subject, having learned correctly that effort does not pay here, stops trying and takes what comes, the giving-up an accurate response to a real contingency. Closes when the Subject has ceased to act, resigned to a situation where action genuinely changed nothing.

**[The Untried Door](plot_the_untried_door.md)**
Cue: the situation changes and control returns. A door opens, a lever now works, escape is a step away, and the Subject, expecting no control, does not test for it. Closes when the way out stands open and unwalked, the expectation outliving the conditions that taught it.

**[The Verdict](plot_the_verdict.md)**
Cue: from outside, passivity looks like character. The Judge reads the Subject's giving-up as laziness or want of grit, a flaw in the person rather than a thing that was taught, and blames them for it. Closes when the passivity has been credited to character, the blame becoming one more outcome the Subject cannot change.

**[The Rungs](plot_the_rungs.md)**
Cue: someone would open the door and walk them through. The Coach would arrange a graded, unmissable demonstration that action now works, repeated until the expectation shifts, against the Subject's certainty and the Judge's verdict. Closes when control is shown small enough to be felt and the Subject tries again ... or the demonstration is waived as coddling, the laziness pronounced once more, and the cage stays open and unwalked.

## Stakes

Whether an actor can be brought to try again, when the thing that would make it try is the thing it has learned not to do.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the giving-up, which the Subject was right to learn and all is deepened by, and away from the tested door, which would show control has returned and which asks an actor expecting no control to do the one thing its expectation forbids. The production cannot make the Subject try from within, because the not-trying is a true lesson faithfully kept, and the expectation of no-control is exactly what stops it testing for control, so the staying is learning working as learning does, not a weakness: the caged one cannot feel that the door has opened. It can only show that no one is lazy or at fault, that the powerlessness was real and the giving-up correct and the reading of passivity as character the natural inference, and that the one move that frees the actor, an outside hand arranging an unmissable demonstration that action now works and crediting the passivity to its cause, must be made by someone else spending the effort the actor will not, against its settled certainty and the verdict of laziness, walking the caged one through an open door again and again, so the lesson was true and the door is open and the animal that will not step through it is not stubborn but taught.
