# Play: Baumol's Cost Disease

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

Baumol's Cost Disease.
A string quartet takes as many player-hours to perform today as it did two centuries ago, and a nurse's bedside hour, a teacher's seminar, a carer's shift cannot be sped up without becoming something less ... yet all around them the factories learn to make more per hour every year, and wages rise across the whole economy to match, and because a player or a nurse could have taken a factory wage, theirs must rise too, so the service that never got slower grows costlier and costlier, year on year, until everyone agrees it is a scandal and no one can find who is to blame.

## Arc

The bend is this: the things that cannot be made faster get dearer forever, and no one wasted a penny.
It runs on two sectors and one labour market.
In the progressive sector, the factory and the chip, output per hour climbs, so the same wage buys ever more goods and wages can rise without prices rising. In the stagnant sector, the quartet and the ward, the labour is the product, and an hour of it cannot be compressed without cutting the thing itself, so output per hour holds flat. But the two share a labour market: a nurse who could earn the factory's rising wage must be paid near it to stay a nurse, so the ward's wages climb with the factory's while its output does not, and its cost per unit rises without limit.
None of it is waste. The factory owes its cheaper goods to real progress, the nurse earns a wage the whole economy sets, and the rising bill is not bloat but arithmetic, the price of the stagnant service measured against goods that keep getting cheaper. So the cost climbs, and it looks exactly like inefficiency, so someone reaches for the cure that works on the factory, more output per hour: raise the caseload, swell the class, speed the shift, and cut the labour, which cuts the cost by cutting the service, because here the labour was the service.
See it for what it is, and the answer is to pay the rising price out of a general prosperity that can well afford it, the same growth that made the goods cheap making the service dearer: but that asks an ever-larger share of spending for care and teaching and art, held against a reflex that reads every rising cost as a fault to be cut, and against the genuine waste that hides in the crowd of honest costs and must be told apart from them.

## Triggers

**[The Advance](plot_the_advance.md)**
In the factory, output per hour climbs year on year, and wages rise across the whole economy to match. The Machinist earns more for real gains, and goods grow cheaper. Closes when a rising general wage is set by a productivity the service does not share.

**[The Stubborn Hour](plot_the_stubborn_hour.md)**
Cue: the quartet, the ward, the classroom cannot be sped up without becoming less. The Server gives the same hour it always did, and, able to earn the factory's wage, must be paid near it to stay. Closes when the stagnant service's wage climbs with the economy while its output per hour does not.

**[The Rising Bill](plot_the_rising_bill.md)**
Cue: the cost of the service climbs, year on year, though nothing in it got worse. The Payer faces an ever-larger bill, finds no fault to explain it, and reaches for a culprit, while the Economist reads the same figure as plain arithmetic. Closes when the mechanical cost is mistaken for waste and a blame is sought.

**[The Squeeze](plot_the_squeeze.md)**
Cue: someone applies the factory's cure to the ward. The Cutter raises the caseload, swells the class, speeds the shift, and the cost falls only because the service does, since here the labour was the service. Closes when cost is cut by cutting the thing the cost was for.

**[The Fair Price](plot_the_fair_price.md)**
Cue: someone names the disease for what it is and moves to pay it. The Economist shows the rising cost is the price of prosperity, not a fault, and the Funder pays it out of the general growth that can afford it. Closes when the service is funded at its true rising price ... or the reflex to cut wins, or the genuine waste hidden among the honest costs is spared with them, and the squeeze resumes.

## Stakes

Whether a society will pay the true price of the things that cannot be made faster, or keep cutting them for a waste that is not there.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the factory's cure, more output per hour, and away from the stagnant service, whose output per hour cannot rise without the service falling. The production cannot make a nurse's hour or a quartet's hour compress without making it something less, so their cost must climb with the economy's wages forever, and climb looking exactly like bloat. It can only show that no one was wasteful, that the factory's progress is real and the nurse's wage is fair and the rising bill is arithmetic, and that the one thing that would keep the service whole, paying its rising price out of the prosperity that causes it, asks an ever-larger share for care and art against a reflex that reads every rising cost as a fault, and against the real waste that hides, and must be found, among the honest ones.
