# Play: The Last Layer

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

The Last Layer.
A society meets a problem by adding complexity: a new office, a longer road, a standing army, another tier of administration, and it works, so the next problem is met the same way. Early on the returns are enormous, because the cheapest and most rewarding solutions are taken first: a little organisation buys a great deal of order, a little irrigation a great deal of grain. But complexity is not free. Every layer laid down has to be fed and maintained forever after, and as the easy gains are used up each new layer costs more and returns less than the one before it. The apparatus keeps growing because growing is the only method the society knows, and the marginal return on it keeps falling, until the society is spending most of what it produces just to hold up the complexity it already has, buying almost nothing new. At that point the system is running to stand still, and a stress it would once have absorbed without noticing, a bad harvest, a frontier war, an epidemic, is now unaffordable. The support base, taxed to the edge and getting less and less back for it, finds that withdrawing from the whole apparatus costs them less than propping it up, and so the society does not fall so much as it is let go: it simplifies, sheds the layers it can no longer pay for, and drops to a level of complexity it can actually afford. Nobody blundered. Each layer was a rational answer to a real problem on the day it was raised, and the collapse, when it comes, is a rational economy for the people who stop paying, not a mystery and not a moral failure, only the far end of a curve that bent over long before anyone chose to notice.

## Arc

The bend is this: complexity is a problem-solving strategy with a falling marginal return, so a society that answers every problem by adding another layer takes the cheap high-return solutions first and the dear low-return ones after, until the standing cost of the complexity it has built outruns what that complexity yields, and the system, unable to afford its own apparatus, simplifies by collapse (Tainter).
It runs on a method that works until it does not.
A society meets a problem by raising complexity against it, and the layer solves the problem and stays, so the method proves itself and becomes the standing answer to every problem after, each new difficulty met by adding organisation, specialists, infrastructure, information (Service; Tainter). The layer is the unit of the strategy, and the strategy is to add another.
And the first layers pay handsomely.
The cheapest, most rewarding complexity is bought first, because a society solves its easiest problems before its hardest, so the early return on organisation is enormous and the cost is small, a little structure buying a great deal of order (Tainter). The return is real and, at the start, it is large.
And every layer bought must be fed forever.
Complexity is not free: each office, road, and army laid down carries a standing cost that never leaves, paid in the energy and surplus the producing base yields, so the upkeep of the apparatus climbs with every layer and does not fall when the layer stops earning (White; Tainter). The upkeep is the weight, and it only accumulates.
And the return on the next layer falls.
Because the best solutions were taken first, each further layer meets a harder problem at a higher cost for a smaller yield, so the marginal return on complexity declines exactly as the classical law of diminishing returns predicts, transposed from inputs on land to problem-solving itself (Turgot and Ricardo; Tainter). The curve rises, flattens, and bends over, and past its peak more complexity buys less.
And the society runs to stand still.
Past the peak the apparatus costs most of what the base produces merely to maintain itself, buying almost no new benefit, so the surplus that once funded growth is consumed by upkeep and the whole system is fragile, a stress it would once have shrugged off now beyond its means (Tainter). The margin is gone, and what looks like strength is a standing cost with nothing behind it.
And the base lets go.
Taxed to the edge for a return that has fallen toward nothing, the producing periphery finds that withdrawing from the apparatus costs it less than sustaining it, so support falls away and the society sheds the complexity it can no longer pay for, dropping by collapse to a simpler, cheaper level (Renfrew; Tainter). The descent is rapid and, for those who stop paying, it is an economy, not a catastrophe.
None of it is folly. The administrator solving a problem with a new office, the ruler funding the apparatus they inherited, the taxpayer withdrawing from a system that no longer pays them: each is doing the sensible thing, and the durable, ruinous overbuild emerges from the sum, no villain and no blunder required, which is exactly why it is so hard to stop before the return goes negative and so hard to call a collapse a choice.
Simplify before the curve goes negative and the ruin is spared: but to do so is to give up capacities the complexity bought, to disband the office and abandon the road while they still function, so the one move that would save the society is the one that looks, on the day it must be made, like throwing away everything that made the society great, and the reckoning is a simplification chosen in time or a collapse suffered later, never a way to keep the whole apparatus and pay for it too.

**[The Cheap Fix](plot_the_cheap_fix.md)**
The Administrator meets a problem by raising a new layer of complexity, and it works at a large return for a small cost, so the method proves itself and becomes the standing answer. Closes when the problem is solved and the layer stays, the strategy confirmed and the return still high.

**[The Standing Cost](plot_the_standing_cost.md)**
Cue: the layers accumulate and must be maintained. The Successor inherits the whole apparatus and funds its upkeep, which climbs with every layer and does not fall when a layer stops earning. Closes when the surplus goes to maintenance and the system runs to stand still, the weight standing whether it pays or not.

**[The Bad Bargain](plot_the_bad_bargain.md)**

Closes when the layer is added anyway, the method outliving the point where it paid.

Closes when support falls away and the system simplifies by collapse, the descent an economy for those who stop paying.

## Stakes

Whether a society can put down the complexity it can no longer afford before the complexity puts it down, or is held to a ruinous apparatus by the very capacities it bought.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward more complexity, which the Administrator is right to raise against a problem and the Successor right to fund once inherited and the base right to support while it still pays, and away from the simplification that would drop the apparatus to an affordable level while it still functions. The production cannot make the collapse a blunder, because each layer was a sound answer to a real problem and the upkeep is the honest price of solutions that worked, so adding the layer is the rational act and funding it the responsible one: the administrator meeting the problem and the ruler holding the apparatus up are each only doing the sensible thing. It can only show that no one overbuilt on purpose, that a strategy of answering every problem with more complexity, sound at every step, drives the marginal return to zero and past it, and that the one move that would spare the ruin, deliberate simplification, means forgoing capacities the society paid dearly for and still uses, so the apparatus holds not by anyone's folly but by the ordinary good sense of everyone who raised a layer, funded it, and could not be the one to tear it down.
