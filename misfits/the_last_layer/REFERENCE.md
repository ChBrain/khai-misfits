# The Last Layer: Reference

The research warrant for [The Last Layer](play_the_last_layer.md).
The dynamic is the diminishing marginal return on complexity: Tainter's thesis of collapse, the energetics that
price complexity's cost, the classical law of diminishing returns it transposes, the evolutionary rise of complexity
it meters, and the systems account of collapse it extends, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the diminishing returns on complexity as a **problem-solving-that-outgrows-its-return structure**: a society
answers each problem by adding complexity, which solves it and stays, so the standing apparatus grows layer by layer,
and because the cheapest and most rewarding solutions are taken first, each further layer costs more and yields less
than the last, until the upkeep of the complexity already built consumes nearly all the surplus the producing base
yields and the marginal return on more complexity falls to zero and past it. The play models the documented mechanics,
the layer added as the standing answer, the return that is large at first and falls at the margin, the upkeep that
accumulates and never leaves, and the descent by which a society unable to afford its own apparatus sheds complexity
and drops to a simpler level, as the load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of decadence or a single
fatal shock. The governing finding of the source is the play's governing law: complexity is a problem-solving strategy
subject to declining marginal returns, so a society that keeps solving problems by adding complexity reaches a point
where the apparatus costs more than it yields, becomes fragile, and simplifies by collapse, a descent that is for the
base that stops paying an economy rather than a catastrophe.

It is distinct from the house's Limits to Growth, where a growth engine is pushed on against a binding stock limit
until overshoot forces a decline: there the ceiling is a fixed resource stock and the failure is running the engine
past it, while here there is no single binding stock, the failure is the falling marginal return on problem-solving
complexity itself, each layer affordable when raised and the accumulation unpayable. It is distinct too from The
Brittle Optimum, where slack is optimised away until a system has no reserve to absorb a shock: there the trap is too
little redundancy bought for efficiency, while here the trap is too much complexity bought for problem-solving, the
opposite motion, an apparatus grown so costly that its own upkeep, not a missing reserve, is what the society cannot
bear.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the collapse as decadence,
  folly, or a moral failure, because the source finding is precisely that no blunder is needed: each layer of complexity
  is a rational answer to a real problem on the day it is raised, the upkeep is the honest price of solutions that
  worked, and the ruin is the sum of sensible acts, not the product of anyone's vice. The administrator meets the
  problem, the ruler funds the apparatus they inherited, the base supports the system while it pays, and the durable
  overbuild emerges from the aggregate, so the misfit is that a strategy sound at every step, answering each problem
  with more complexity, drives the marginal return to zero and past it, no decadence and no single fatal shock required.
  The collapse is a rapid simplification, and for the base that stops paying it is an economy, not a disaster visited on
  the innocent.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into an apparatus saved by better management. The one move that
  would spare the ruin is deliberate simplification, shedding complexity before the return goes negative, but that means
  disbanding offices and abandoning works while they still function, forgoing capacities the society paid dearly for and
  still uses, so it looks on the day it must be made like throwing away the very things that made the society great and
  is refused for exactly that reason. The reckoning is a simplification chosen in time or a collapse suffered later, not
  a way to hold the whole apparatus up and pay for it too, so the cure for a complexity that costs more than it yields is
  a loss of capacity no one at the apex will choose while the layers still work.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never claimed as the
  house's, and its reach is not overstated. The staging is the house's; the collapse-by-declining-return-on-complexity
  thesis is Tainter's, the energetics that make complexity costly are White's, the law of diminishing returns transposed
  onto it is Turgot's and Ricardo's, the evolutionary rise of complexity it meters is Service's, and the systems account
  of collapse it extends is Renfrew's, each carried as carefully as the finding itself: the misfit stages complexity
  outgrowing its return and credits that each layer was sound and the collapse an economy, not a judgement on those who
  built.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per element.

- **[The Layer](piece_the_layer.md)**: carries the unit of complexity added to solve a problem (Tainter; Service), the
  organisation, infrastructure, or specialism raised against a difficulty and kept.
- **[The Return](piece_the_return.md)**: carries the benefit complexity yields (Turgot and Ricardo; Tainter), large at
  first and falling at the margin as the cheap solutions are used up.
- **[The Upkeep](piece_the_upkeep.md)**: carries the standing cost of the complexity already built (White; Tainter),
  the maintenance paid in energy and surplus that climbs with every layer and never leaves.
- **[The Descent](piece_the_descent.md)**: carries the phenomenon whole (Tainter; Renfrew), the rapid simplification by
  which a society unable to afford its apparatus sheds complexity and drops to a level it can pay for.
