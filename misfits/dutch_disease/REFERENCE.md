# Dutch Disease: Reference

The research warrant for [Dutch Disease](play_dutch_disease.md).
The naming is _The Economist_'s and the model is Corden and Neary's; the play runs on their booming-sector
account, through the resource-curse literature and the sovereign-fund practice, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging Dutch disease as an **economic system**: a country that receives a resource windfall, whose factors
rationally move to the booming sector and whose new income is spent, so that the extra demand for non-traded
goods raises domestic prices and wages, the real exchange rate appreciates, and the traded manufacturing
sector, selling at the world price while paying the country's risen costs, is undercut abroad and outbid at
home and hollows out, taking with it the learning and export capacity a resource in the ground does not build.
The play models the documented mechanics, the windfall and the foreign exchange it earns, the resource-movement
effect that draws labour and capital to the boom, the spending effect that lifts the price of the non-traded and
so the real exchange rate, the traded sector crushed from both sides, the forfeited dynamic gains of the sector
lost, and the sovereign fund that alone spares it by saving the windfall offshore, at a cost, as the
load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of a corrupt government or a squandered fortune. The
governing finding of the source is the play's governing law: a boom in one tradable sector (or a windfall spent
at home) appreciates the real exchange rate and contracts the lagging tradable sector, so a genuine gain
deindustrialises the economy that receives it. It is distinct from the house's Seed Corn, where the future is
deliberately consumed for a present number: there a stock is knowingly eaten, here no one chooses to sacrifice
the factories, a real gain hollows them through relative prices. It is distinct from the house's Baumol's Cost
Disease, where a productivity gap makes stagnant services ever dearer: there the driver is unbalanced
productivity growth and the casualty is the cost of services, here the driver is a windfall's spending effect
and the casualty is the traded manufacturing base. And it is distinct from the house's Limits to Growth, where a
binding limit is met by pushing the engine harder: here the trouble is not a limit but a bounty.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the government as
  corrupt or the citizens as profligate, because the source finding is precisely that each move is rational:
  striking a resource is fortune and not fault, labour and capital moving to the best-paid work is right for
  each factor, spending a windfall is what a richer nation reasonably does, and the exchange rate only reflects
  the risen prices. The hollowing is authored by no one and emerges from the structure, a real gain spent
  through a currency that must rise, so the misfit is that a genuine bonanza, enjoyed as any windfall sensibly
  is, dismantles the traded base that would have carried the country after it.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a costless correction. The one move that spares the
  traded sector is to bank the windfall abroad and live on its return, sterilising the boom so the real exchange
  rate does not rise, and it asks a country flush with new money to lock its fortune away untouched while
  present needs go wanting, against every pressure of the boom and the ballot, and to hold that discipline for
  the decades the resource lasts. The reckoning is a windfall received and deliberately not enjoyed, refused at
  the moment it is most abundant and most tempting, not a free correction, so the cure for a country hollowed by
  its bonanza is the fortune saved and forgone.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never claimed
  as the house's. The staging is the house's; the naming is _The Economist_'s, the booming-sector model is
  Corden and Neary's, the resource-curse evidence is Sachs and Warner's, and the sovereign-fund remedy is the
  practitioners', and the condition the trap actually requires, a windfall spent at home through a real exchange
  rate free to appreciate against a traded sector that is a price-taker on world markets, is carried as
  carefully as the finding itself.

  that hollows the economy that receives it.
