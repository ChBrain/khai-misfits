# Play: Dutch Disease

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

Dutch Disease.
A country strikes it rich: gas under the sea, a mineral the world wants, a price that soars, and the money pours in. Incomes rise, the currency strengthens, the shops fill, and everyone does the obvious sensible thing with a windfall: the workers and the capital go where the pay is highest, to the booming sector, and the new wealth is spent, on homes and haircuts and everything a richer country buys ... and here the good fortune turns. Because the spending falls on things that cannot be imported, their prices rise, and a country whose prices and wages have risen is a country whose currency has strengthened in real terms, and a strong currency means the nation's factories, which must sell at the world's price and now pay the country's risen costs, can no longer compete. So the manufacturing that earned its way on export markets is undercut at home by the boom, its workers drawn off to the fields and its goods priced out abroad, and it quietly shuts. No one wrecked it. The boom was real, the wages were fair, the spending was reasonable, and the exchange rate only did what exchange rates do. But manufacturing was the sector that learned and improved and carried the future, and the resource is finite, so when the gas runs low or the price breaks, the country is left with neither the boom nor the base it traded for it, poorer for having struck it rich.

## Arc

The bend is this: a resource windfall is a genuine gain that each holder is right to chase and spend, and its spending lifts the real exchange rate and prices the traded sector out, so a real bonanza hollows the economy that received it.
It runs on a windfall met by a currency that can rise.
A boom in a resource earns foreign exchange and pays the highest wages, so labour and capital move to it, and the income it brings is spent. That much is only rational: factors go where the return is, and a richer country buys more. But some of what it buys, the house, the meal, the service, cannot be imported, so the extra demand falls on non-traded goods and drives their prices up, and rising domestic prices and wages are a real appreciation of the currency, whatever the nominal rate does.
And the strong currency guts the traded sector. Manufacturing sells on world markets at the world's price, which it cannot lift, while its costs, its wages and rents and inputs, have risen with the boom, so its margin is crushed from both sides: undercut abroad by the strong currency and outbid at home for workers by the booming fields. It shrinks, and shrinking, it takes with it the learning and the productivity growth and the export capacity that a resource in the ground does not build, so the country swaps a sector that compounds for a windfall that depletes.
None of it is folly or vice. Striking a resource is fortune, not fault, moving to the better-paid work is right for every worker, spending a windfall is what a richer nation reasonably does, and the exchange rate only reflects the new prices: no one deindustrialises on purpose, and the hollowing emerges from the sum, a real gain spent through a currency that must rise. What enriches the country in the moment dismantles the base that would have carried it.
Bank the windfall abroad, save the resource revenue in a fund held offshore and spend only its steady return, sterilise the boom so the currency does not rise and the traded sector is spared, and the disease can be held off: but that asks a country flush with new money to not spend it, to lock a fortune away while schools and roads and wages go wanting now, against every pressure of the boom and the ballot, and to keep the discipline for the decades the resource lasts, so the cure is a windfall received and deliberately not enjoyed, refused at the moment it is most tempting and most abundant.

The Boom earns foreign exchange and pays the highest wages, and the Mover, the country's factors and spenders, feels the new wealth arrive. Closes when the windfall is flowing and incomes are rising, the boom the best-paying thing in the land.

**[The Rush](plot_the_rush.md)**
Cue: everyone does the sensible thing with a windfall. The Mover sends its labour and capital to the booming fields and spends the new income, while the Boom draws them in with the highest returns. Closes when the factors have flowed to the boom and the windfall is being spent through the economy.

**[The Strong Rate](plot_the_strong_rate.md)**
Cue: the spending falls on what cannot be imported. The Mover bids up the prices of non-traded goods, so domestic costs and wages rise, and the Factory, watching its cost base climb, sees the real exchange rate strengthen against it. Closes when the currency has appreciated in real terms and the country's costs have risen above the world's.

**[The Hollowing](plot_the_hollowing.md)**
Cue: manufacturing sells at the world's price and pays the country's risen costs. The Factory is undercut abroad by the strong currency and outbid at home for workers by the boom, its margin crushed from both sides, and it shrinks. Closes when the traded base has hollowed out, its learning and export capacity gone with it.

**[The Fund](plot_the_fund.md)**
Cue: someone moves to save the boom instead of spending it. The Steward would bank the windfall abroad and spend only its return, sterilising the boom so the currency does not rise, against the Mover's pull to spend now and the pressure of the boom and the ballot. Closes when the windfall is saved and the traded sector spared ... or the money is spent, the currency rises, and the hollowing runs on.

## Stakes

Whether a country can receive a real windfall and not be hollowed out by it.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the spending, which each holder is right to do and all are deindustrialised by doing, and away from the saving, which would spare the traded sector and which asks a flush country to lock its fortune away untouched. The production cannot make the windfall enrich the country without pricing the factories out, because spending the boom raises the price of what cannot be imported and a country with risen prices has a risen real exchange rate, so the traded sector is undercut exactly as the wealth is enjoyed: the hollowing is the boom being spent, not being squandered. It can only show that no one is reckless or wicked, that striking a resource is fortune and chasing the better wage is right and spending a windfall is reasonable and the exchange rate only reflects the prices, and that the one move that keeps the base, banking the windfall offshore and living on its return, must be made against the temptation of new money and the needs of the present and held for the life of the boom, so the fortune is real and the hollowing is real and the country is left poorer for having struck it rich.
