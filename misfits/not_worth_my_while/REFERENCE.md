# Not Worth My While: Reference

The research warrant for [Not Worth My While](play_not_worth_my_while.md).
The dynamic is Geoffrey Rose's prevention paradox: a preventive measure that brings a large benefit to a whole
population offers almost nothing to each participating individual, so each rationally declines a negligible share
and the real aggregate is lost, while the tempting high-risk strategy is individually worth it and misses the many
at modest risk who supply most of the cases. Rose's 1981, 1985 and 1992 work is the spine, cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the prevention paradox as a **diluted-sum structure**: a real benefit is spread across a whole population
so thinly that each participant's share is negligible, so each person, weighing a real cost against a fraction of a
percent off a risk already low, rationally declines, and the sum, which lived only in the adding up of the shares,
is lost by no error but by many sound refusals. The play models the documented mechanics, the measure whose cost
is real and present while its personal benefit is negligible, the individual calculus that soundly refuses a bad
personal bargain, the aggregate benefit that is vast in total and nothing in any part, the arithmetic bulk of the
many at modest risk who supply most of the cases because a small risk borne by very many is most of the harm, the
high-risk strategy that is genuinely useful for its targeted few and structurally insufficient, and the costly
structural remedy that shifts the whole distribution so the many move together, as the load-bearing vectors of the
structure, not as a tale of apathy or short-sightedness. The governing finding of the sources is the play's
governing law: what is worth doing collectively can be not worth doing individually, because a benefit large enough
to matter to a population is diluted enough to be negligible to each person in it, so a rational population can
forfeit a real aggregate benefit with every individual reasoning correctly.

It is distinct from The Commons, and the two are close enough that the cut must be exact: there a rivalrous shared
stock is over-extracted, each individual gaining a real private benefit from taking more while the shared cost is
diffused, so the good is depleted by rational grabbing; here a collective good is undersupplied rather than a stock
depleted, and the individual's own benefit is genuinely negligible even when all act, so the failure is not
over-taking a real private gain but declining a real private nothing. It is distinct from Everybody's Business, the
bystander effect and the diffusion of responsibility, where the individual could act meaningfully and refrains
because they assume others will: here the individual's own benefit is truly negligible regardless of what anyone
else does, so declining stays rational even with no one else in the room and no responsibility to diffuse. And it
is distinct from The Firefighter's Medal, where prevention goes uncredited because the averted harm is invisible:
here the benefit is fully visible and honestly credited in the aggregate and still individually negligible, so the
loss is not of credit for a hidden good but of a good that no visible person's share was ever worth keeping.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the individual as
  apathetic or short-sighted or the advocate as a scold, because the source finding is precisely that no one errs:
  the personal benefit really is negligible, the individual's refusal is genuinely rational, the population benefit
  is real, and the high-risk strategy is genuinely useful for its few. The loss is authored by no one and emerges
  from a benefit diluted across a population so that each share is negligible while the sum is vast, so the misfit
  is that what is worth doing collectively can be not worth doing individually, and a strategy worth each person's
  while cannot reach the many whose small risks are most of the harm.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a costless correction. The one remedy that reaches the
  sum is to shift the whole population's distribution by structural means, regulating the environment and
  reformulating the supply, and it means paying a collective cost that falls on no one who chose it and benefits no
  one who can name their own saved outcome, moving the many together over the sound objection of every individual
  that the measure was never worth their while. The reckoning is a sum recovered only by structural means at a cost
  no individual selected, not a free correction won by persuading people harder.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never claimed as
  the house's. The staging is the house's; the prevention paradox, the population versus high-risk strategies, and
  the structural remedy of shifting the whole distribution are Geoffrey Rose's, set out in his 1981, 1985 and 1992
  work. The honest boundary, that the individual's calculus is genuinely rational, that the population benefit is
  real, and that the high-risk strategy is useful but insufficient, is carried as carefully as the finding itself.

- **[The Sum](piece_the_sum.md)**: carries the real aggregate benefit (Rose, the population strategy), large in
  total and held by no individual.
- **[The Bulk](piece_the_bulk.md)**: carries the many at moderate risk who supply most of the cases (Rose, the
  population versus individual distinction), a small risk borne by very many becoming most of the harm.
- **[The Dilution](piece_the_dilution.md)**: carries the prevention paradox whole (Rose), the benefit real in the
  sum and negligible in every share, so what is worth doing collectively is not worth doing individually.
- **[The Dose](piece_the_dose.md)**: carries the real personal cost of the individual calculus, the certain price
  paid now that the negligible share cannot repay.
- **[The Decline](piece_the_decline.md)**: carries the rational refusal of the individual calculus, the sound
  decision whose repetition across the many forfeits the sum.
