# Play: Not Worth My While

## Estate

## Name

Not Worth My While.
There is a measure that would help, and the help is real: take it across a whole population and the cases fall, the sum saved is large, the benefit sits there in the aggregate plain to count. So an advocate offers it to each person, one at a time, because a population is reached one person at a time, and each person does the only sensible thing, which is to weigh it. And here is the arithmetic that undoes the offer: the person weighs what the measure gives to them, and what it gives to them is almost nothing, because they are, like almost everyone, at low or moderate risk, and the chance the measure changes their own outcome is tiny. The dose has a cost, a nuisance, a swallow, a habit given up, an appointment kept, a small real price paid now and certain. And on the other side of the scale sits a personal benefit so small it cannot be felt, a fraction of a percent shaved off a risk that was already low. So the person declines, or half-complies, or forgets, and the decline is not laziness and not error: the personal expected benefit really is negligible, and against a real cost a negligible benefit does not pay, so refusing is the rational move and the person is right. And the advocate cannot answer them, because the advocate is telling the truth on both sides: the population benefit is real and the personal benefit is tiny, and there is no honest way to make the measure worth this one person's while, because the sum was never in any one person's share. The sum lives in the adding up. It is distributed across the whole population so thinly that each slice is nothing, and yet the slices, summed over the many, are vast, because there are so many of them. This is the paradox with its teeth showing: a benefit that is real in total and negligible in each part, so each part rationally declines its negligible share and the real total is lost. And the tempting escape makes it worse in a subtle way. Someone says: then do not offer it to everyone, offer it only to the few at highest risk, the people for whom the personal benefit is large enough to pay, the high-risk strategy, targeted and efficient and individually worth it. And for those few it is worth it, genuinely. But most of the cases do not come from the few at highest risk; most of the cases come from the many at modest risk, because there are so many of them, so a measure that helps only the high-risk few, however much it helps them, misses the bulk of the cases and cannot save the sum. The many at modest risk each carry a small risk, and a small risk times a large number is most of the harm. So the targeted strategy is useful and insufficient at once, and the untargeted strategy is sufficient and unwanted at once, and neither is a mistake. The only remedy that reaches the sum is the costly one: stop asking each person to make a bad personal bargain, and shift the whole population's risk downward by structural means, regulate the environment, reformulate the supply, move the whole curve a little so that the many move with it, at a cost that no individual pays and no individual chooses. Nobody is foolish here. The individual's calculus is sound, the advocate's population is real, the screener's targeting is efficient, and every one of them is doing arithmetic correctly. It is only that a benefit large in the sum and small in the share is a benefit each will rationally decline, and a strategy worth each person's while cannot reach the many whose small risks are most of the harm.

## Arc

The bend is this: a measure brings a large benefit to a whole population and almost nothing to each person in it, so each rationally declines a negligible share, the high-risk strategy that is individually worth it misses the many at modest risk who supply most of the cases, and the sum is reached only by shifting the whole distribution structurally at a cost no individual chooses.
It runs on a benefit real in the aggregate and negligible in the part.
The sum saved across a whole population is large and countable, but it is spread across everyone so thinly that each participant's expected benefit is tiny (Rose, on the prevention paradox, a measure that brings much to the population offering little to each individual). So the sum is real and the share is negligible, and this is the trap and not a fault, because there is no dishonesty on either side of the scale.
And each person weighs the share against the cost and declines. Because the dose carries a real cost, a nuisance or a price paid now and certain, and the personal benefit is a fraction of a percent off a risk already low, the person weighing them refuses, correctly (the individual calculus, a negligible expected benefit that a real cost outweighs). The refusal is rational, so it cannot be scolded away.
And the sum is lost precisely because each was right. Because the benefit lived only in the adding up and no one holds the whole of it, each rational decline of a negligible share subtracts a slice, and the slices summed are the sum, so the aggregate the population would have gained is forfeited by no error, only by many sound refusals of many small shares.
And the tempting escape misses the bulk. Because most of the population's risk sits in the many at modest risk and not in the few at highest risk, a high-risk strategy that targets only those for whom the measure is individually worth it, however efficient, misses most of the cases (Rose, on high-risk versus population strategies, the bulk of cases arising from the many at moderate risk). The targeted move is genuinely useful and structurally insufficient, worth each chosen person's while and unable to reach the sum.
And the sum is reached only by shifting the whole curve. Because the harm is the sum of many small risks, the only remedy that recovers it is to move the whole population's distribution downward by structural means, regulating the environment or reformulating the supply, so the many shift together at a cost no individual pays or chooses (Rose, on the radical structural remedy, altering the causes of incidence in the population rather than persuading individuals one by one). The remedy works by not asking anyone to make the bad personal bargain, and it is costly and unchosen for exactly that reason.
None of it is folly or bad faith. The individual's arithmetic is sound, the advocate's population benefit is real, the screener's targeting is efficient, and each is reasoning correctly, so no one errs and the sum is simply lost between them. What makes a measure worth doing at all, that it helps the whole population, is exactly what leaves it not worth any one person's while, and a strategy that is worth each person's while cannot reach the many whose small risks are most of the harm.
Shift the distribution structurally rather than persuade individuals one by one, and the sum can be recovered without asking anyone to accept a bad personal bargain: but this means paying a collective, structural cost that falls on no one who chose it and benefits no one who can feel it, regulating an environment and reformulating a supply against people who each, rightly, saw nothing in it for them, so the sum is reached only by moving the whole population at once, over the sound objection of every individual in it that the measure was never worth their while.
