# Vetocracy: Reference

The research warrant for [Vetocracy](play_vetocracy.md). The title is Fukuyama's vetocracy; the play
runs on the account of veto players and political decay, from the veto-player theory through the
decision-cost tradeoff, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging vetocracy as a **social system**: a body that has accreted checks, each a legitimate guard
against some past failure, so that a decision must clear every one of them, and because blocking needs
one veto while acting needs all, the many legitimate checks compound into an inability to act, at a
cost paid invisibly in decisions never made. The play models the documented mechanics, the veto that
is one hand's legitimate power to stop, the thicket into which the checks accrete, the asymmetry that
prices blocking at one and acting at every yes, the extraction by which a veto is spent as leverage,
the foregone decision whose cost takes the form of a thing that never happened, and the guard that is
the real protection each veto affords, as the load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of
obstruction. The governing finding of the source literature is the play's governing law: a system with
too many veto points, actors each able to block collective action, becomes unable to make decisions,
so its capacity to act decays even as each check remains individually justified, and the balance
between the capacity to act and the checks that make it accountable has no clean setting. It is the
governance cousin of the house's Anticommons: where the Anticommons fragments the right to use a
resource among many owners so the resource sits idle, vetocracy fragments the power to decide among
many actors so no decision can be made, one an underused thing and the other an unmade choice.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the
  veto-holders as obstructionists, because the source finding is precisely that each veto is
  legitimate: it was created to guard against haste, overreach, or a trampled interest, and is mostly
  used exactly as intended, and even the holdout who extracts a price defends a genuine stake. The
  paralysis is emergent, built entirely of legitimate blocks and chosen by no one as a whole, so the
  misfit is that a thing defensible one check at a time becomes, in sum, a wall.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a costless reform. The one move that
  restores the capacity to act is to cut veto points and concentrate authority, and it removes the
  guards and looses the very power the checks were built to restrain, trading a system that cannot act
  for one that cannot be stopped, against the plain fact that a vital check and a mere obstruction wear
  the same face and cannot be cleanly told apart. The reckoning is a capacity regained at the price of
  the accountability it was meant to have, not a free correction.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here,
  never claimed as the house's. The staging is the house's; the vetocracy diagnosis, the veto-player
  theory, the consent-cost tradeoff, and the sclerosis literature are theirs, and the condition the
  misfit actually requires, checks each legitimate whose sum is a paralysis and whose cost is invisible,
  is carried as carefully as the finding itself.
