# Play: Nothing to Lose

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

Nothing to Lose.
Liability is meant to make an actor careful by making it pay: cause a harm, owe the damages, and because you will owe them you take the care that costs less than the harm it prevents, so the threat of paying does the work of prevention before anything breaks. And it works, exactly and only, up to what the actor can pay. Because a judgment can be awarded in full but collected only against what the actor has, an actor whose potential harm runs past its own net worth faces a threat that is capped: it can be made to pay up to its assets and not a penny past them, so every pound of harm above that ceiling is, to the actor, free. It will never pay for it. And an actor is rational, and computes its care against what it will actually pay, so it invests in precaution up to the harm it can be charged for and stops, because guarding against the harm above the ceiling buys down a cost it was never going to bear. The care it sheds is precisely the care against the biggest harms, the ones that exceed its worth, because those are the ones whose damages it will never see. So the actor under-guards, not out of recklessness but out of arithmetic: it is fully liable in law and judgment-proof in fact, and the two together mean the law's full threat lands as a partial one. And then the actor, seeing that its exposure is capped at its assets, does the further rational thing: it keeps its assets low. It capitalises thin, it spins the hazardous work into a shell subsidiary with little of its own, it carries no insurance it is not forced to carry, because every asset it holds is an asset a judgment can reach, and holding less means owing less when the harm lands. Each of these is legal, each is a sensible use of limited liability, which is itself a deliberate and valuable institution built to let people take enterprise risk without betting everything they own. So the actor does nothing hidden and nothing fraudulent: its conduct is fully observable, its books are honest, it buys no insurer to dampen its care, and still the deterrence collapses, because the collapse needs none of those things. It needs only the ceiling. And then the harm lands, the real one, the one that runs past the assets, and the victim sues, and the court, in good faith, awards the whole of it, and the sheriff goes to collect and finds a shell with the assets of a shell, and the judgment above the ceiling is a number on paper that no one will ever pay. The victim of the excess is uncompensated, the actor that caused it under-deterred, and no one lied. Each firm's choice was privately optimal, legal, and rational; the sum across the actors who can cause more than they are worth is a standing under-deterrence of the very largest harms and a class of victims the law can name but cannot make whole. It is only that a threat you cannot collect is not a threat, that an actor guards up to what it will pay and no further, and that the ceiling which caps the payout caps the care, so the harms that matter most are the ones the law is least able to prevent.

## Arc

The bend is this: liability deters only up to the injurer's assets, so an actor whose potential harm exceeds its worth treats the excess as free, guards only up to the ceiling and structures itself to keep the ceiling low, and the sum is a systematic under-deterrence of the largest harms and a victim of the excess left uncompensated.
It runs on a threat that can be awarded in full but collected only up to what the actor has.
Liability works by making the injurer bear the cost of the harm, so it invests in care up to the point where care costs more than the harm it prevents (Shavell, on liability as the internalisation of harm, deterrence achieved when the injurer faces the full cost of what it causes). But collection is capped at the injurer's assets, so an actor whose harm can exceed its net worth faces the full cost only up to that ceiling and zero above it, and this is what makes it a trap rather than a wrong, because the ceiling is a genuine limit: you cannot collect from anyone more than they have.
And the actor guards only up to what it will pay. Because it computes its precaution against the cost it will actually bear, and it will bear damages only up to its assets, the actor invests in care against harm up to the ceiling and stops, treating the harm above it as free, so it under-invests in precaution against exactly the largest harms (Shavell, on the judgment-proof problem, the injurer whose assets are less than the harm taking suboptimal care). The care shed is the care against the biggest harms, because those are the ones it will never pay for.
And the actor keeps the ceiling low. Seeing that its exposure is capped at its assets, it does the further rational thing and holds fewer assets: thin capitalisation, the hazardous activity spun into an under-capitalised subsidiary, no voluntary insurance, because every asset held is an asset a judgment can reach (LoPucki, on judgment-proofing by asset structuring; Ringleb and Wiggins, on hazardous activity contracted out to small firms). Limited liability, a deliberate and valuable institution, is used exactly as designed, and the use lowers the ceiling further.
And nothing is hidden and nothing is fraudulent. The conduct is fully observable, the books are honest, and there is no insurer buying down the actor's care, so the deterrence does not fail from concealment or from risk-shifting: it fails from arithmetic alone, the payout capped below the harm, and it would fail the same way with perfect information and no insurance (Summers, on the disappearing defendant, full liability meeting an empty pocket).
And the harm lands and the judgment is uncollectible. The court awards the whole harm in good faith, the sheriff reaches only the assets, and the damages above the ceiling stand as a number no one will pay, so the victim of the excess is uncompensated and the actor under-deterred, each firm's choice privately optimal and the sum a standing failure of the law to reach its largest cases.
None of it is a villainy or a hidden hand. The actor is rational and legal, the court is diligent and full, limited liability is a valuable institution, and still the largest harms go under-deterred, because a threat you cannot collect does not deter and the ceiling that caps the payout caps the care.
Set the ceiling by hand before the harm: require minimum capital, compulsory insurance, or a posted bond up to the scale of the harm the activity can cause, or pierce the corporate veil when the thinness is contrived, and the actor is made to hold a stake up to what it can destroy so the care returns: but this means charging every actor for a harm most will never cause, pricing small and honest enterprise out along with the judgment-proof, forcing capital to sit idle against a tail, and drawing by hand the line between prudent limited liability and contrived thinness, so the deterrence is restored only by paying, in forgone enterprise and administered lines, the cost of a ceiling the market set for free.

## Stakes

Whether the law can deter the largest harms, when it can collect only up to what the injurer owns and the injurer guards only up to what it will pay.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the ceiling honoured, which the actor is rational to guard up to and legal to keep low, and away from the ceiling raised, which would charge every actor for a harm most will never cause and price the honest out with the judgment-proof. The production cannot make the threat collect past the assets, because you cannot take from anyone more than they have, and the actor's discounting is rational and its thinness is a lawful use of a deliberate and valuable institution, so the under-deterrence is liability working as liability does against a capped pocket, not a fraud: no one lied and each choice was privately optimal. It can only show that the conduct is fully observable and no insurer dampens the care, that the deterrence fails from arithmetic alone, the payout capped below the harm, that the harms shed are exactly the largest and the victims uncompensated exactly the worst hurt, and that the one move that restores the care, setting the ceiling by hand with capital, insurance, or a bond up to the scale of the harm, must be made by paying, continuously, the cost of charging for a harm most will never cause and drawing by hand the line between prudent limited liability and contrived thinness, so the actor is rational and legal and full in law, and the largest harm is the one the law is least able to prevent.
