# Nothing to Lose: Reference

The research warrant for [Nothing to Lose](play_nothing_to_lose.md).
The dynamic is the judgment-proof problem: liability deters harm only up to the injurer's assets, so an actor
whose potential harm exceeds its net worth treats the excess as free, under-guards against exactly the largest
harms, and structures itself thin to keep the ceiling low. Steven Shavell's judgment-proof result is the spine,
with Summers on the disappearing defendant, LoPucki and Ringleb and Wiggins on judgment-proofing by asset
structuring, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the judgment-proof problem as an **asset-ceiling structure**: liability internalises harm and so deters
it, but only up to what the injurer can be made to pay, so an actor whose potential harm exceeds its net worth
faces the full cost only up to its assets and zero above them, treats the excess as free, invests in precaution
only up to the ceiling, and rationally holds fewer assets to keep the ceiling low, each choice private and
rational and legal and the sum a systematic under-deterrence of the largest harms with the victims of the excess
uncompensated. The play models the documented mechanics, the asset ceiling that caps recovery below the harm, the
rational discounting of harm above the ceiling to zero, the under-precaution against exactly the biggest harms,
the lawful thinning of the balance sheet to lower the ceiling, the full judgment that is uncollectible above the
assets, and the costly remedy of minimum capital, compulsory insurance, bonding, and piercing the veil, as the
load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of fraud or concealment. The governing finding of the sources
is the play's governing law: a threat that cannot be collected does not deter, so full liability meeting an asset
ceiling below the harm leaves the largest harms under-deterred and their victims uncompensated, purely by
arithmetic, with fully observable conduct and no insurer required.

It is distinct from the house's Moral Hazard, and the two are close enough that the cut must be exact: there the
engine is hidden action plus insurance that dampens care, the insured party taking less precaution because a
policy has shifted the cost of the loss off it, so it needs both an unobservable choice and a risk-shifting
contract; here Nothing to Lose needs neither, for with fully observable conduct and no insurer at all the
deterrence still collapses, purely because the injurer's asset ceiling caps the payout below the harm, an
arithmetic ceiling on liability rather than a hidden-action or risk-shifting problem. The distinctness is the
whole point of the boundary: nothing is concealed, no insurer buys down the care, and still the largest harms go
under-deterred.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the actor as a
  fraudster or the court as negligent, because the source finding is precisely that no one errs: the actor is
  fully liable in law, keeps honest books, acts in the open, and uses limited liability as it was designed to be
  used, while the court awards the whole harm in good faith. The under-deterrence is authored by no one and
  emerges from an asset ceiling that caps recovery below the harm, so the misfit is that a threat you cannot
  collect does not deter, and an actor whose worth is less than the harm rationally treats the excess as free.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a costless correction. The one move that restores the
  care is to set the ceiling by hand, requiring minimum capital, compulsory insurance, or a posted bond up to the
  scale of the harm, or piercing the veil where the thinness is contrived, and it means charging every actor for a
  harm most will never cause, pricing prudent limited liability out along with contrived thinness, idling capital
  against a tail, and drawing by hand the line the market never had to draw. The reckoning is a deterrence
  restored only by paying, continuously, the cost of a ceiling the market set for free, not a free correction.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never claimed as
  the house's. The staging is the house's; the judgment-proof problem and the under-precaution result are
  Shavell's, the disappearing defendant is Summers's, judgment-proofing by asset structuring is LoPucki's, and the
  spinning of hazardous activity into thin subsidiaries is Ringleb and Wiggins's. The honest boundary, that the
  asset ceiling is a genuine limit, that the actor's discounting is rational, and that limited liability is a
  deliberate and valuable institution, is carried as carefully as the finding itself.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per element.

- **[The Assets](piece_the_assets.md)**: carries the asset ceiling (Shavell), the injurer's net worth as the limit
  a judgment can collect.
- **[The Excess](piece_the_excess.md)**: carries the harm above the ceiling (Shavell; Summers), free to the actor
  and uncompensated to the victim.
- **[The Judgment](piece_the_judgment.md)**: carries the uncollectible award (Summers; Shavell), full liability
  meeting the empty pocket, the judgment-proof wall.
- **[The Thinness](piece_the_thinness.md)**: carries judgment-proofing by structuring (LoPucki; Ringleb &
  Wiggins), the lawful thinning and spinning-off that lowers the ceiling.
- **[The Precaution](piece_the_precaution.md)**: carries the under-precaution result (Shavell), the care shed
  against exactly the largest harms.
- **[The Free Harm](piece_the_free_harm.md)**: carries the judgment-proof problem whole (Shavell), the largest
  harms under-deterred because they exceed what anyone can be made to pay.
