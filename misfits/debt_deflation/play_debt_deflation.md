# Play: Debt-Deflation

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

Debt-Deflation.
Times turn hard, and you are carrying debt, so you do the responsible thing: you pay it down. You sell what you can, tighten the belt, hand the money back to the lender, and get your house in order ... and so does everyone else, because they are prudent too. But when all sell at once to raise the cash, prices fall, and here is the trap: your debt is fixed in money, so as prices fall its real weight rises. The house you sell fetches less, the dollars you owe buy more, and the harder everyone pays down, the heavier what remains becomes. The more the debtors pay, the more they owe, because liquidation lowers the price of everything except the debt, and drives the very slump it was meant to escape.

## Arc

The bend is this: paying down a debt is right for one household and, done by all at once, raises the real burden of the debt itself, so the prudent act deepens the trouble it answers.
It runs on a debt fixed in money against a world whose prices can fall.
A debt is a fixed number of dollars, owed whatever those dollars come to be worth. In calm that is unremarkable, but when hard times set everyone to paying down at once, the way to pay is to sell, and a whole economy selling to raise cash drives prices down. Now the fixed debt meets falling prices, and its real weight, what it costs in goods and labour and assets to clear, rises. The borrower sells a field for half what it fetched last year, and the loan against it has not shrunk at all.
And the rise feeds the fall. Heavier debt means more distress, more forced selling, lower prices still, and lower prices raise the debt again, so the two turn each other downward: net worth collapses as asset prices drop under fixed liabilities, firms fail, the banks that lent shrink or break and stop lending, spending and prices fall further, and the real debt climbs through all of it. The economy tries to lighten its load and makes it heavier with every effort, the more it pays the more it owes.
None of it is folly or vice. Paying down debt in hard times is prudent, a lender calling a shaky loan is protecting itself, selling to raise cash is the only way to pay: each move is sound for the one who makes it, and the ruin is in their sum, the fallacy of composition run through the price of money, what is safe for one debtor impossible for all debtors at once.
Raise the price level back, or cut the nominal debt directly, reflate or forgive, and the spiral can be halted: money loosened, deficits spent, debts written down or restructured, until the real burden stops climbing. But reflation asks a hand to create money and run deficits exactly when caution screams to do the opposite, forgiveness asks creditors to swallow a loss and rewards the borrower who took the risk, and both cut against the deep instinct to let the debt be paid and the bust clear itself, so the cure is the thing every sound instinct forbids at the moment it is most needed.

## Triggers

**[The Call](plot_the_call.md)**
Hard times set in over an economy carrying heavy debt, and the reckoning begins: creditors call in shaky loans, and debtors resolve to pay down. The Creditor demands repayment, and the Debtor moves to sell and settle. Closes when the whole economy has turned at once to paying down its debts.

**[The Fire Sale](plot_the_fire_sale.md)**
Cue: to pay, they must sell. The Debtor liquidates assets to raise cash, and with everyone selling at once the Deflation sets in, prices falling across the board. Closes when the selling has driven prices down and cash has grown dear.

**[The Weight](plot_the_weight.md)**
Cue: the debt is fixed in money and the money is worth more. The Debt holds its sum as prices fall, so its real weight rises, and the borrower who sold a field for half its price finds the loan against it undiminished. Closes when it is plain that paying down has made the remaining debt heavier, not lighter.

**[The Spiral](plot_the_spiral.md)**
Cue: heavier debt drives more distress. The Deflation and the Debt turn each other downward, net worth collapsing and banks failing and prices falling further, the real burden climbing through it all. Closes when the economy is paying harder and owing more, the slump deepening on its own prudence.

**[The Reflation](plot_the_reflation.md)**
Cue: someone moves to halt the spiral. The Reflator would raise the price level or cut the debt, loosening money and spending deficits and writing debts down, against the Creditor's loss and every instinct to let the debt be paid and the bust clear. Closes when the level is lifted and the burden stops climbing ... or the paying-down goes on, and the spiral runs to the floor.

## Stakes

Whether an economy can ever pay its way out of debt, or only ever deeper into it.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the paying-down, which each debtor is right to do and all are crushed by doing at once, and away from the reflation, which would halt the spiral and which every sound instinct forbids in a bust. The production cannot make a general deleveraging lighten the load, because the debt is fixed in money and paying it by selling lowers the price of everything the debt is measured against, so the real burden rises exactly as the payments are made. It can only show that no one was reckless or vicious, that paying down is prudent and calling a bad loan is sensible and selling to raise cash is the only way, and that the one move that stops the descent, lifting the price level or forgiving the debt, must be made against the deepest instinct of the moment and at the creditor's expense, so the prudence is real and the spiral is real and the more the debtors pay, the more they owe.
