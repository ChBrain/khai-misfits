# Play: It Saved My Life

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

It Saved My Life.
A screening test finds a disease early, before it would ever have been felt, and the person is treated and lives. They are grateful, and they say so: the test saved my life. That is the most natural thing in the world to feel, and for some it is exactly true, because some of what screening catches would have grown and killed, and catching it early is a real rescue. But screening does not find only the lethal early. It also finds the indolent, the disease that is disease to the pathologist and no threat to the body, the kind that would have sat still and never surfaced in a lifetime, and the person who carries that kind is diagnosed, and treated, and lives, and cannot tell, from inside a living body, that they were never going to die. To them the survival feels like the rescue, because a survival that follows a diagnosis and a treatment is indistinguishable, in a single life, from a survival the treatment bought. So they are grateful too, and they say so, and their gratitude is real and their story is moving, and they become advocates: they campaign, they raise money, they tell everyone they know to be screened. And that is where the paradox turns, because the more a programme overdiagnoses, the more of these grateful survivors it makes, so the harm it does is converted into the demand that expands it, and a programme that finds a great deal of harmless disease is not exposed by it but beloved for it. Nobody lied. The doctor believes in early detection, the patient is sincerely thankful, the advocate campaigns from real feeling, and the survival statistics rise honestly, because finding disease sooner and finding disease that never needed finding both lift the survival rate without postponing a single death. The test is most loved exactly where it overdiagnoses most, and the gratitude that surrounds it is not evidence that it works but, in the part that comes from the overdiagnosed, evidence of the harm it does.

## Arc

The bend is this: the more a screening programme overdiagnoses, the more grateful survivors it manufactures, and because those survivors become its advocates, its popularity rises with the harm it does, so the demand that expands it is generated by the very overdiagnosis that should count against it (Raffle and Gray).
It runs on a detection that finds the harmless.
Because screening looks for disease before the body would ever report it, it catches not only the lethal early but the indolent that would never have surfaced, so a share of every round is overdiagnosis, real disease to the pathologist and no threat by its nature, found in people who would have lived out their lives never knowing (Welch and Black). The detection is honest, and it cannot sort the two kinds at the moment of the catch.
And the overdiagnosed cannot know they were not saved.
Because no one can tell, in a single body, the tumour that would have killed from the one that would have sat still, the person treated for the harmless kind lives and reads the living as rescue, their survival indistinguishable from inside from a survival the treatment truly bought (Zelen and Feinleib). The not-knowing is nobody's fault, and it turns a harm into a gratitude.
And the grateful become the programme's advocates.
Because gratitude for a life believed saved is real and moving, the overdiagnosed tell their story, raise money, and press the test on everyone they know, so the programme draws its most fervent support from precisely the people it overdiagnosed, the harmed speaking for the thing that harmed them (Raffle and Gray). The advocacy is sincere, and it is the harm wearing the face of a rescue.
And the advocacy inflates the demand that makes more of them.
Because each grateful voice draws more of the well in to be screened, the programme expands, finds more harmless disease, and mints more grateful survivors, so the loop turns and gathers, popularity feeding screening feeding overdiagnosis feeding popularity (Raffle and Gray). The turning needs no push, each round supplying the next its advocates.
And the numbers rise exactly as it overdiagnoses.
Because survival is counted from the moment of diagnosis, both finding the lethal earlier and finding the harmless at all lift the five-year survival without deferring one death, so the programme's own favourite statistic climbs in step with the overdiagnosis, the metric most cited to prove it works moving with the harm and not the good (Welch, Schwartz and Woloshin). The rise is arithmetic, and it looks just like success.
And the true measure arrives cold and late.
Because only a randomised comparison of screened against unscreened, counted to death and not to diagnosis, can tell a life saved from a life merely relabelled, the honest finding takes years and comes back a mortality unmoved and an incidence inflated, a quiet null set against a room full of moving testimony (Bailar). The measure is right and unwelcome, and it persuades fewer than one grateful survivor.
None of it is a fix. The doctor who offers the test believes in early detection, the person treated for the harmless kind is sincerely grateful, the advocate campaigns from real feeling, and the survival curve rises honestly: each acts soundly, and the beloved harm emerges from the sum, no fraud and no liar required. Correct it, and the correction is dear: it means a randomised trial counted to mortality, years long and against the grain of every instinct to find disease early, and then telling a grateful constituency that the test they credit with their lives postpones few or none and overdiagnoses many, against every moving story and the programme's own climbing numbers, so the one move that reads the programme rightly must outlast the testimony and outvote the people the programme made. Short of it the harm is measured in the very gratitude that hides it, a null result no one wants against a survivor everyone believes, and a programme most loved exactly where it overdiagnoses most, its supporters manufactured from its harm and sincere in every word.

## Triggers

**[The Catch](plot_the_catch.md)**
A screening round and a body that feels nothing. The Physician finds a disease before it would ever have spoken, some of it lethal and caught in time and some of it the harmless kind that would never have surfaced, and treats what is found without any way to tell the two apart at the moment of the catch. Closes when the harmless kind is diagnosed and treated in a person who would have lived out their life never knowing, a rescue and an overdiagnosis recorded identically.

**[The Thank-You](plot_the_thank_you.md)**
Cue: a person treated and living. The Patient, told the disease was caught early and dealt with, lives on and credits the test, unable to tell from inside a living body that they were never going to die. Closes when the survival is read as a rescue and the gratitude forms, the overdiagnosed thanking the test for a life it never had to save.

**[The Campaign](plot_the_campaign.md)**
Cue: a grateful survivor with a story to tell. The Patient campaigns, raises money, and presses the test on the well, and the programme expands on the strength of the testimony, screening more and finding more of the harmless kind. Closes when the advocacy inflates the demand and the loop turns, the harmed grown into the programme's constituency and the next round of overdiagnosis its next round of advocates.

**[The Cold Trial](plot_the_cold_trial.md)**
Cue: someone would measure it truly. The Trialist runs a randomised comparison counted to death rather than to diagnosis, and finds the mortality unmoved and the incidence inflated, a life saved indistinguishable from a life relabelled except in the count of the dead. Closes when the null returns years late against a room full of testimony, the true measure arriving cold and outvoted, or the trial never run and the gratitude believed.

## Stakes

Whether the gratitude around a screening programme is evidence that it works, or is in part the sound of the harm it does, the overdiagnosed thanking the test that overdiagnosed them because no one can tell rescue from relabelling inside a single living body.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the beloved harm, which the Physician is right to believe in catching disease early, and the Patient right to be grateful for a life they believe was saved, and away from the measure that would tell a rescue from an overdiagnosis at the cost of years and of contradicting the grateful. The production cannot make the programme a fraud, because the detection is honest and the gratitude is sincere and the survival rate rises by arithmetic and not by lying, so screening the well and thanking the test and citing the climbing numbers are each only reasonable acts. It can only show that a programme which overdiagnoses manufactures its own advocates from the people it harms, that the more harmless disease it finds the more grateful survivors it makes and the more beloved it grows, so its popularity tends to rise with its overdiagnosis and not with the lives it saves, and that the one measure which could tell the two apart, a randomised count to mortality, arrives late and cold and less persuasive than a single moving story, so the harm is hidden not by anyone's deceit but by the ordinary gratitude of everyone who lived after being found.
