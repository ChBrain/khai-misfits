# Pitch: Loved for the Harm It Does

## Taxonomy

[It Saved My Life](play_it_saved_my_life.md). The single hook: a screening programme that overdiagnoses turns the people it harms into grateful survivors, and grateful survivors become advocates, so the more harmless disease it finds the more beloved it grows, its popularity rising with the harm and not the good.

## Owner

- Project: khai-misfits

## Tenor

A test caught it early, so it saved a life, and the saved are grateful.
The tenor is the plainest gratitude: disease found early and dealt with, a person alive who might not have been, thanks owed to the test and freely given, exactly the feeling anyone would have and exactly the case for screening more.

## Undertow

Some of the saved were never dying.
Underneath, a share of what screening finds is the harmless kind that would never have surfaced, so some of the grateful were treated for a disease that was never going to kill them, alive not because the test rescued them but because there was nothing to be rescued from, and unable to tell the difference from inside a living body.

## Nerve

The harmed are the advocates, so popularity rises with overdiagnosis.
The nerve is that the overdiagnosed, sincerely grateful, become the programme's most fervent supporters, campaigning and pressing the test on others, so the more harmless disease a programme finds the more advocates it manufactures, and its support grows with the harm it does rather than the lives it saves.

## Echo

The one true measure is cold, late, and outvoted.
What lingers is that only a randomised count to mortality can tell a life saved from a life relabelled, and it arrives years later as a quiet null against a room full of moving testimony, less persuasive than a single grateful survivor, so the harm stays hidden inside the gratitude that is taken for proof.
