# It Saved My Life: Reference

The research warrant for [It Saved My Life](play_it_saved_my_life.md).
The dynamic is the popularity paradox of screening: Raffle and Gray's finding that overdiagnosis makes a
programme more beloved, Welch and Black's account of overdiagnosis, Welch, Schwartz and Woloshin on the survival
statistic that misleads, Zelen and Feinleib's lead-time bias beneath it, and Bailar's insistence on mortality
in a randomised comparison, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the popularity paradox of screening as a **the-harmed-become-the-advocates structure**: a screening programme
finds disease before the body would report it, and a share of what it finds is the harmless kind that would never have
surfaced, so a share of the people it treats are overdiagnosed, alive not because the test rescued them but because there
was nothing to be rescued from. Because no one can tell, in a single body, a lethal disease caught in time from a
harmless one found needlessly, the overdiagnosed credit the test with their lives and, sincerely grateful, become its
advocates, so the more harmless disease a programme finds the more grateful survivors it manufactures and the more
beloved it grows. The play models the documented mechanics, the overdiagnosis at the root, the indistinguishability that
turns it to gratitude, the advocacy that grows the demand, the survival rate that rises with the harm, and the
randomised mortality count that alone could tell rescue from relabelling, as the load-bearing vectors of the structure,
not as a tale of a huckster selling a useless test or a fool taken in. The governing finding of the source is the play's
governing law: the greater the overdiagnosis, the more people believe they owe their lives to the programme, so a
screening programme's popularity can rise with the harm it does rather than the benefit, its most fervent support drawn
from the very people it overdiagnosed. It is a tendency and not an iron law, holding where overdiagnosis is substantial
and the disease's course cannot be read in the individual, and weakening where a programme's benefit is large and its
overdiagnosis small, so the play stages the pull of the loop, not a rule that every screening programme is loved for
harm.

It is distinct from the house's The Survivors, where a sample is biased by passive attrition, units failing or
disappearing so that only the survivors remain to be read, and an analyst draws a false lesson from the residue: there
the selection is passive and the error is inferential, a mistake made by someone reading a filtered sample. Here the
survivors are not a residue to be read but agents who act: the overdiagnosed do not merely bias a statistic, they credit
the test, campaign, and grow the programme, so the engine is a self-reinforcing feedback loop driven by the survivors'
own gratitude, and the harm is not a lesson misread but a demand manufactured. The Survivors mistakes a sample; It Saved
My Life builds a constituency, the biased survivors turned from a passive residue into the active base that expands the
thing that produced them. It is distinct too from First Do Harm, whose engine is the pressure to act producing
iatrogenic harm, in that the load-bearing move here is not the harm but the popularity it earns, and from Metric
Fixation, whose number is a target gamed, in that the survival rate here is honestly computed and honestly misleading,
inflated by lead time and overdiagnosis with no one gaming anything.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the doctor as a huckster or
  the patient as a dupe, because the source finding is precisely that no bad faith and no gullibility is needed: the
  physician believes in catching disease early and genuinely saves some (Welch and Black), the patient is sincerely
  grateful and cannot from inside one life tell a rescue from an overdiagnosis (Welch and Black), the advocate
  campaigns from real feeling, and the survival rate rises by honest arithmetic (Welch, Schwartz and Woloshin). So the
  misfit is that a programme which overdiagnoses manufactures its own grateful advocates and grows more beloved the more
  it harms, no fraud and no folly required, the screening and the gratitude and the campaigning each sound and the
  beloved harm the sum, the doctor who screens the well as reasonable as the survivor who thanks the test.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a programme corrected by a single clear head. The one measure
  that tells a saving from a relabelling is a randomised comparison counted to disease-specific mortality (Bailar), and
  it arrives years late, often null, and less persuasive than one grateful survivor, so the correction rests on a finding
  quieter than the testimony it corrects and dearer than the demand it must resist. To act on it is to curtail a beloved
  test over the objection of the constituency the programme created, telling grateful people that the thing they credit
  with their lives harmed many of them, so the true reading arrives as a cold rate against a warm story and is outvoted
  by the people it would spare, not a way to read the programme rightly and be thanked for it.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never claimed as the
  house's, and its reach is not overstated. The staging is the house's; the popularity paradox is Raffle and Gray's, the
  account of overdiagnosis is Welch and Black's, the survival-statistic bias is Welch, Schwartz and Woloshin's, the
  lead-time bias beneath it is Zelen and Feinleib's, and the insistence on mortality in a randomised comparison is
  Bailar's, each carried as carefully as the finding itself: the misfit stages a programme loved for the harm it does and
  credits that no one lied and the gratitude is sincere, holding it a tendency where overdiagnosis is substantial and not
  a law that every screening programme is beloved for harm.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per element.

- **[The Constituency](piece_the_constituency.md)**: carries the popularity paradox whole (Raffle and Gray), the
  programme's base of grateful advocates built from the people it overdiagnosed, growing with the harm and defending it.
- **[The Harmless Kind](piece_the_harmless_kind.md)**: carries overdiagnosis (Welch and Black), the indolent disease found
  and treated that would never have surfaced, indistinguishable from the lethal at the moment of the catch.
- **[The Survival Rate](piece_the_survival_rate.md)**: carries the survival-statistic bias (Welch, Schwartz and Woloshin),
  inflated by lead time (Zelen and Feinleib) and by overdiagnosis, climbing with the harm and cited as proof.
- **[The Endpoint](position_the_endpoint.md)**: carries the mortality correction (Bailar), the randomised count to death
  that alone separates a saving from a relabelling, arriving late and cold against the testimony.
