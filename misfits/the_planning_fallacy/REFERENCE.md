# The Planning Fallacy: Reference

The research warrant for [The Planning Fallacy](play_the_planning_fallacy.md).
The term is Kahneman and Tversky's; the play runs on the inside-view account of prediction, from their
naming through Buehler, Griffin and Ross's experiments and Flyvbjerg's reference-class forecasting, all
cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the planning fallacy as a **forecasting system**: an estimate of a task's time and cost, built from
the inside view of the task's own plan, comes out too low, because the plan is the run in which nothing in
particular goes wrong and the reality is that run minus the swarm of small surprises no plan can name, so the
forecast built most carefully from the case in hand is beaten by the record of cases like it. The play models
the documented mechanics, the inside view that reasons from this task's specifics, the outside view held in
the base rate of similar tasks and set aside as not applicable, the unforeseen that the plan omits by
construction and reality supplies without fail, the estimate shaded lower still where a low number wins the
approval, the overrun that reads the gap only after the fact, and the reference-class forecast that answers it
at a symmetric cost, as the load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of carelessness. The
governing finding of the source literature is the play's governing law: prediction from the inside view of a
singular case ignores the distribution of outcomes it belongs to, and because a plan traces the successful
scenario, the estimate it yields is optimistic in one direction, so a forecast is corrected not by planning
harder but by pricing the task from the class of tasks it resembles. It is distinct from the house's Seed
Corn, where the future is spent for the present by deliberate choice: here nothing is traded away on purpose,
the shortfall is an honest error of view. It is distinct from the house's Watermelon Reporting, where a known
red is dressed green up a hierarchy: here the estimate is sincerely believed by the one who makes it, not a
truth concealed. And it is distinct from the house's Overfitting, its kin in reasoning from the particular:
there a model built to match one sample's noise overshoots into it, where here a forecast built from one
case's plan never consults the distribution at all.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the planner as
  careless or the estimate as a lie, because the source finding is precisely that each move is right: laying
  the task out is the job, the plan is the best knowledge there is, the estimate is sincerely meant, and no
  one can forecast which particular surprise will come. Where a low number also wins the approval, the shading
  lower is an incentive acting on an honest optimism, not a fraud replacing it. The overrun is authored by no
  error and emerges from the structure, a forecast built from the one scenario a plan can draw and a reality
  built from all the ones it cannot, so the misfit is that a task estimated exactly as it should be, thought
  through as hard as it can be, is promised too soon.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a costless correction. The one move that prices the
  task right is to set the estimate from the record of similar tasks and not from the plan of this one, and it
  returns a figure no one wants to hear, discards the genuine particulars of the case, depends on a reference
  class and the honesty to belong to it, and pressed too hard pads the estimate until the work expands to fill
  the buffer. The reckoning is a date deliberately made later in the promise to be met in the world, a bet on
  how much the record should outweigh the plan, not a free correction, so the cure for an estimate too soon is
  the standing risk of one too slack.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never
  claimed as the house's. The staging is the house's; the planning fallacy, the inside and outside views,
  reference-class forecasting, and the optimism and strategic misrepresentation behind cost overruns are
  theirs, and the condition the trap actually requires, a singular task estimated from its own plan while the
  distribution of like tasks is set aside, is carried as carefully as the finding itself.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per element.

- **[The Plan](piece_the_plan.md)**: carries the inside view (Kahneman and Tversky), the estimate reasoned from
  this task's own steps, vivid and particular and drawn along the run where the work goes well.
- **[The Base Rate](piece_the_base_rate.md)**: carries the outside view (Kahneman and Tversky; Flyvbjerg), the
  distribution of how like tasks really went, present and correcting and set aside as not this case.
- **[The Estimate](piece_the_estimate.md)**: carries the forecast that goes out (Lovallo and Kahneman), low by
  honest optimism and, where a low number wins the approval, lower by the incentive that meets it.
- **[The Unforeseen](piece_the_unforeseen.md)**: carries the swarm of small surprises (Buehler, Griffin and
  Ross), each unpredictable and so unnameable in the plan, together certain and so unfailing in the world.
- **[The Overrun](piece_the_overrun.md)**: carries the gap between estimate and actual (Flyvbjerg), the fallacy
  quantified, invisible while the plan is drawn and legible only once the work is done.
- **[The Padding](piece_the_padding.md)**: carries the far arm of the tradeoff (Parkinson), the buffer added too
  freely and consumed by a task that expands to fill it, the symmetric waste on the other side of the fault.
- **[The Planning Fallacy](piece_the_planning_fallacy.md)**: carries the phenomenon whole (Kahneman and
  Tversky), a task estimated from its own plan and promised too soon, and its remedy in a forecast taken from
  the record at a symmetric cost.
