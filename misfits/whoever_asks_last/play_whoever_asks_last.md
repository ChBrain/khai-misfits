# Play: Whoever Asks Last

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

Whoever Asks Last.
A room of people must choose among options, and they do it the fair way: each ranks the options by their honest lights, the rankings are counted, the majority carries. Every person in the room is reasonable, their preferences settled and consistent, no one confused and no one lying. And still the group can want nothing in particular. Put the options against each other two at a time and a majority can prefer A to B, and a majority B to C, and a majority C to A, a ring with no head, so there is no option the group prefers to all the others and no honest reading of the votes that names one. What names one is the order of the questions. Ask A against B first and put the winner against C, and one option takes it; ask B against C first, and another does; the same votes, the same sincere rankings, a different winner for every running order, and the winner is chosen not by the room but by whoever set the order the room voted in. No one need rig a thing: the ring is in the honest preferences themselves, and someone has to decide what gets asked when, so the agenda decides, and whoever asks last is holding the pen. And there is no fair rule that fixes it, because any rule that always gives the group a clear and consistent choice, honours a unanimous preference, and lets no single person dictate, must, once there are three options in play, break one of those promises: the coherence can be bought only by narrowing what people are allowed to want, or by letting intensity and comparison back in, or by handing one seat the deciding say, each a price paid in a fairness the vote was supposed to keep. So the room decides, and the deciding is real, and it is nobody's will made law: not because the room was fooled or divided against itself, but because a group of reasonable people, each wanting something clear, can add up to a group that wants nothing clear at all, and then the order of the asking is the only thing left to settle it.

## Arc

The bend is this: rational, sincere individual rankings can aggregate into a majority preference that cycles, so the group has no coherent choice of its own, and the winner is set by the order in which the options are put to the vote, the coherence supplied by the agenda and not by the assembly, and no fair aggregation rule can supply it instead (Condorcet; Arrow).
It runs on preferences that are each in order.
Because every voter can hold a complete and consistent ranking, honestly reported, individual rationality is not in question anywhere in the room, so whatever goes wrong is not a fault in any person but a property of the adding-up, the incoherence emerging from sound parts and belonging to none of them (Condorcet). The rankings are clean, and the trouble is only in their sum.
And the sum has no head.
Because pairwise majorities over three or more options can run in a ring, a majority for A over B and B over C and C over A, there is no option that beats all the others and so none the group can be said to prefer, and in more than one dimension of choice this is the general case and not the exception, no centre holding that a majority will not walk away from (Condorcet; McKelvey). The ring is real, and it leaves the group with no top.
And so the order decides.
Because a winner must still be found and the options can only be taken a pair at a time, the sequence of those pairings selects the survivor, and whoever sets the sequence selects the winner, steering a room of sincere voters to any option they like by the running order alone, no vote miscast and no preference misread (McKelvey). The agenda is decisive, and it is decisive without a lie.
And no fair rule takes the decision back.
Because any rule that turns individual rankings into one group ranking, if it works for every possible set of honest preferences, honours a choice all prefer, and judges each pair on that pair alone, must with three or more options either contradict itself or hand all the power to one voter, there is no method that is at once general, faithful, independent, and shared (Arrow). The impossibility is exact, and it forecloses the clean fix.
And the escapes are bought, not found.
Because the ring can be dissolved by requiring preferences to line up on a single axis, where a median holds and a true winner appears, coherence can be had, but only by forbidding the honest rankings that do not fit the line, or by admitting the intensity and comparison the bare vote leaves out, or by seating a decider, so every route to a settled outcome spends a fairness the vote was raised to keep (Black; Arrow). The remedy exists, and it has a price on it.
None of it is a fix. Each voter ranks honestly because that is what an honest voter does, the majority is taken because that is what a fair vote is, someone sets the order because a decision cannot be made all at once, so each step is only the procedure working as designed, and the arbitrariness of the outcome emerges from the sum, no fraud and no folly required. Escape it, and the escape is dear: it means narrowing what the room is allowed to want, or dropping the plainness of a straight ranked vote, or granting one seat the last word, each a fairness surrendered to buy a coherence the group could not supply. Short of that the room votes, honestly and in full, and the winner it returns is the running order's and not the room's, a decision real and binding and authored by no one's preference, the will of the meeting made by whoever set the meeting's order and not by the meeting at all.

## Triggers

**[The Honest Vote](plot_the_honest_vote.md)**
The room takes up its choice, and each member ranks the options by their true lights and reports them straight. The Voter casts a complete and consistent ranking, no confusion and no strategy in it. Closes when every honest ranking is in and counted, the vote as fair as a vote can be, and nothing yet gone wrong in any part of it.

**[The Loop](plot_the_loop.md)**
Cue: the pairings are compared. A majority prefers A to B, and a majority B to C, and a majority C to A, so the options run in a ring with no head, and in a choice spread over more than one issue no centre holds. Closes when it is plain there is no option the group prefers to all others, the majority sound in each pair and senseless as a whole.

**[The Last Question](plot_the_last_question.md)**
Cue: a winner must still be found. The Convenor sets the order of the pairings, and the sequence selects the survivor, the same votes returning a different winner for every running order. Closes when the outcome is fixed by the order of the asking, the room steered to one option by whoever set the sequence, no vote changed.

**[The Amendment](plot_the_amendment.md)**
Cue: someone would make the rule fair. The Framer seeks a method that is general, faithful, independent, and ruled by no one voter, and finds that with three options in play no rule keeps all four. Closes when the fix is shown to be a trade, coherence bought only by narrowing what may be wanted or seating a decider, no fair rule left that pays nothing.

## Stakes

Whether the outcome of a fair and honest vote is the will of the group it appears, or is in part the work of the running order, a decision real and binding that no coherent group preference stands behind because none exists.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the made majority, which the Voter is right to rank honestly and the Convenor right to order the business, since a decision cannot be taken all at once and someone must set the sequence, and away from the fair rule that would let the group choose for itself, which Arrow forecloses. The production cannot make the trouble a fraud, because no one lies and no one is fooled: the rankings are sincere and consistent, the majority is taken faithfully in every pair, and the ordering is a duty and not a trick, so voting and counting and sequencing are each only the meeting working. It can only show that reasonable people each wanting something clear can compose an assembly that wants nothing clear, so the vote tends to return a winner set by its order and not by its members, and can be steered to any option by the hand that holds the agenda, and that the one move which would fix it, a rule both fair and decisive, cannot exist for three or more options, so the coherence the group lacks can be bought only at a fairness it would rather keep. It is a tendency and not a certainty: the ring need not form, and one straight axis of choice can dissolve it, but where the choice runs over many issues and the preferences genuinely differ, the room's decision is the order's, made by whoever asked last and binding on them all.
