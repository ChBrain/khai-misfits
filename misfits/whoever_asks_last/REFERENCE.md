# Whoever Asks Last: Reference

The research warrant for [Whoever Asks Last](play_whoever_asks_last.md).
The dynamic is the incoherence of preference aggregation: Condorcet's finding that sincere, rational individual rankings
can yield a cyclic majority, Arrow's theorem that no fair rule turns individual rankings into a coherent group one,
McKelvey and Schofield on the agenda control this opens in more than one dimension, and Black on the single-dimension
escape, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the aggregation paradox as a **rational-parts-incoherent-whole structure**: every voter holds a complete,
consistent, sincerely reported ranking, so no individual is irrational and no one lies or is fooled, yet the pairwise
majority relation over three or more options can run in a ring, a majority for A over B and B over C and C over A, so the
group has no option it prefers to all others and no coherent choice of its own. A decision must still be reached, and the
options can be taken only a pair at a time, so the order of the pairings selects the winner, and whoever sets that order
selects it, steering a room of honest voters to any option by the running order alone. The play models the documented
mechanics, the sincere ranking that is each voter's due, the cycle the rankings sum to, the sequence that resolves it
arbitrarily, and the impossibility that forecloses a fair fix, as the load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a
tale of a rigged committee or a duped electorate. The governing finding is the play's governing law: no method of
aggregating individual orderings into a social ordering can, for three or more alternatives, be at once defined for all
preferences, faithful to unanimity, independent across pairs, and free of a dictator, so the coherence the group lacks
cannot be supplied by any fair rule and is supplied instead by the agenda. It is a tendency under stated conditions and
not an iron rule: the ring need not form, and it does not where preferences line up on a single axis, but it is the
general case where the choice spans more than one dimension and the preferences genuinely differ, so the play stages the
arbitrariness a real vote can carry, not a fate of every ballot.

The distinct load-bearing vector is that the whole is **incoherent**, not merely blocked, unwanted, or misperceived, and
the play is pinned to it. It is distinct from Vetocracy, whose surplus of veto points produces a stall: there the
pathology is inaction and the outcome is coherent, nothing passing and the status quo holding, while here the process
reaches a decision and the defect is that no coherent group preference stands behind it, blocked-but-coherent against
proceeds-but-incoherent. It is distinct from The Anticommons, whose fragmented exclusion rights cause a resource to be
underused: that is a property-and-exclusion mechanism with a coherent, measurable deficit, where this is a
preference-aggregation mechanism whose defect is the incoherence of the ordering itself. It is distinct, most sharply,
from The Trip to Abilene, whose members privately agree but each misreads the others and so assent to what none wants:
that failure is epistemic and dissolves under perfect information, the true preferences coinciding, while here the
preferences genuinely differ and the cycle survives full honesty, so Abilene fails because the group cannot see what it
wants and this fails because there is no what-it-wants to see. It is distinct from The Tyranny of Small Decisions, whose
unlinked private choices sum to an unintended but coherent state with a real counterfactual the piecemeal process missed:
here the members vote directly and jointly on the very thing that comes out incoherent, and there is no coherent
collective choice they would have made instead. And it is distinct from The Sort, whose mild preferences tip through a
threshold into a stark but definite segregated equilibrium: that is a dynamic, path-dependent process ending in a
coherent state, where this is a static logical property of a single preference profile, ending in no stable state at all.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast a rigged committee or a
  duped electorate, because the source finding is precisely that no fraud and no folly is needed: every voter ranks
  honestly and consistently and the incoherence is a property of the sum and not of any person (Condorcet), and the one
  who sets the order is not a cheat but a necessity, since a decision cannot be taken all at once and the pairings must
  come in some sequence, so someone must supply the coherence the room lacks (McKelvey). So the misfit is that a group of
  reasonable people, each wanting something clear, can compose an assembly that wants nothing clear, and then the order
  of the asking settles it, no lie and no mistake required. The play stages sincere voting throughout, and stays clear of
  the separate result that voters can gain by misrepresenting their preferences, the Gibbard and Satterthwaite theorem on
  strategic manipulation, which belongs to a story about honesty and not about the aggregation of honest votes.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a group saved by a fair rule. The one move that would let the
  assembly choose for itself is a method both fair and decisive, but Arrow proves that for three or more options no rule
  is at once general, faithful to unanimity, independent across pairs, and ruled by no single voter, so every escape
  gives one of these up: restricting preferences to a single axis buys a winner but forbids the honest rankings that do
  not fit the line (Black), admitting intensity and comparison buys coherence but drops the plainness of a straight
  ranked vote, and seating a decider buys a settled outcome but grants one seat the last word. The remedy is therefore a
  fairness traded for a coherence and never a free fix, the group made to decide but never made to cohere. It is a real
  remedy and not an absent one, since a single well-ordered dimension genuinely dissolves the ring, but it is bought, and
  the play holds the price in view rather than a rule that pays nothing.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never claimed as the
  house's, and its reach is not overstated. The staging is the house's; the cyclic majority of sincere rankings is
  Condorcet's, the impossibility of a fair aggregation rule is Arrow's, the multidimensional agenda control is McKelvey
  and Schofield's, and the single-peaked escape is Black's, each carried with its guardrails: the cycle is a structural
  possibility and not a claim that every vote cycles, common where options multiply and preferences are unstructured and
  rare where they line up or correlate; Arrow's inputs are ordinal rankings with no intensity or interpersonal
  comparison, his conclusion holds only for three or more alternatives, and his dictator is a technical always-decisive
  voter and not a ruler; and McKelvey's chaos requires more than one dimension and the absence of a core, tamed by the
  institutions and the single axes that real assemblies impose. The misfit stages a decision authored by its order and
  not its members, and credits that no one lied and no one erred, holding it a tendency under stated conditions and not a
  law that fixes every vote at the arbitrary.
