# Play: The Best on Paper

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

The Best on Paper.
A decider faces a slate of options and, for each, an honest estimate of its worth. The estimates are unbiased: no thumb on the scale, each as likely to come in high as low, the analyst doing everything right. The decider does the sensible thing and picks the option that scored highest. And here the trap springs, silently, in the arithmetic of the choice itself: to select the top estimate is to select, disproportionately, the estimate that erred upward, because an option is likeliest to sit at the top of the list not only when it is truly best but when its noise happened to flatter it. The winner is the option where true worth and lucky error added up highest, so its estimate is biased above its truth by the very fact that it won. When the choice is realised, the value comes in below the number that chose it, and the decider is surprised, though nothing was rigged and no estimate was wrong on average. The disappointment is a property of the maximum: pick the highest of many noisy readings and you have picked, in part, the largest error. And the shape recurs wherever a chooser ranks options on noisy estimates and takes the top: the vendor with the rosiest projection, the drug with the luckiest trial, the hire who interviewed best, each selected as much for the variance that lifted them as for the merit beneath, so the more options ranked and the noisier the read, the harder the best on paper falls short in fact. Nobody lied. The analyst was unbiased and the decider was rational, and the letdown is stitched into the act of optimising itself, the price of always taking the top of a list the world wrote in pencil.

## Arc

The bend is this: when a decider chooses the option with the highest estimate, and the estimates are noisy but individually unbiased, the choice selects for the largest upward error along with the largest true value, so the chosen option's estimate is biased above its worth and the realised value disappoints, the letdown a property of selecting the maximum of noisy readings (Smith and Winkler).
It runs on a valuation that is honest and a selection that is not neutral.
Each option is valued as fairly as can be, the estimate as likely to fall short of the truth as to overshoot it, so no single valuation is wrong on average and the analyst is beyond reproach (Smith and Winkler). The estimate is unbiased, one option at a time.
And the noise is mean-zero and blameless.
Inside each estimate is an error, the gap between the reading and the truth, symmetric and centred on nothing, as ready to understate as to overstate, so the error is no one's fault and averages away across many options (Galton). The error is innocent, taken on its own.
And the maximum is where the errors are not innocent.
Selecting the highest estimate is selecting across options on their errors as much as their worth, because the option that tops the list is the one whose truth and whose lucky error summed highest, so the act of taking the maximum quietly conditions on upward error and the winner's estimate is flattered above its truth (Smith and Winkler; Capen, Clapp and Campbell). The selection is the bias, injected by choosing.
And the winner is overstated.
The chosen option's estimate stands above its worth not because it was mis-measured but because it was chosen, so a valuation that was unbiased before the pick is biased after it, the overstatement a conditional fact created by selection and invisible in any estimate seen alone (Harrison and March). The overstatement is real and belongs to the winner only.
And the realisation regresses.
When the choice plays out its value returns toward the truth the flattered estimate departed from, coming in below the number that selected it, so the decider meets a postdecision surprise that no error and no dishonesty explains, only the regression of a selected extreme toward its mean (Galton; Harrison and March). The comedown is built in, the disappointment owed to the maximum and not to any mistake.
None of it is a mistake. The analyst valuing each option fairly, the decider taking the highest, the world realising the truth beneath the noise: each does exactly the right thing, and the letdown emerges from the sum, no error and no bias in any single step required, which is exactly why it feels like bad luck and repeats like a law.
Shrink the estimates toward the prior before choosing and the curse is corrected: but to do so is to pull the boldest number back toward the average, to trust the dazzling projection least where it dazzles most, so the one adjustment that de-biases the choice also forgoes the very bet that looked best, and the reckoning is a chooser who discounts their own best estimate and passes on the top of the list, or a chooser who takes it and is, on average, let down.

## Triggers

**[The Fair Read](plot_the_fair_read.md)**
The Analyst values each option as fairly as it can be valued, the estimate as likely to fall short as to overshoot, no bias in any single reading. Closes when every option carries an honest, unbiased estimate and the analyst is beyond reproach.

**[The Top Pick](plot_the_top_pick.md)**
Cue: the options are ranked and the highest is chosen. The Selector takes the top estimate, and in taking the maximum takes the option most flattered by upward error along with the one of highest worth. Closes when the winner is chosen and its estimate stands above its truth by the very fact of being chosen.

**[The Comedown](plot_the_comedown.md)**
Cue: the choice is realised. The chosen option's value returns toward the truth the flattered estimate left, coming in below the number that selected it, a postdecision surprise no error explains. Closes when the realised value disappoints and the letdown is seen to be a property of the maximum, not a mistake.

**[The Haircut](plot_the_haircut.md)**
Cue: a sceptic would de-bias the choice. The Sceptic shrinks the estimates toward the prior before choosing, correcting the curse but pulling the boldest number back and passing, sometimes, on the top of the list. Closes when the estimates are shrunk and the choice de-biased, at the cost of the very bet that looked best.

## Stakes

Whether a decider can trust the top of their own ranked list, or is disappointed by the very act of optimising on estimates that were each, alone, unbiased.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the flattered winner, which the Analyst is right to value fairly and the Selector right to rank and take the highest of, and away from the shrunk estimate that would de-bias the choice at the cost of the boldest bet. The production cannot make the letdown a blunder, because each estimate is unbiased and taking the highest is the rational rule, so valuing each option fairly is the honest act and picking the top the sensible one: the analyst who plays it straight and the decider who optimises are each only doing the correct thing. It can only show that no one erred, that selecting the maximum of noisy but unbiased estimates conditions on upward error and overstates the winner, so the realised value regresses and the choice disappoints, and that the one move that would correct it, shrinking the estimates toward the prior, discounts the analyst's honest read and forgoes the top of the list, so the surprise is owed not to anyone's mistake but to the ordinary arithmetic of always taking the best on paper.
