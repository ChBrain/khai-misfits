# The Arms Race: Reference

The research warrant for [The Arms Race](play_the_arms_race.md).
The term is the strategists' and the economists'; the play runs on the theory of positional goods and
positional competition, from Hirsch's naming through Schelling's strategy of conflict and Frank's positional
externalities, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the positional arms race as a **competitive system**: a prize that is relative rank rather than an
absolute good, so that each contender's rational spending to get ahead is matched by the others' equally
rational spending, the gains cancel to leave the standings unchanged, and the whole field bears a real cost
for a position no one improved. The play models the documented mechanics, the positional prize that is scarce
by its nature and cannot be made more of, the unilateral escalation that truly buys a higher place, the
symmetric matching that nets every gain to zero across the field, the deadweight of a cost paid in full for
no collective benefit, the ratchet by which standing still is losing so no one can stop alone, and the mutual
limit that alone frees the field at the price of binding all against each one's incentive to defect, as the
load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of vanity or aggression. The governing finding of the
source literature is the play's governing law: where the good sought is position, individual effort is a
transfer of rank and not a creation of it, so aggregate effort raises the cost without raising the prize, and
the equilibrium is a race that consumes resources to move no one. It is distinct from the house's Rent-Seeking,
where real resources are burned to capture a transfer that someone does win: here nothing is captured at all,
the escalation nets to zero and the loss is pure. It is distinct from the house's The Commons, where a shared
resource is drawn down by use: here nothing is depleted, the waste is in the competing itself. And it is
distinct from the house's The Winning Bid, where one contest is overpaid once: here the overpayment is
perpetual and mutual, renewed each round by the fear of falling behind.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the contender as
  vain or the rival as aggressive, because the source finding is precisely that each move is right: getting
  ahead is a fair aim, spending to compete is rational, and matching a rival who spends is plain prudence,
  since to stand still while others climb is not to hold a place but to lose it. The waste is authored by no
  error and emerges from the structure, a prize that is a position and a position that is scarce, so the
  misfit is that a field of contenders each doing the sensible thing spends itself higher and poorer in the
  very same order it began.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a costless correction. The one move that frees the
  field is a limit laid on every contender at once, and it must catch all or it catches none, runs against
  each player's live incentive to slip it and pull ahead, and caps a freedom each would rather keep and deny
  the rest, so it is built against every private reason that drove the race. The reckoning is a peace held
  only by binding all hands together, a treaty or a cap or a tax, not a free correction, so the cure for a
  race that no one can win is a limit that no one can safely be first to break.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never
  claimed as the house's. The staging is the house's; positional goods, positional externalities, the
  strategy of conflict, evolutionary arms races, and the theory of status competition are theirs, and the
  condition the trap actually requires, a prize that is relative rank and not an absolute good, is carried as
  carefully as the finding itself.

- **[The Rank](piece_the_rank.md)**: carries the positional prize (Hirsch), relative standing scarce by its
  nature, which effort can only redistribute and never enlarge.
- **[The Escalation](piece_the_escalation.md)**: carries the unilateral spend (Schelling), the move that truly
  buys a higher place when one makes it alone.
- **[The Cancellation](piece_the_cancellation.md)**: carries the netting to zero (Frank), the matched gains
  that leave every contender higher in effort and none in rank.
- **[The Tiptoe](piece_the_tiptoe.md)**: carries the deadweight (Frank), the cost paid in full by all for a
  standing not one of them improved, the field risen and no one seeing better.
- **[The Ratchet](piece_the_ratchet.md)**: carries the compulsion (Dawkins and Krebs), the fact that stopping
  alone is losing, so the race locks on and no one can stand down first.
- **[The Truce](piece_the_truce.md)**: carries the mutual limit (Schelling; Frank), the cap laid on all at once
  that frees the field at the price of binding each against its own incentive.
- **[The Arms Race](piece_the_arms_race.md)**: carries the phenomenon whole (Hirsch; Schelling), a field
  spending real resources to move no one, and its remedy in a limit held against every private drive that made
  it.
