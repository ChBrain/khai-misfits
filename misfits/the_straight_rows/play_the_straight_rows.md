# Play: The Straight Rows

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

The Straight Rows.
A centre cannot govern what it cannot see. To tax a forest, conscript a village, plan a city, or manage a farm, the state must first make the place legible: reduce its tangle to a grid it can read from an office, a map, a table, a standard unit. So the wild forest, unreadable and unaccountable, is remade as scientific forestry made it: one species, even-aged, planted in straight rows, the underbrush cleared, the whole of it a countable stand of timber the ledger can hold. Now the yield can be seen, the harvest planned, the revenue forecast. And for one generation it is glorious: the legible forest delivers, and the method spreads. Then the second rotation comes in sick, and the third fails, a slow death the Germans named Waldsterben. For the straight rows had cleared away everything the ledger never recorded: the mixed species and the undergrowth, the fungal webs and the insects and the birds, the fallen wood and the century of soil the wild forest fed itself on, all the tacit working knowledge of a living system, the métis, that no survey could see and the plantation did not keep. The forest the centre could finally read was a forest that could no longer live. And the move runs everywhere the centre must see to act: the cadastral map that flattens a web of customary tenure into owned squares, the planned city that razes the disorderly street whose life it could not diagram, the imposed surname, the collective farm. None of it is malice. The centre genuinely cannot tax or plan or feed what it cannot see, so it makes the domain legible because that is the only way to govern it at all, and the grid it draws is a real and useful tool. But the grid is thinner than the life it replaces, and the knowledge it cannot record is the knowledge that made the thing work, so the simplification that lets the centre see the domain is the same act that hollows it out, and the map, imposed, remakes the territory into its own thin image and then cannot keep it alive.

## Arc

The bend is this: when a centre can act only on what it can see, it imposes a legible grid on the domain, and the grid clears away the tacit local knowledge, the métis, that the survey cannot record and the working system relied on, so the domain is remade into the thin image the centre can read and loses the function the métis carried, a ruin authored by no malice and reached through a tool that genuinely governs.
It runs on a centre that must see to act.
A state cannot tax, conscript, plan, or manage a domain it cannot read, so it has to reduce the tangle to something legible, a map, a table, a standard unit it can hold from the centre (Scott). The drive to legibility is not vanity but the precondition of acting at all.
And so the grid is imposed.
The domain is remade to be readable: the forest replanted in countable rows, the customary tenure flattened into owned squares, the crooked street straightened into a diagrammable plan, so what could not be seen is made into what can, the map's order pressed down onto the ground (Scott; Lowood). The simplification is real and, at first, it works.
And the métis is cleared away.
The tacit, local, practical knowledge that actually ran the system, the forester's mixed stand, the peasant's rotation, the sidewalk's unplanned order, is not recorded by the grid because it cannot be, being unwritten and particular and known only in the doing, so imposing the grid displaces it, bans it, or lets it die (Detienne and Vernant; Hayek; Jacobs). The knowledge the survey could not see is the knowledge the survey clears.
And the yield comes in.
For a season the legible system delivers, the first rotation heavy, the plan on schedule, the revenue counted, so the method is vindicated and doubled down on, the early success the very reason the grid is trusted and spread (Lowood). The centre reads its own confirmation in a harvest the métis it cleared had made possible.
And the ground gives out.
Run now on only what the grid can see, the system loses the function the métis carried and fails, the second rotation sick and the third dead, the planned city unlivable, the collective farm barren, a slow collapse of the very domain the centre made legible to save (Scott; Lowood). The map remade the territory into something that matches the map and can no longer live.
None of it is malice. The centre making the domain legible so it can govern, the plan imposed by people who mean to improve the place: each is doing the reasonable thing, and the ruin emerges from the sum, no tyrant needed for a destruction that a tool of real use carries out, which is exactly why it recurs wherever a centre must see to act.
Hold the legibility below the point where it displaces the métis it cannot capture, and the domain might keep: but a centre that must see mistrusts what it cannot read, and the confidence that its grid is the whole of the knowledge is the confidence that clears the rest, so the reckoning is a domain saved only by a restraint the drive to govern resists, not an order that keeps the life beneath it once the map is pressed down.

## Stakes

Whether a centre can govern a place without hollowing it, or must always trade the knowledge that made it work for the legibility that lets it be run.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the legible order, which the centre is right to impose because it cannot act on what it cannot see, and away from the living domain, which asks the centre to trust a knowledge it cannot read or record. The production cannot make the centre govern by métis, because métis is unwritten and particular and the centre must act from a distance on what it can hold in a table, so imposing the grid is the sound move and standardising the domain the sound method: the planner making a place legible and the state reading its revenue from a map are each only doing the reasonable thing. It can only show that no one is a tyrant, that a centre that must see, met by a domain whose working knowledge it cannot see, remakes the place into a thin image it can read and cannot keep alive, and that the one move that would spare it, holding the grid below the point where it clears the métis, is met by the same drive to govern that imposed it, so the restraint pays no ruler and the domain is hollowed not by anyone's malice but by the reasonable reach of a centre that can act only on what it can see.
