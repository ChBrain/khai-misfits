# Only the Close Ones: Reference

The research warrant for [Only the Close Ones](play_only_the_close_ones.md).
The dynamic is the Priest-Klein selection hypothesis: their finding that settlement selects the tried cases and drives the
win rate toward one half, Landes's settle-versus-litigate model beneath it, Gross and Syverud's study of which cases go
to trial, Kessler, Meites and Miller's deviations from the fifty-percent rule, and Heckman's correction for sample
selection, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the Priest-Klein selection hypothesis as a **settlement-selects-the-sample structure**: trying a case is costly
and settling is not, so two sides who both foresee an outcome settle on terms that reflect it, and a case leaves the
record exactly when its result is predictable, leaving only the disputes on which the two cannot agree to be tried. The
play models the documented mechanics, the settlement filter that removes the predictable cases, the near-even rate a
pool of toss-ups produces, the honest reader misled by taking the tried sample for the disputes, and the correction that
must model the settled cases it cannot see, as the load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of a dishonest
counter or a manipulated record. The governing finding of the source is the play's governing law: because cases are
selected for litigation by the failure of settlement, and settlement fails on the genuine uncertainties, the plaintiff
win rate at trial tends toward fifty percent regardless of the underlying legal standard, so inference about the merits
or the law from decided cases is misled by a selection on the very outcome being studied. It is a tendency and not an
iron rule, holding under the model's assumptions and deviating where parties misjudge, stakes are asymmetric, or
information differs, so the play stages the pull toward one half, not a law that fixes there.

It is distinct from the house's The Survivors, where a sample is biased by passive attrition, units failing or
disappearing so that only the survivors remain to be seen: there the selection is one-sided and passive, the dropouts
lost by their own failure, and the residue is the successful. Here the cases leave the sample by settlement, an active
and joint decision by two adversaries who must both agree to close a case, and the filter selects on shared
predictability, so the residue is not the winners but the toss-ups, and the tried rate is pulled toward even rather than
toward success. The engine is a strategic mutual selection on the outcome, not an attrition of the weak, and the
correction is a reconstruction of what settled rather than a count of what was lost.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the parties as manipulators
  or the reader as careless, because the source finding is precisely that no bad faith and no error of arithmetic is
  needed: each side settles the cases it can call because settling is cheaper than a pointless verdict, and tries the
  ones it cannot because no settlement both will take exists (Landes; Priest and Klein). The reader counts an honest,
  clean, complete record of trials, and is misled only because that record was selected on the outcome before it
  arrived. So the misfit is that a sample chosen by the predictability it removes clusters near even whatever the merits,
  and honest inference from it goes wrong, no fraud and no miscount required, the settling and the counting each sound
  and the misleading measure the sum, the litigant who settles what they can foresee as reasonable as the reader who
  counts what is left.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a record read rightly by care alone. The one move that repairs
  the inference is to correct for the selection, modelling the settled cases that never reached trial and estimating the
  disputes from the population rather than the residue (Heckman), but that means resting the answer on the cases the
  record omits, which are private, their terms unrecorded and their number unknown, so the correction can never be as
  firm as the plain count it replaces. The true reading arrives as a range where the tally offered a number, less
  persuasive for being honest about the unobserved, so the reckoning is a measure that clusters at even and means
  nothing, or an inference that must model the cases it will never see, not a way to read the trial record straight and
  be right.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never claimed as the
  house's, and its reach is not overstated. The staging is the house's; the selection hypothesis and the fifty-percent
  tendency are Priest and Klein's, the settle-versus-litigate model beneath it is Landes's, the empirical study of
  selection for trial is Gross and Syverud's, the deviations that make the fifty percent a tendency rather than a law
  are Kessler, Meites and Miller's, and the general correction for sample selection is Heckman's, each carried as
  carefully as the finding itself: the misfit stages a record selected on its own outcome and credits that no one
  cheated and the fifty-fifty is the filter's.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per element.

  sample selected by settlement, near even by the filter and misleading to read straight.
- **[The Coin Flip](piece_the_coin_flip.md)**: carries the fifty-percent tendency (Priest and Klein), qualified as a pull
  and not a fixed rule (Kessler, Meites and Miller), the near-even rate a pool of toss-ups yields whatever the merits.
- **[The Sieve](piece_the_sieve.md)**: carries the settlement filter (Landes; Priest and Klein), the economy of settling
  the predictable that selects the trial pool on the outcome itself.
- **[The Unlitigated](piece_the_unlitigated.md)**: carries the missing settled cases and the need to model them (Heckman),
  the informative absence the correction must reconstruct because the record never held it.
