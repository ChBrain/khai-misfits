# Play: Only the Close Ones

## Estate

## Name

Only the Close Ones.
To learn how the law leans, or how a kind of claim tends to fare, you read the record of decided cases and count who won. That is only sense: outcomes are evidence, and a pile of them should show the pattern. But the cases that reach a verdict are not a sample of the disputes; they are what is left after settlement has taken its pick. Two sides who both can see how a case will come out do not spend the cost of trying it: they settle, on terms that reflect the outcome they both foresee, and the case leaves the record before it is ever counted. What cannot be foreseen, the genuine toss-up where the two sides read the odds differently, is what goes to trial, because that is the only kind of case on which they cannot agree to settle. So the trial record fills with the close calls and empties of the clear ones, and a docket of toss-ups is a docket that comes out near even, whatever the underlying merits, however lopsided the law. The reader counts the verdicts, finds the win rate hovering at half, and concludes that claims of this kind are a coin flip, or that the law has no lean, when all they have measured is the residue of what would not settle. Nobody cooked the books. Each side settled the cases it could call because settling was cheaper than trying, and tried the ones it could not because there was no settlement both would take, and the reader read the only record there was. The clear cases settle, the close ones are tried, and the record of trials, selected by the very predictability it hides, tells the honest reader a fifty-fifty story about a world that was never fifty-fifty.

## Arc

The bend is this: the cases that go to trial are not a random draw but what settlement leaves behind, so the tried win rate is driven toward one half regardless of the underlying merits, and inference from decided cases misleads (Priest and Klein).
It runs on settlement that selects.
Because trying a case is costly and settling is not, two sides who both foresee the outcome settle on terms that reflect it, so a case leaves the sample exactly when its result is predictable, the settlement priced by the outcome both expect (Landes). The settling is sound, and it removes the cases whose outcome is known.
And what will not settle is the toss-up.
Because a case settles only when both sides can agree on where it lands, the ones that reach trial are the ones on which they cannot agree, the genuine uncertainties where each side rates its own odds high enough to fight, so the tried pool is filled with close calls and emptied of clear ones (Priest and Klein; Gross and Syverud). The selection is joint, made by two adversaries who must both refuse to settle, and it keeps only the uncertain.
And a pool of toss-ups comes out near even.
Because the tried cases are the ones whose outcome was a real question, their verdicts split close to half whatever the merits of the wider dispute, so the trial win rate is pulled toward fifty percent by the selection and not by the law, a docket of coin flips landing, as coin flips do, near even (Priest and Klein). The clustering is the sample's, not the world's.
And the honest reader is misled.
Because the record shows a near-even split, the reader who counts it concludes the claims are a wash or the law has no tilt, taking a property of the selected sample for a property of the disputes, so the very predictability that settled the clear cases out is the thing the trial record cannot show (Priest and Klein). The inference is sound on the data and wrong about the world, because the data is the residue of a filter.
And the filter is invisible to the count.
Because the settled cases leave no verdict to tally, the reader sees only what was tried and nothing of what was resolved, so the selection does its work off the page, and no amount of careful counting of the record recovers the disputes the record never held (Kessler, Meites and Miller). The missing cases are missing precisely where the inference needs them.
None of it is a fix. Each side settling the calls it can make, trying the ones it cannot, and the reader counting the only record there is: each acts soundly, and the misleading measure emerges from the sum, no fraud and no error of arithmetic required. Correct for the selection, reason about the settled cases that never appear, and the inference can be repaired (the selection model, the accounting for what did not reach trial): but that means estimating a population you cannot see, since settlements are private and their terms unrecorded, and rebuilding the distribution the record filtered out from the little that leaks, so the one move that reads the trial record rightly is a reconstruction of everything it left off, and the reckoning is a measure that clusters at even and means nothing, or an inference that must model the cases it will never observe (Heckman). The fifty-fifty is real in the record and false about the world, and only a correction for the filter that made it can tell the two apart.

## Triggers

**[The Clear Case](plot_the_clear_case.md)**
A dispute both sides can call, its outcome plain to each. The Litigant settles it on terms that reflect the result they both foresee, since trying it would only spend the cost of confirming what they know. Closes when the clear case leaves the record by settlement, taken out of the sample exactly because its outcome was certain.

**[The Trial](plot_the_trial.md)**
Cue: a dispute neither side can call. The two read the odds differently, each rating its own chances high enough to fight, so no settlement both will take exists, and the case goes to trial. Closes when the toss-up is tried and its verdict lands near even, the record keeping only the cases whose result was a real question.

**[The Misread](plot_the_misread.md)**
Cue: the reader counts the verdicts. The Tallyman finds the win rate hovering at half and concludes the claims are a wash or the law has no tilt, taking the selected sample for the world. Closes when a property of the filtered record is read as a property of the disputes, the honest count arriving at a false picture.

**[The Adjustment](plot_the_adjustment.md)**
Cue: someone would read the record rightly. The Realist accounts for the settled cases that never reached trial, modelling the population the record filtered out, and pays in estimating what cannot be seen. Closes when the inference is corrected for the selection at the cost of reconstructing an unobserved world, or left uncorrected and the fifty-fifty believed.

## Stakes

Whether a body of outcomes can be read for what it seems to show, or is a sample selected before it is counted, near even by the filter that made it and silent about the world it came from.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the misleading measure, which the Litigant is right to settle the cases it can call and try the ones it cannot, and the Tallyman right to count the only record there is, and away from the correction that would read the record rightly at the cost of modelling what it never held. The production cannot make the fifty-fifty a fraud, because each settlement is sound and each trial is of a genuine uncertainty and the reader counts honestly, so settling the sure cases and trying the close ones and tallying the verdicts are each only sensible acts. It can only show that a record selected by the predictability it removes clusters near even whatever the merits, that the honest reader who takes the tried sample for the disputes is misled by a filter that left no trace on the page, and that the one move which reads it rightly, correcting for the cases that settled away, means estimating a population it cannot observe, so the measure comes out even and means nothing not by anyone's dishonesty but by the ordinary good sense of everyone who settles what they can foresee and counts what is left.
