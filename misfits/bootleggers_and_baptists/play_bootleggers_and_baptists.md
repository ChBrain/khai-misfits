# Play: Bootleggers and Baptists

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

Bootleggers and Baptists.
In a county where drink is legal six days a week, two very different people want the liquor store shut on Sunday. The Baptist wants it shut because drinking on the Sabbath is a sin: they mean it, they preach it, they vote it, and they ask nothing for themselves. The bootlegger wants it shut because every Sunday the legal store is closed, the illegal trade is the only game in town: they profit, quietly, and they will fund whatever keeps the law on the books. The two never meet. They may hold each other in contempt. But they push the same rule from opposite sides, and between the sermon and the money the Sunday ban becomes untouchable, because a legislator who moved to repeal it would face the moralists' outrage and the profiteers' war chest at once. The principle gives the law its public face and its legitimacy; the profit gives it its muscle and its staying power. No honest weighing of costs and benefits would keep a rule whose main effect is to feed a black market, but this rule is politically immovable, because to attack it is to insult sincere believers and threaten quiet earnings in the same breath. And the shape tends to recur wherever a regulation has a moral champion and a private beneficiary: the taxi medallion defended for safety and kept for the incumbents, the sugar tariff sold as jobs and banked by the growers, the licence demanded for the customer's protection and prized by the licensed. Nobody conspired. The Baptist is sincere and the bootlegger is rational, and the rule endures on the strength of a coalition neither of them had to form, the profit riding hidden inside the principle and the principle never knowing it carried a passenger.

## Arc

The bend is this: when a rule is wanted by a sincere moral constituency for its principle and by a quiet interest for its profit, the two back it from opposite sides without coordinating, so the principle lends the profit a public legitimacy and the profit lends the principle a resourced defence, and a rule no honest cost-benefit would keep becomes politically immovable, a durable bad law authored by no conspiracy and held up by a coalition no one convened.
It runs on a rule that serves two masters.
A regulation is on the table that answers a moral concern and, in the same stroke, hands a private interest a protected gain, the Sunday ban that honours the Sabbath and shelters the bootlegger, so one rule has two constituencies who want it for reasons that never touch (Yandle). The rule is the single thing the two hold in common.
And the Baptist supplies the principle.
The moral constituency argues the rule in good faith, publicly, asking nothing for itself, and its sincerity is real and is the rule's legitimacy, the reason a legislator can support the law without embarrassment and a citizen can back it without suspicion (Yandle). The principle is the public face, and it is honestly held.
And the bootlegger banks the profit.
The private interest gains quietly from the rule and spends to keep it, the concentrated beneficiary who organises where the diffuse public will not, funding the defence of a law it never has to praise in its own name (Stigler; Olson). The profit is the muscle, and it never shows its face.
And the two align without meeting.
Neither coalition need coordinate with or even acknowledge the other, because each is served by the same rule for its own reason, so the principle and the profit converge on one law as if by arrangement though none was made, the moral case unknowingly carrying the private interest that shelters behind it (Yandle and Smith; Becker). The coalition is real and was never formed.
And the rule cannot be moved.
A legislator who would repeal it must face the moralists' outrage and the profiteers' resources at once, principle and money defending the same law from opposite flanks, so the rule outlasts every honest accounting of its costs, immovable not because it is good but because attacking it is doubly expensive (Becker; Yandle and Smith). The bad law endures on a coalition that pays in both conviction and cash.
None of it is a plot. The Baptist arguing a sincere principle, the bootlegger backing a rule that helps them, the legislator keeping a law with two constituencies: each is doing the ordinary thing, and the durable bad rule emerges from the sum, no conspiracy and no cynic required, which is exactly why it is so hard to name and harder to repeal.
Name the profit behind the principle and the coalition might break: but to do so is to tell sincere believers their cause is a front and the profiteers their game is up, so the naming offends both at once and is resisted by both, and the reckoning is a rule undone only by an accusation that insults the honest along with the interested, not a coalition anyone can dissolve by appealing to either half alone.

## Stakes

Whether a public can repeal a rule that serves a hidden interest, or is held to it by the very sincerity of the people who back it for better reasons.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the durable rule, which the Baptist is right to argue on principle and the bootlegger right to defend for profit and the legislator right to keep for its two constituencies, and away from the honest accounting, which would repeal a law whose costs exceed its benefits. The production cannot make the coalition break by reason, because the principle is sincere and cannot be argued away and the profit is quiet and need never answer, so preaching the ban is the honest act and banking it the rational one: the moralist serving their conscience and the profiteer their interest are each only doing the ordinary thing. It can only show that no one conspired, that a sincere principle and a private profit, converging on one rule without ever meeting, make a bad law immovable, and that the one move that would break it, naming the profit behind the principle, insults the honest believer as much as it exposes the interested, so the rule holds not by anyone's cynicism but by the ordinary conviction and ordinary self-interest of the two who keep it from opposite sides.
