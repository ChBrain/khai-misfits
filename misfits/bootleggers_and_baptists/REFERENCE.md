# Bootleggers and Baptists: Reference

The research warrant for [Bootleggers and Baptists](play_bootleggers_and_baptists.md).
The dynamic is the bootleggers-and-Baptists coalition: Yandle's naming of it, the book-length taxonomy of its forms,
the public-choice theory of regulation it sits on, the logic of collective action that explains its asymmetry, and
the pressure-group competition that makes it durable, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the bootleggers-and-Baptists coalition as a **principle-shelters-profit structure**: a rule answers a
sincere moral concern and, in the same stroke, hands a private interest a protected gain, so the rule has two
constituencies, a moral one that argues it in good faith and asks nothing, and a profiting one that gains quietly and
spends to keep it, and because each is served by the same rule for its own reason the two back it from opposite sides
without ever coordinating or acknowledging each other, the principle lending the profit a public legitimacy and the
profit lending the principle a resourced defence. The play models the documented mechanics, the rule that serves two
masters, the sincere principle that is its public face, the quiet profit that is its muscle, the tacit alignment that
needs no meeting, and the durability that makes a rule no honest cost-benefit would keep politically immovable, as the
load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of a hypocrite or a conspiracy. The governing finding of the
source is the play's governing law: where a rule is wanted on principle by one constituency and for profit by
another, the moral case shelters the private interest that hides behind it, and the coalition, formed by no one,
holds the bad rule in place because attacking it offends sincere believers and threatens quiet money at once.

It is distinct from the house's The Iron Triangle, where a committee, an agency, and an interest group lock into a
closed triad of mutual exchange, each vertex knowingly supplying what the other two need, a coordinated self-dealing
ring stable by reciprocity: here the alliance is two-sided, not three, and above all it is tacit, the moralist and
the profiteer needing neither to coordinate nor even to acknowledge one another, the coalition an emergent overlap of
a principle and a profit on one rule rather than a ring of traded favours. It is distinct too from Regulatory
Capture, where a single agency is bent from within by the industry it was meant to police until it serves that
industry: here nothing is captured, the rule is backed from outside by a coalition, and its moral half is genuinely
disinterested, the profiteer free-riding on a sincere moral movement's political force rather than turning a watchdog
to its own ends, so where capture is an interest colonising a regulator, this is an interest sheltering behind a
conviction it never has to share.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the Baptist as a
  hypocrite or the bootlegger as a schemer, because the source finding is precisely that no hypocrisy and no scheme is
  needed: the moral constituency argues its principle in good faith and asks nothing for itself, the private interest
  backs a rule that helps it exactly as any interest backs its own advantage, and the two need never meet, so neither
  deceives the other and no one manufactures the principle to launder the profit. The durable rule is authored by no
  conspiracy and emerges from a sincere conviction and a rational interest converging on the same law, so the misfit
  is that an honest principle and a quiet profit, wanting one rule for reasons that never touch, make a bad law
  immovable, no cynic and no plot required. The bootlegger's gain is a fact of the rule, not a trick worked on the
  Baptist, who argues the same case whether or not anyone profits from it.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a repeal carried by argument. The one move that would break
  the coalition is to name the profit hiding behind the principle, to show the public that the moral case shelters a
  private interest, but that naming tells sincere believers their cause is a front and the profiteers their cover is
  blown, so it offends the honest as much as it exposes the interested and is resisted by both at once. The reckoning
  is a rule undone only by an accusation that insults conviction along with self-interest, not a coalition that
  dissolves when either half is answered on its own terms, so the cure for a principle-sheltered profit is a charge
  that lands on the guiltless beside the guilty.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never claimed as the
  house's, and its reach is not overstated. The staging is the house's; the bootleggers-and-Baptists coalition and its
  name are Yandle's, the taxonomy of its covert and coordinated forms is Yandle and Smith's, the public-choice theory
  of regulation-for-interest is Stigler's, the logic of collective action that explains the coalition's asymmetry is
  Olson's, and the pressure-group equilibrium that makes it durable is Becker's, each carried as carefully as the
  finding itself: the misfit stages the principle that shelters a profit and credits that the moral half is sincere
  and the coalition one no party had to convene.
