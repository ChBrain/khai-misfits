# Place: The Statute

## Taxonomy

## Owner

## Shown

The law itself, the chamber, the vote not taken, the rule that stays year after year because no one moves to touch it.
The Legislator reading the room, principle pressing from one side and money from the other, a diffuse public feeling the cost too thinly to press at all, and the statute standing.

## Holds

The rule kept, and the balance that keeps it.
Here the two halves of the coalition converge on one law, so the Statute holds the durability of the trap: a legislator who would repeal faces the moralists' outrage and the profiteers' resources at once, so the rule outlasts every honest accounting of its costs, kept not because it is good but because attacking it is doubly expensive.
The Statute is where a bad law becomes permanent, held in place by a coalition no one convened and defended from both flanks.

## Offers

A rule with two constituencies, and a legislator every reason to keep it.
Because the Statute is defended by principle and money and opposed only diffusely, it offers the legislator a rule that is politically cheap to keep and costly to touch, handing out the sound electoral read, and in the same motion it offers the public a permanent law whose costs exceed its benefits, the immovability and the harm delivered together.

## Withheld

Repeal, and any accounting that would earn it.
The Statute can keep the rule year on year, but it withholds the repeal that its actual cost-benefit would warrant, since the balance of pressure overrides the accounting, and it withholds any moment at which the honest sum is even taken, so it hands over a law that endures on its coalition and never on its merits, immovable and unexamined at once.
