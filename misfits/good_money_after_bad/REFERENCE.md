# Good Money After Bad: Reference

The research warrant for [Good Money After Bad](play_good_money_after_bad.md). The name
is the old idiom for throwing sound resources after unsound ones; the mechanics the play
runs on are grounded in the escalation-of-commitment and sunk-cost literature cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the sunk-cost trap as a **systemic system**: an organisation in which every gate
decides defensibly and the failing project is funded anyway, because each decision weighs
the investment already made rather than the value still to gain. The play models the
documented mechanics, the sunk-cost effect, escalation of commitment under personal
responsibility, loss aversion in the domain of losses, self-justification, and the
institutional gate that cannot say stop, as the load-bearing vectors of the structure,
not as a tale of fools or crooks. The governing finding of the source literature is the
play's governing law: the escalation is driven by looking backward at what has been
spent, not forward at marginal value, so it is one-directional, because sunk cost only
accumulates, and a rule that funds in proportion to prior loss funds a failure in
proportion to how badly it has failed.

## Origin

The phenomenon in practice, and the research spine beneath it.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to
  cast any seat as reckless or corrupt, because the source finding is precisely that each
  continuation is defensible: protect the investment, honour the commitment, avoid the sure
  loss. The misfit is that a chain of rational defences is an irrational whole.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into clear-eyed courage arriving in
  time. The kill comes from a seat that staked nothing, an incoming regime or a dry account,
  doing the plain arithmetic the invested seats could not bear, not from insight winning the
  room.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed
  here, never claimed as the house's. The staging is the house's; the findings are theirs.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per element.

- **[The Sunk Cost](piece_the_sunk_cost.md)**: carries the sunk-cost effect (Arkes & Blumer),
  the accumulated spend that is irrelevant to the future and decisive to the room.
- **[The Backward Rule](piece_the_backward_rule.md)**: carries the sunk-cost fallacy itself,
  deciding on what is spent rather than on marginal cost against marginal value.
- **[The Receding Finish](piece_the_receding_finish.md)**: carries entrapment (Teger), the payoff
  kept perpetually one phase away.
- **[The Good Money](piece_the_good_money.md)**: carries escalation of commitment (Staw), the
  sound increment recruited to defend the unsound one before it.
- **[The Reputation Stake](piece_the_reputation_stake.md)**: carries self-justification (Festinger)
  and personal responsibility for the prior choice (Staw), the champion who cannot afford to see.
- **[The Green Light](piece_the_green_light.md)**: carries institutional escalation (Ross & Staw),
  the gate that is built to stop it and can only ever proceed.
- **[The Ratchet](piece_the_ratchet.md)**: carries loss aversion (Kahneman & Tversky), the geared
  commitment that climbs and never falls, gambling to avoid the sure loss.
- **[The Zombie](piece_the_zombie.md)**: carries the phenomenon whole, the project dead on merit and
  alive on history, held whole by nobody.
