# Moral Hazard: Reference

The research warrant for [Moral Hazard](play_moral_hazard.md). The title is the insurers'
term, worn into economics; the play runs on the economics of hidden action and the
principal-agent literature, from Arrow's and Pauly's account of insurance through Holmström's
observability model to the systemic case, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging moral hazard as a **systemic system**: covering a risk lowers the care taken against it,
without a single fraud, because the party who acts no longer bears the loss and the party who
bears the loss cannot observe the act. The play models the documented mechanics, the risk written
over to another, the action hidden from the one who now carries the downside, the private price of
care rising above its private reward, the precaution rationally eased, the loss falling on the pool
that insured it, and the deductible or co-insurance that alone restores the incentive at a cost, as
the load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of recklessness. The governing finding of
the source literature is the play's governing law: when the cost of a bad outcome is borne by one
party and the care that prevents it is chosen by another whose care cannot be seen, the privately
rational level of care falls below the socially efficient one, so protection buys risk. This is the
hidden-action twin of its neighbour: adverse selection turns on a quality hidden before the deal, and
moral hazard on an action hidden after it.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the
  insured as reckless or the insurer as a dupe, because the source finding is precisely that neither
  is needed: easing the care once the loss has moved is a rational economy (Pauly), and the insurer's
  blindness to the care is structural, not negligent (Holmström). The misfit is that the same cover
  which carries the risk removes the reason to prevent it.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into prudence returning of its own accord. A
  deductible or a co-share can restore the care, but only by handing part of the loss back to the
  insured, so the reckoning is protection traded for prevention, a hole cut in the cover on purpose,
  not a policy that came good while still covering everything.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here,
  never claimed as the house's. The staging is the house's; the welfare account, the observability
  model, and the optimal-contract remedy are theirs, and the condition moral hazard actually requires,
  a hidden action and a downside borne by another, is carried as carefully as the finding itself.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per element.

- **[The Cover](piece_the_cover.md)**: carries the risk transfer (Arrow), the downside written over to a
  party who will bear the loss the insured no longer fears.
- **[The Hidden Hand](piece_the_hidden_hand.md)**: carries unobservable action (Holmström), the care the
  insurer cannot see and therefore cannot price or require.
- **[The Free Downside](piece_the_free_downside.md)**: carries the rational response to price (Pauly), the
  loss made someone else's so that skipping precaution costs the actor nothing they still hold.
- **[The Slipped Care](piece_the_slipped_care.md)**: carries the reduced precaution (Arrow; Shavell), care
  eased to the level that is optimal once its reward belongs to the insurer.
- **[The Pooled Loss](piece_the_pooled_loss.md)**: carries the socialised outcome, the bad event paid from the
  pool or the public purse and spread across the careful, not billed to the hand that eased.
- **[The Skin](piece_the_skin.md)**: carries the incentive remedy (Shavell; Holmström), the deductible or share
  that restores care by leaving part of the loss on the insured, at the cost of the protection given up.
- **[The Moral Hazard](piece_the_moral_hazard.md)**: carries the phenomenon whole (Arrow; Pauly), and its
  systemic form in the guaranteed downside (too-big-to-fail), protection that buys the risk it covers,
  held whole by nobody.
