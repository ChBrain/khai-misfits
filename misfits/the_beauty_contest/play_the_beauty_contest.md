# Play: The Beauty Contest

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

The Beauty Contest.
Keynes set it in a newspaper competition: a hundred photographs, and the reader wins who picks the six faces the most readers pick. Think it through. You do not win by choosing the faces you find prettiest. You do not even win by choosing the faces you think most readers will genuinely find prettiest. You win by choosing the faces you think most readers think most readers will pick, and the sharpest reader knows the others are reasoning the same way, so it climbs: what does average opinion expect average opinion to be, and then a degree above that, and a degree above that. The face's own beauty has dropped out of the problem entirely. What is being priced is not the face but the room's guess about the room. Now put a market where the newspaper was. The share, the house, the coin, the token: its worth is what it will earn, what it can be used for, what it is. But the trader is not paid to be right about that. The trader is paid to buy today what the room will bid up tomorrow, and the room is every other trader, each also guessing what the room will do. So each stops weighing the thing and starts weighing the others' weighing of the thing, and the price becomes a tower of guesses about guesses, rising because everyone expects everyone to keep buying and falling when that expectation turns, unmoored from any worth beneath it. And nothing in it is folly. The one who values the thing correctly and buys it and waits is not wrong about the thing: they are wrong about the game, whose prize was never accuracy but agreement, and while they wait to be proved right the price runs the other way and the money to hold the position runs out. Each player anticipating the others is doing exactly what the payoff rewards, and the sum of sound players each guessing the crowd is a price set by opinion about opinion, a market that has stopped pricing the thing and started pricing itself.

## Arc

The bend is this: when the payoff turns on matching the crowd rather than on the thing itself, each rational player forecasts the others' forecasts in an unending regress, so price comes to track opinion about opinion and floats free of any value beneath it, held only by an anchor that can be carried off before it is proved right, and no folly is needed for a market of sound forecasters to stop pricing the thing and start pricing itself.
It runs on a prize for matching, not for being right.
The reward goes to whoever best anticipates where the crowd will go, not to whoever best judges what the thing is worth (Keynes). So being right about the fundamental earns nothing if the crowd does not agree, and guessing the crowd earns everything even when the crowd is wrong: the payoff is the match.
And so the fundamental drops out.
Because valuing the thing correctly against the crowd loses, the rational player sets the thing's worth aside and turns to the only question the payoff rewards, what the others will do, so the anchor that would tie price to value is let go by the very players best able to hold it (Keynes; Shleifer and Vishny).
And the guessing climbs a tower.
Each player, knowing the others are also guessing the crowd, must guess the crowd's guess, and then the crowd's guess about the crowd's guess, a regress from the first degree to the second to the third that every rational player must climb because everyone else is climbing it too (Nagel; Allen, Morris and Shin). Price becomes a weighted average of expectations of expectations, and sits systematically further from the fundamental than any single belief.
And the tower swells into a bubble.
The self-referential price rises because everyone expects everyone to keep buying, and the rise confirms the expectation and draws the next buyer, a feedback loop that inflates the price so long as the expectation holds and collapses when it turns (Shiller). What reads as a market discovering value is a market pricing its own momentum.
None of it is mania or fraud. The trader anticipating the room, the fund tracking what will be bid up, the analyst reading sentiment: each is playing the payoff exactly as it is set, and the detachment emerges from the sum, no one needing to be foolish or false for a price to float free of worth.
Anchor on the fundamental and trade against the crowd, and the game could be disciplined: but arbitrage against a self-referential price is risky and capital-constrained, so the anchor can be carried out on the tide before the price corrects, margin called and conviction spent while the mispricing runs on, so the one move that would tie price back to value is a bet that can go broke being right too early, and the market prices opinion about opinion for as long as the game runs.

## Stakes

Whether a market can price a thing by its worth, or is always dragged to pricing the crowd's opinion of the crowd.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the self-referential price, which each player is right to chase because the payoff rewards matching and not truth, and away from the anchored price, which asks a valuer to bet on worth against the crowd and to outlast a mispricing that runs against them. The production cannot make the price track value, because the reward is the match and not the fact, so guessing the crowd is the sound play and holding to the fundamental the losing one: the trader anticipating the room, the fund tracking what will be bid up, each is only doing what the payoff asks. It can only show that no one is foolish, that a market of sound forecasters each guessing the others prices a thing by opinion about opinion, and that the one move that would tie price back to worth, anchoring on the fundamental and trading against the crowd, must be made against a self-referential price and on capital that can run out before the correction comes, so the players are rational and the prize is agreement and the market that was meant to price the thing prices its own guess about itself.
