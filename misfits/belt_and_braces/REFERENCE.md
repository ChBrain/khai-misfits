# Belt and Braces: Reference

The research warrant for [Belt and Braces](play_belt_and_braces.md).
The dynamic is the redundancy paradox: Scott Sagan's problem of redundancy, Charles Perrow's interactive
complexity and common-mode failure, and Latane and Darley's diffusion of responsibility, all cited below,
by which adding a redundant safety component to raise reliability can, in a coupled system, lower it.

## Line of Work

Staging the redundancy paradox as a **backup-that-fails-the-system structure**: a component added in good faith to
remove a single point raises the computed reliability, and in a coupled socio-technical system the same addition
adds interactive complexity, shares a common cause with what it backs, and diffuses the vigilance it was meant to
add, so past a point each further backup lowers the true reliability it was added to raise while the figure that
counts it still climbs. The play models the documented mechanics, the locally rational addition that reads as more
defence in depth, the interactive complexity each part opens along paths no designer drew, the common-mode cause
that fells the supposedly independent backups at a stroke, the diffusion of responsibility and risk compensation by
which the backup relaxes the watch and invites the risk it was to guard, the deceptive reliability figure that
rises with every parallel unit as though the parts failed independently, and the costly independence audit that
alone surfaces the coupling in time, as the load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of bad engineering
or reckless operators. The governing finding of the sources is the play's governing law: redundancy raises
reliability only for parts that fail independently, and in a tightly coupled system the added backup is not that
independent part, so more redundant safety can produce less safety, the addition sincerely believed to defend the
system it has begun to imperil.

The honest boundary is carried as carefully as the finding: redundancy genuinely does raise reliability when the
backups are truly independent and the additions add no coupling, no unforeseen interaction, and no behavioural
compensation. Classic reliability theory holds for idealised independent parallel components, where two chances
really are better than one. The paradox bites only where the added redundancy introduces common-mode coupling,
interactive complexity, or risk compensation, that is, in tightly coupled socio-technical systems and not in
idealised independent arrays, and the pattern runs regardless of which engineers or operators are inside it,
because it is a property of redundancy in a coupled system and not of anyone's competence.

It is distinct from the house's Moral Hazard, and the two are close enough that the cut must be exact: there a
shield transfers a loss to a third party who cannot observe one insured actor's care, so the protected actor takes
less care because someone else bears the cost, a transfer of loss and a single hidden actor; here the shield is a
technical or organisational backup whose very presence breeds common-mode failure, adds complexity, and diffuses
responsibility across many, with no loss transferred to anyone and no single insured party, the specific engine
being redundancy-as-cause, the added guard the thing that fails the system rather than an incentive that dulls one
actor's diligence.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the designer as a bad
  engineer or the operators as reckless, because the source finding is precisely that no one errs: the backup is
  added in good faith to remove a real single point, the arithmetic of parallel redundancy is done correctly, the
  defence in depth is real defence, and the operators respond sensibly to a reserve that is really there. The turn
  is authored by no one and emerges from a backup wired and cooled and crewed and trusted with the system it backs,
  so the misfit is that a component added to defend a coupled system couples it, and a reading of reliability sound
  for independent parts is unsound for parts that share a cause.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a costless correction. The one move that holds the
  reliability is to count the true independence before adding, trace the shared power, crew, premise and
  environment, price the complexity each backup brings, and remove the coupling rather than adding the spare, and it
  means refusing a backup that reads as safe, spending to find a common cause no incident has yet exposed, arguing
  against a defence in depth everyone can see is prudent, and doubting a reliability figure precisely because it
  climbs. The reckoning is a reliability held only by paying, continuously, the cost of refusing another guard,
  not a free correction.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never claimed as
  the house's. The staging is the house's; the problem of redundancy and its three mechanisms are Scott Sagan's, the
  interactive complexity and tight coupling and common-mode failure are Charles Perrow's, and the diffusion of
  responsibility is Latane and Darley's. The honest boundary, that redundancy does raise reliability for genuinely
  independent parallel parts and the paradox bites only where the addition adds coupling, complexity, or
  compensation, is carried as carefully as the finding itself.
