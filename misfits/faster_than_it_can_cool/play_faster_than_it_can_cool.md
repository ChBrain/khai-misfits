# Play: Faster Than It Can Cool

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

Faster Than It Can Cool.
A system makes heat and sheds it, and for the whole of its life the two are matched: the reaction inside it warms it, the surroundings carry the warmth away, and whenever it runs a little hot the loss grows to meet the gain and settles it back down. This is stability, and it is real: nudge the temperature up and it comes back, every time, so the operating point is not fragile and not lucky, it is genuinely, provably steady, and every check made on it, at every instant, passes. But the two are not made the same way. The heat the reaction generates rises exponentially with temperature, faster and faster the hotter it gets, while the heat the system loses rises only linearly, in plain proportion to how much hotter it is than the air around it. A curve against a line. For all the ordinary range they live in, the line stays above the curve and the system is safe, and the gap between them is the margin, the distance to disaster, and it looks comfortable.
And then the system ages. Nothing breaks and nothing wears through: the cell's internal resistance creeps up, the cooling fins foul with dust, the ambient climbs a few degrees, the ordinary things that happen to ordinary systems over years of ordinary use. None of it is damage in the sense of a crack or a flaw, the system stays sound, but each of them lifts the generation curve a little or lowers the loss line a little, and the gap between curve and line narrows. The margin was never spent by a shock; it was spent by time. And because the curve is exponential and the line is straight, they do not just get closer, they approach a single point where the line stops crossing the curve and only touches it, tangent, and that point is a hard threshold, sharp and computable, no soft edge and no surprise: it is set by the shapes of the two curves, and ordinary drift alone brings it down across the years toward the temperature the system actually runs at.
At that point everything reverses. One degree below it, a rise in temperature still makes the loss grow faster than the generation, and the system pulls itself back: stable. One degree above it, the same rise makes the generation grow faster than the loss, and the system pushes itself further up: not stable. The sign of the feedback has flipped, and it has flipped not because anything failed but because the curve finally out-climbs the line, and now every increment of heat makes more heat than it sheds, so the temperature runs away, accelerating, the reaction feeding on its own warmth, and there is no higher equilibrium to catch it because past the tangent point there is no equilibrium at all. What cooled it cannot catch it: the loss rises as fast as it can rise, linearly, and the generation is rising faster, and faster still, and the system goes to venting, to fire, to fracture, to whatever its particular destruction is, in seconds, from a temperature that an hour before was merely warm.
And nothing in it did wrong. The reaction obeyed its rate law, which is exponential because chemistry is exponential in temperature and that is not a fault. The cooling obeyed its law, which is linear because that is how heat crosses to the surroundings, and it never stopped working, it was simply outrun. The ageing was ordinary and no one could have prevented all of it. The operating point was truly stable and the checks that passed were not wrong to pass. The engineer who set the margin set it correctly and derated the system to sit well below the point, at the cost of the capacity given up to buy the distance, and still the point drifted down across the years toward the operating temperature, because holding the margin does not stop the ageing, it only buys time against it. The catastrophe is authored by no one and struck by nothing: it is the exponential quietly, lawfully, out-climbing the linear, at a threshold that ordinary time brings down to meet the system, past which the very feedback that made it safe is the thing that destroys it.

## Arc

The bend is this: heat generation rises exponentially with temperature and heat loss only linearly, so a genuinely stable operating point sits a fixed distance from a critical threshold that ordinary drift alone can reach, and the instant it is crossed the stabilising feedback reverses its sign and the system runs away, with no shock and no fault.
It runs on an asymmetry of two laws.
A system that makes heat and sheds it is stable wherever the loss rises faster with temperature than the generation does, because then a small warming is met by a larger cooling and erased. For the whole ordinary range this holds, and the operating point is truly steady, self-correcting, and passes every instantaneous check (Semenov). The stability is not an illusion and not a margin of luck: it is real, and that is what makes the trap, because a system that were merely fragile would show it, and this one shows nothing at all.
And the two laws are not symmetric. Generation rises exponentially with temperature, loss only linearly, a curve against a line, so the gap between them, the margin, is not fixed by how gentle the conditions are but by how far the operating point sits below the temperature where the curve finally catches the line (Semenov; Frank-Kamenetskii). That temperature is a hard critical point, a sharp bifurcation computable from the curves, and above it no steady state exists at all.
And ordinary drift closes the gap. Nothing has to break: an ageing cell's resistance rising, a cooling path fouling, an ambient creeping up lifts the curve or lowers the line by ordinary degrees over ordinary time, none of it damage and none of it a shock, and the margin that a blow never touched is spent by the years (Feng, Ouyang, and colleagues). The point does not stay put waiting to be reached; ordinary ageing brings it down toward the operating temperature, so the system holds where it always ran while the threshold above it sinks to meet it.
And at the point the feedback reverses. Below it, a warming grows the loss faster than the generation and the system settles back; above it, the same warming grows the generation faster than the loss and the system climbs; the sign of the net feedback flips at the tangency, and past it every degree makes more heat than it sheds, so the temperature accelerates to destruction with nothing left to catch it, the loss rising as fast as a line can and the generation rising faster (Semenov; Frank-Kamenetskii; Feng and colleagues). What steadied the system is now what runs it away.
None of it is fault or abuse. The exponential rate law is correct chemistry, the linear loss is correct heat transfer, the stable point was truly stable, and the drift that reached the threshold was ordinary ageing no vigilance fully prevents: the runaway emerges from the asymmetry of an exponential source against a linear sink, authored by no one. No slack was cut and no shock struck, so the exact load carried safely a thousand times becomes fatal because the point drifted onto it, not because anything was removed or anything hit.
Hold the margin, derate the operating point far below the threshold, cool harder, vent, and keep the drift monitored, and the system can be kept whole for a long time: but holding the margin costs the capacity, energy, and mass given up to buy the distance, it never abolishes the asymmetry that an exponential will always out-climb a linear, and it cannot stop the point from drifting closer as the system ages, so the safety is a permanent tax paid against a threshold that never stops approaching, and past the point there is no correction left at all, only the containment of a runaway already underway.

## Stakes
