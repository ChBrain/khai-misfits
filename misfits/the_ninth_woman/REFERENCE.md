# The Ninth Woman: Reference

The research warrant for [The Ninth Woman](play_the_ninth_woman.md). The title names
Brooks's own image; the play runs on Brooks's Law and the software-project dynamics
literature, from the original statement through the models that confirmed it, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging Brooks's Law as a **systemic system**: adding people to a late software project
makes it later, because the newcomers must be trained at the expense of the productive, the
communication paths multiply as the square of the team, and the critical task cannot be
partitioned across more hands. The play models the documented mechanics, the slipping
deadline, the reinforcements added, the ramp-up that drains before it gains, the combinatorial
cross-talk, the indivisible task, and the descope that alone makes the date real, as the
load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of weak workers. The governing finding of
the source literature is the play's governing law: men and months are not interchangeable, so a
schedule that buys time with headcount pays for the headcount in the very time it was buying.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the
  newcomers as weak or the manager as foolish, because the source finding is precisely that neither
  is: the new hires are capable and will be productive once assimilated, and adding capacity to a
  crisis is the obvious, responsible instinct. The misfit is that a late project is the worst time to
  add people, and the schedule cannot see the difference between a head and a hand.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a heroic sprint. No amount of effort makes
  a month hold nine people's worth of an indivisible task; the one move that works is a retreat, cutting
  the scope or staffing early, and it costs the promise the project was made on. The date is not saved by
  pushing harder; it is made real by promising less, at a price.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never
  claimed as the house's. The staging is the house's; the law, the dynamics model, and the team-cost
  account are theirs, and the condition Brooks's Law actually requires, a late project and an
  unpartitionable, communication-heavy task, is carried as carefully as the law itself.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per element.

- **[The Deadline](piece_the_deadline.md)**: carries the fixed date a late project is measured against
  (Brooks), the promise the schedule will try to save with headcount.
- **[The Reinforcements](piece_the_reinforcements.md)**: carries the people added to a late project
  (Brooks; DeMarco & Lister), capable and willing, added at the worst possible time.
- **[The Ramp-Up](piece_the_ramp_up.md)**: carries the training drain and assimilation delay (Abdel-Hamid
  & Madnick; DeMarco & Lister), the new hand a net loss before it is a gain.
- **[The Cross-Talk](piece_the_cross_talk.md)**: carries intercommunication overhead scaling as n(n-1)/2
  (Brooks), coordination growing as the square of the team while output does not.
- **[The Partition](piece_the_partition.md)**: carries the unpartitionable task (Brooks, the ninth woman),
  the sequential constraint that caps how far work can be divided.
- **[The Slip](piece_the_slip.md)**: carries the schedule extending despite the additions (Brooks;
  Abdel-Hamid & Madnick), the date moving the wrong way.
- **[The Descope](piece_the_descope.md)**: carries the remedy (practitioner; Brooks), cutting the promise or
  staffing early rather than adding people late.
- **[The Man-Month](piece_the_man_month.md)**: carries the phenomenon whole (Brooks), the myth that people
  and months are interchangeable, and its remedy in a smaller, honest promise.
