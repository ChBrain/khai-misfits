# Play: Onto the Survivors

## Name

Onto the Survivors.
A load is carried by many members at once, side by side: a cable of strands, a bundle of fibres, a bank of parallel lines, and no single one of them carries it alone. This is redundancy, and it is real: the load is divided across all of them, each holds only its share, and each share sits comfortably below what the member can bear, so the structure is sound and the design is honest and every member, checked on its own, passes. The strength of the whole is meant to be more than the strength of any one, because if a member should ever give, the others are there to take up its part. That is the whole point of sharing a load: no member is a single point of failure, and the bundle is safe in a way no lone member could be.
But the members are not all the same. Their strengths are drawn from a spread, some a little stronger, some a little weaker, as real members always are, and so there is always a weakest. Raise the load, and the weakest reaches its limit first and parts. This is nothing yet: one member of many, and its share was small, and the design expected it. But its share does not vanish when it parts. Its load is passed to the survivors, divided among the members still holding, and every one of their shares goes up. The rule that made the bundle safe, share the load across all of them, is the rule that now shares the failed member's load across all of them too. And most of the time the survivors take it, and hold, and the bundle settles at a slightly higher strain, and carries on.
Most of the time. Because the survivors are drawn from the same spread, and their shares have just gone up, the raised share may now exceed the strength of the next-weakest among them, and that member parts too, and its load is passed to the survivors in turn, raising every remaining share again, perhaps past the strength of the next, and the next. Each transfer is small, and each is exactly the sharing the design intended, and each individual member does precisely the right thing, holding until its share exceeds its strength and then handing its load to the rest. But the handing-on is a cascade: a parting raises the shares, the raised shares cause a parting, and below a certain fraction of members lost the cascade is finite and the survivors absorb it, while past that fraction it is not, because now every transfer breaks the next member and there is no share small enough to be safe on the few that remain.
And past that critical fraction the whole thing goes at once. The load that a hundred members carried is thrown onto ninety, then seventy, then thirty, each still doing the right thing, each parting only when its share exceeds its strength, and the shares are climbing faster than any surviving member can bear, so the avalanche runs to the end and the bundle ruptures completely, in an instant, from a state that a moment before was holding. The redundancy did not fail because it was too little. It failed because the mechanism that made it redundancy, the sharing of the load onto whoever remains, is the mechanism that carried the failure from the first weak member all the way through the strong ones. The survivors were never spared. They were only next.
And nothing in it did wrong. Every member was sound and held to its own true strength. The spread of strengths was ordinary and unavoidable, real members are never identical. The load-sharing rule was correct and is exactly what redundancy means. The engineer who sized the bundle sized it honestly, added members, widened margins, made the whole far stronger than the load, and none of that touched the fact that a shared load rises on every survivor as members drop, so past a fraction the sharing propagates the failure faster than any margin absorbs it. The rupture is authored by no one and struck by nothing: it is the load, passed lawfully onto the survivors, one sound member at a time, until there are too few left to survive it.

## Arc

The bend is this: the members of a redundant bundle share the load, so when the weakest parts its load falls onto the survivors and raises every share, and past a critical fraction of members lost each transfer breaks the next, so the very sharing that made the bundle safe carries the failure through it to complete rupture, with no weak member and no shock.
It runs on a shared load and a spread of strengths.
A bundle of parallel members carries a load together, each holding a share well below its own strength, so the structure is genuinely redundant and sound, and every member checked alone passes (Daniels). The redundancy is real, not thin: the load is divided, no member stands alone, and the whole is meant to outlast any one part. But the members' strengths are drawn from a spread, so there is always a weakest, and raising the load brings the weakest to its limit first.
And the sharing is the propagation. When a member parts, its share does not vanish, it is passed to the survivors, and every remaining share rises (Daniels). This is exactly the rule that makes redundancy work, the load shared across whoever holds, and it is exactly the rule that carries a failure onward, because the raised share may now exceed the next-weakest survivor's strength, and part it too.
And the cascade has a threshold. Below a critical fraction of members lost, the avalanche of successive partings is finite and the survivors absorb it and the bundle holds at a higher strain; past that fraction, the load per survivor has climbed so high that every transfer breaks the next member, and the avalanche runs to the end, its size diverging as the load nears the critical point, sharpest under local sharing where a parted member's load falls on its neighbours and the cascade localizes into a running front (Pradhan, Hansen, and Chakrabarti).
And the rupture is complete. Past the critical fraction there is no arrangement of the survivors that holds, because the shares now exceed even the strong, so the whole bundle parts at once, from a state that an instant before was carrying, the redundancy fully spent (Daniels; Pradhan, Hansen, and Chakrabarti).
None of it is a weak member or a skimped design. Every member held to its true strength, the spread of strengths is ordinary and real, the sharing rule is correct and is what redundancy means, and the failure emerges from the structure: a shared load meeting a spread of strengths, propagated onto the survivors one sound member at a time. No slack was cut and no common cause struck, so the redundancy fails not from too little of it but by the very sharing that made it redundant.
Add members, widen the margins, break the local sharing so a parting cannot fall on its neighbours, size the survivors for the load they will hold after the first partings, and the bundle can be held far from the critical fraction for a long time: but every one of these costs real material, weight, and capacity, and none abolishes the fact that a shared load rises on every survivor as members drop, so past the fraction no arrangement of sound members holds, and the redundancy is a distance bought against a cascade the sharing will always, past a point, carry through.

**[The Run](plot_the_run.md)**
Cue: a raised share exceeds the next member's strength. Closes when the avalanche has run and, this time, stopped, the survivors absorbing it and the bundle holding at a higher strain still, each burst so far finite and the fraction lost still short of the point past which they are not.

**[No Margin Left](plot_no_margin_left.md)**

Closes when the last margin is gone, the next burst is the one that cannot be absorbed, and the cascade that every earlier time was finite is now the one that runs to the end.

**[Nothing Holds](plot_nothing_holds.md)**

In [the Cross-Section](place_the_cross_section.md), the load thrown from each parted member onto the survivors exceeds even the strong, and the avalanche runs to completion, the whole bundle parting at once. Closes when the rupture is complete, from a state an instant before that was holding, the redundancy fully spent and no member left to survive, no weak part and no shock anywhere in it, only the load passed lawfully onto the survivors until too few remained.

## Stakes

Whether a genuinely redundant, sound structure can be kept whole, when the very sharing that makes it redundant is the sharing that carries a failure through it.
Every plot but the first moves the same way: a member parts, its load falls onto the survivors, every share rises, and the fraction of members lost climbs toward the critical point past which the avalanche no longer stops. The production cannot make the sharing spare the survivors, because sharing the load is what redundancy is, and sharing the load is what passes the failure on: the same rule cannot do the one without the other. It can only show that no member was weak and no design skimped, that every member held to its true strength and every transfer was small and exactly the sharing intended, that the spread of strengths was ordinary and the load-sharing correct, and that the one move which keeps the bundle whole, holding it far below the critical fraction with more members and wider margins, is a distance bought at real cost against a cascade the sharing will always, past a point, carry through, so the redundancy is real and the members are sound and the survivors, taking each failed member's load exactly as they were built to, were never spared but only next.
