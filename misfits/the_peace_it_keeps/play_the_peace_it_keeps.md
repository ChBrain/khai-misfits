# Play: The Peace It Keeps

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

The Peace It Keeps.
A body is not a thing but a truce. It is a commonwealth of cells that long ago gave up competing, each holding its own reproduction in check so the whole could live, and it stays a body only because that peace is kept, held down every hour by the machinery that tells a cell when to specialise, when to stop dividing, when to die. The blood keeps the peace hardest of all, because the blood must be remade for a lifetime: the marrow renews it wave on wave, and every division is a fresh chance for a cell to change. Most changes are nothing, and most that are something are caught and cleared. But the peace has no enforcer for its own. Let one cell gain a heritable edge, a small advantage in dividing or in not dying, and the oldest logic in biology resumes inside the body: the cell that leaves more copies leaves more copies, and its line spreads, not by malice and not by plan, but because that is simply what an advantage does. The line is the body's own, made of the body, indistinguishable from it in kind, so there is nothing foreign to expel, no invader to name. It does not conquer by eviction: it poisons the ground and jams the others, so the marrow can be full and the blood still fail, the normal cells present and unable to finish. The body is undone by one of its own, a coup with no usurper from outside, the whole brought down by a part of itself doing the one thing an advantage always does. And the only way to stop the line is to strike at dividing cells, which is what the body's own renewal is, or to replace the whole system, or to cut the line back and leave the resistant remnant room, so the remedy cannot spare what it must spend, and the cost of removing the harm is the renewal the body cannot live without. It is not a cure that is missing but a clean one: the peace, once broken from within, can be fought to a standstill and sometimes truly won, but never at no price, because the enemy is kin and the weapon cannot tell kin apart.

## Arc

The bend is this: a body is a truce that exists only by holding its own cells' competition down, and a cell that gains a heritable edge resumes that competition inside the body, so the whole is undone not by an invader but by a component of itself, a lower order rising against the higher order that suppressed it, the killer made of its victim (Nowell; Buss).
It runs on a peace that must be actively kept.
Because multicellularity is a subordination and not a given, a state in which cell-level selection was pressed down under the good of the whole by the machinery of differentiation, programmed death, and senescence, the body persists only so long as that suppression holds, a higher-level individual standing on the held-down competition of the cells it is made of (Buss; Michod). The truce is an achievement, and it is never finished.
And the keeping never rests, because the blood is remade.
Because the marrow must renew the blood for a lifetime, dividing its stem cells wave on wave, every division is a selective round and a fresh chance for a variant, so the very upkeep that keeps the tissue alive is what keeps the competition armed, and mutant lines accrue with age as a structural matter and not an accident, common in the old and near-universal at depth (Jaiswal and Genovese; Watson). The renewal is the exposure, and it cannot be switched off without ending the tissue.
And a cell gains an edge, and the edge wins.
Because a heritable change can give one line a small advantage in proliferating or in escaping death, the oldest rule resumes: the line that leaves more copies leaves more copies, expanding through blind selection with no intent and no malice, the cell fitting its niche and not choosing the harm (Nowell; Greaves and Maley). The selection is real and central, and it is the same Darwinian logic that built the living world, now running one level down.
And the line does not evict, it jams.
Because the growing clone remodels the marrow and blocks the maturing of the normal cells rather than shoving them out, the blood can fail while the stem cells persist, the marrow crowded with cells that cannot finish and the counts falling all the same, the harm dealt by a poisoned ground and a jammed line and not by eviction (Miraki-Moud and colleagues). The mechanism is suppression of the body's own making, and its reach runs past the marrow into the whole frame (Jaiswal and colleagues).
And the killer cannot be cast out, because it is kin.
Because the line is the body's own cell, of the same kind as the tissue it destroys, there is nothing foreign to expel and no border to defend: the enemy is a part of the self, so the body cannot disown it, and the clone in turn is, in the canonical case, confined to the one body, dying with the host and passing through no germline, a fight per organism whose rare exceptions are the transmissible cancers (Aktipis and colleagues). The identity is the trap's floor, and it is what tells this apart from any collapse of a population outside a body.
And calling it off means cutting the renewal.
Because the only way to lower the line is to strike at dividing cells, or to ablate and replace the whole blood system, or to cut the sensitive clone and leave the resistant remnant unopposed, every remedy spends the renewal or the system the body needs, on a knife-edge where too little loses to the clone and too much loses the marrow (Greaves and Maley; Gatenby). The trade is unavoidable, and it is costly, not futile: the peace can be fought to a standstill and some leukaemias truly cured, but no dose removes the harm and keeps the protection clean.
None of it is a fix. The body suppresses its cells because that is what makes it a body, the blood renews because it must, the line expands because an advantage expands, so each part is only biology doing exactly what it does, and the undoing of the whole emerges from the sum, no villain and no malice required. Halt it, and the halt is dear: it means striking the renewal the body lives by, or replacing the system whole, on a knife-edge where the weapon cannot tell the kin apart. Short of it the line, doing only what fitness rewards, spreads through the marrow it is made of and jams the blood it is made of, the body brought down not by any enemy at the gate but by the peace it keeps failing at one cell, the truce that made it a body broken by a member of the truce.

## Stakes

Whether a body renewing itself is the health it appears, or is also the ground of its own undoing, the whole kept alive by a peace that has no enforcer for its own and can be broken by a single one of its cells.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the coup, which no cell intends and no malice drives, the line only doing what an advantage does and the checks only sometimes missing one, and away from the remedy that would lower the line but cannot spare the renewal it is made of. The production cannot make the disease an invasion, because there is nothing foreign in it: the cell is the body's own, of the same kind as the tissue it wrecks, so suppressing it and renewing the blood and clearing a bad line are each only the body being a body. It can only show that the truce which makes a body is a truce a member can break, so the whole tends to be undone by a part of itself and can fail of its own overgrown tissue with no enemy at the gate, and that the one move which would stop the harm, striking the dividing line, is the move that also strikes the dividing renewal, on a knife-edge with no clean setting. The cost is real and the fight is not hopeless: the peace can be held to a standstill and some leukaemias cured outright, but never at no price, because the enemy is kin and the weapon raised against it falls on the kin it is made of.
