# Play: The Spent Margin

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

The Spent Margin.
Fit the car with anti-lock brakes and it stops shorter on a wet road: a real gain, measured on the track, a margin of safety that was not there before. And then watch the driver. Not a reckless one, an ordinary one, who keeps, as we all do, a rough sense of how much danger is acceptable for the reward of getting there sooner, and who now feels the road as safer than it was. So the margin reads as slack. The following distance closes a little, the speed on the bend creeps up, the eyes leave the road a moment longer, each adjustment small and each one sensible for a driver who feels a buffer under them, until the felt danger has climbed back to exactly where it sat before the brakes were fitted, and the margin the engineer built is gone, spent not on safety but on arriving sooner. The device did everything it promised. The stopping distance is shorter. And the crash rate has not moved, because the driver banked none of the gain and drew all of it as speed. And where the risk went, when it was re-taken, was not only back onto the driver who chose it: some of it landed on the pedestrian at the kerb and the cyclist at the verge, who were handed no brakes and no margin, and who now share a road driven a little harder for the safety they never received. Nothing in it is recklessness. The engineer built a real protection. The driver kept the same balance of danger and haste they always keep. And the sum of a working safety measure and a defended target of risk is a gain consumed by the behaviour it invited, so the thing built to save lives saves fewer than its test promised, and moves some of the danger it removed onto those outside the car.

## Arc

The bend is this: a safety measure that genuinely works hands the protected agent a felt margin, and the agent, holding a roughly constant target of accepted risk, re-spends the margin as behaviour until the felt danger is back at target, so the engineered gain is consumed, partly or wholly, by the conduct the safety invited, from no recklessness at all.
It runs on a margin that is real.
The measure does what it was built to do: the anti-lock brake stops the car shorter, the belt holds the body, the helmet takes the blow, and on the track, with behaviour held fixed, the gain is exactly as large as the engineer measured (Peltzman; Aschenbrenner and Biehl). The protection is not an illusion, and that is the beginning of the trouble, because a real margin is a real thing to spend.
And the agent keeps a target.
Each of us holds a rough level of accepted risk, a felt danger we trade against the reward of speed or ease, and we defend it: too much danger and we slow, too little and we press on (Wilde; Adams). A safety measure that lowers the felt danger leaves the agent under target, with risk, as it feels to them, now going spare.
And the margin is spent.
Reading the road as safer, the agent re-takes the risk to restore the balance: closes the following distance, carries more speed, lends the road less attention, each move small and sensible for someone who feels a buffer beneath them, until the felt danger has climbed back to target and the margin is gone (Aschenbrenner and Biehl). The device works perfectly and the crash rate does not move, because the gain was drawn as speed, not banked as safety.
And the risk can shift.
The re-taken risk does not all fall on the one who chose it. A road driven harder for the driver's new margin is a more dangerous road for the pedestrian and the cyclist, who were fitted with no brakes and handed no buffer, so part of the danger the measure removed from inside the car is transferred to those outside it (Peltzman), who bear a share of a spend they had no part in.
None of it is recklessness or error. The engineer who built a working device, the driver who kept the same balance of danger and reward they keep every day: each is doing exactly the sound thing, and the offset emerges from the sum, no one needing to drive badly for a real protection to be consumed by the behaviour it invites. What is meant to save lives saves fewer than its test.
Keep the gain by changing the felt danger and not the real one, so the target is met without spending the margin: sharpen the sense of risk, strip out the false comfort, the shared space with its kerbs and lines removed, the thought of the spike in the steering wheel, and the driver, feeling less safe, holds the speed down of their own accord: but it means asking people to feel less safe in order to be safer, giving up the comfort the measure was sold as, and it cannot be carried past the protection real crashes still need, so the gain is kept only by trading away the feeling of safety, against the whole reason the measure was wanted.

## Triggers

**[The Fitting](plot_the_fitting.md)**
The Fitter installs the safety measure and proves it on the track, behaviour held fixed, so a real margin appears exactly as large as the test measures. Closes when the device works as built and the gain is banked on paper, the margin real and, as yet, unspent.

**[The Feel](plot_the_feel.md)**
Cue: the margin reaches the driver on the road. The Driver, keeping a target of accepted risk, reads the road as safer than it was and feels the felt danger fall below target, with risk now going spare. Closes when the margin is sensed as slack, the balance under target and the driver holding room they did not have before.

**[The Spend](plot_the_spend.md)**
Cue: the driver has room to spend. The Driver re-takes the risk to restore the balance, closing the distance, carrying more speed, lending the road less attention, each move sensible for one who feels a buffer beneath them. Closes when the felt danger is back at target and the margin is consumed, the device working perfectly and the crash rate unmoved.

**[The Shift](plot_the_shift.md)**
Cue: the re-taken risk has to land somewhere. The road driven harder for the driver's margin is more dangerous for the Exposed at the verge, who was fitted with no brakes and handed no buffer, so part of the danger removed from inside the car is transferred outside it. Closes when the uncovered bear a share of a spend they had no part in, the gain not only shrunk but moved.

**[The Redesign](plot_the_redesign.md)**
Cue: someone would keep the gain instead of spending it. The Redesigner changes the felt danger rather than the real one, sharpening the sense of risk and stripping the false comfort so the target is met without spending the margin. Closes when the driver, feeling less safe, holds the speed of their own accord and the gain is kept ... or the comfort the measure was sold as is demanded back, the felt danger is smoothed again, and the margin is spent once more.

## Stakes

Whether a real safety gain can be kept as safety, or is always re-spent as performance by the agent it protects.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the consumed margin, which each small adjustment is sensible to make and all of which the driver is right to make, and away from the kept gain, which would ask the driver to feel less safe than the measure makes them. The production cannot make the margin stay banked, because the agent holds a target of accepted risk and a felt margin reads, to them, as risk going spare, so the spend is a sound balance kept, not a fault: the engineer building a working device and the driver holding the same danger against the same reward are each only doing the sensible thing. It can only show that no one is reckless, that a real protection meeting a defended target sums to a gain drawn off as speed, and that the one move that keeps the gain, changing the felt danger so the target is met without spending the margin, must be bought by asking people to feel less safe in order to be safer, giving up the comfort the measure was sold as, and cannot be carried past the protection that real crashes still need, so the device is sound and the balance is kept and the margin that was built to save lives is spent by the very behaviour a working safety invites.
