# The Spent Margin: Reference

The research warrant for [The Spent Margin](play_the_spent_margin.md).
The dynamic is risk compensation: Peltzman's offsetting of mandated safety, Wilde's risk homeostasis, and Adams's
risk thermostat, with the Munich anti-lock brake study as the field case and the partial-offset limit credited, all
cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging risk compensation as a **behavioural feedback structure**: a safety measure lowers the risk in one channel
and hands the protected agent a felt margin, the agent holds a roughly constant target level of accepted risk and
reads the margin as risk now going spare, so they re-spend it, driving faster, following closer, attending less,
until the felt danger is back at the target and the engineered gain has been consumed, partly or wholly, by the
behaviour it invited, and the risk it removed from the protected can land on those who got no margin at all. The
play models the documented mechanics, the real safety gain that opens a margin, the target risk the agent defends,
the reading that senses the margin as slack, the spend that re-takes the risk to restore the balance, the transfer
by which the re-taken risk shifts onto the uncovered bystander, and the redesign that alone keeps the gain by
changing the felt danger rather than the real one, as the load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of a
reckless driver. The governing finding of the source is the play's governing law: where a safety measure improves
the felt margin without changing the target risk the agent keeps, the agent re-spends the margin as behaviour, so
the realised safety gain is less than the engineered one, and can fall to nothing where the margin is salient and
cheap to spend.

It is distinct from the house's Moral Hazard, and the distinction is the whole point of the cut: moral hazard turns
on who bears the cost, a party shielded from the price of a risk by insurance or a backstop takes less care because
someone else pays, whereas risk compensation needs no shifted cost at all, the agent still bears the full price of
a crash and re-takes the risk anyway, because a felt margin, not a transferred bill, is what the behaviour answers,
a defended target of risk rather than a slipped incentive. It is also distinct from the house's Belt and Braces,
where added redundancy breeds new failure modes in the system itself, complexity and common cause and shirking:
there the device gains failure paths, here the device works exactly as designed and it is the operator who spends
the margin, so the loss is behavioural adaptation to a real protection, not a fault the protection introduced. Belt
and Braces names risk compensation only as one shirking mechanism among its three; this play stages it whole and
carries the rest of it.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the driver as
  reckless or the engineer as naive, because the source finding is precisely that each is behaving soundly: the
  device genuinely works and delivers a real margin, and the agent who re-spends it is only holding the same
  target of accepted risk that every one of us holds crossing a road, trading a felt danger against a reward. The
  offset is authored by no one and emerges from a real protection meeting a defended target, so the misfit is that
  a safety measure that works exactly as built calls forth the behaviour that spends its benefit, no recklessness
  and no error required for the gain to shrink.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a costless correction. The one move that keeps the gain
  is to change the felt danger rather than the real one, so the target is met without spending the margin:
  sharpening the sense of risk, removing the false comfort, the shared space that takes out the kerbs and lines, or
  the spike in the steering wheel, Tullock's provocation that Adams carries into the risk-thermostat frame. It means asking people to feel less safe in order to be
  safer, forgoing the comfort the measure was sold as, and it cannot be pushed past the protection that real
  crashes still need, so the reckoning is a gain kept by trading away the feeling of safety, not a free correction.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never claimed as
  the house's, and its reach is not overstated. The staging is the house's; the offsetting of mandated safety and
  the shift of risk onto non-occupants are Peltzman's, the homeostatic target that behaviour defends is Wilde's,
  the risk thermostat and the personal balance are Adams's, the anti-lock brake field case is Aschenbrenner and
  Biehl's, and the honest limit, that the offset is real but usually partial and the net gain most often survives reduced, tempering Peltzman's stronger claim of a near-complete offset (Crandall and Graham; Robertson),
  is carried as carefully as the finding itself: the misfit stages the behavioural feedback and credits that it
  seldom erases the whole of what the measure delivered.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per element.

- **[The Margin](piece_the_margin.md)**: carries the real safety gain the measure opens (Peltzman; Aschenbrenner &
  Biehl), the felt buffer proven on the track and handed to the agent to spend.
- **[The Balance](piece_the_balance.md)**: carries the target risk the agent defends (Wilde; Adams), the
  roughly constant setting of felt danger against reward that converts a margin into re-taken risk.
- **[The Transfer](piece_the_transfer.md)**: carries the risk shifted onto the uncovered (Peltzman), the share of
  the re-spent risk that lands on the pedestrian and the motorcyclist who got no margin.
- **[The Thermostat](piece_the_thermostat.md)**: carries the phenomenon whole (Wilde; Adams), the feedback loop
  that reads a margin, spends it, and holds the felt danger at target so the engineered gain is consumed.
