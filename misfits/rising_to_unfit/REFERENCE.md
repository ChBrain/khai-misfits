# Rising to Unfit: Reference

The research warrant for [Rising to Unfit](play_rising_to_unfit.md). The mechanism is the
Peter Principle; the play runs on the management, economics, and empirical literature on
promotion and its distortions cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the Peter Principle as a **systemic system**: a hierarchy in which every promotion is
earned and the ladder fills with people in the wrong jobs, because promotion rewards mastery of
the current role with a different role it does not predict fit for, and never moves anyone back.
The play models the documented mechanics, performance in one role used to select for another,
regression to the mean when the new role tests untested gifts, the manager-versus-maker skill
gap, and the up-only ladder that lifts on success and will not lower on failure, as the
load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of foolish people. The governing finding of
the source literature is the play's governing law: promoting on present performance, a weak
predictor of performance in a different role, and never demoting, tends over time to fill every
post with someone risen to their level of incompetence.

## Origin

The phenomenon in practice, and the research spine beneath it.

| Source                | Key Work / Event                                                                           | Scope                                                                                                                                                                     |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Peter & Hull**      | "The Peter Principle: Why Things Always Go Wrong" (1969)                                   | The naming and the law: in a hierarchy, employees rise to their level of incompetence, so in time every post tends to be held by someone unfit for it. The play's spine.  |
| **Lazear**            | "The Peter Principle: A Theory of Decline", _Journal of Political Economy_ (2004)          | A formal model: performance has a transitory component, so those promoted were partly lucky and **regress to the mean** in the new role. The Rise and the Regression.     |
| **Benson, Li & Shue** | "Promotions and the Peter Principle", _Quarterly Journal of Economics_ (2019)              | Empirical: firms promote their best salespeople into management, though sales performance **negatively** predicts managerial performance. The Wrong Signal, measured.     |
| **Lazear & Rosen**    | "Rank-Order Tournaments as Optimum Labor Contracts", _Journal of Political Economy_ (1981) | Tournament theory: promotion doubles as prize and as sorting, so using it to reward the last job conflicts with using it to fill the next. Why the ladder reads backward. |
| **Galton**            | "Regression Towards Mediocrity in Hereditary Stature" (1886)                               | Regression to the mean: an extreme measure is followed by a less extreme one, the statistical root beneath Lazear's account of the post-promotion decline.                |
| **Practitioner**      | career ladders, the maker-to-manager track, "up or out", and the no-demotion norm          | The naming in organisational life: the one-way ladder, and the standing difficulty of rewarding people without moving them out of what they are good at.                  |

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast any
  seat as foolish or scheming, because the source finding is precisely that everyone is reasonable:
  the mastery is real, promoting the best performer on demonstrated performance is fair, and not
  demoting people is humane. The misfit is that a fair reward, read from the wrong role, installs
  against the wrong job.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a wise reform arriving in time. The
  stranded master cannot be moved back without indicting the promotion, and the next star is already
  rising the same slope, so the reckoning recurs rather than resolves, never turning on one catchable
  decision.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here,
  never claimed as the house's. The staging is the house's; the findings are theirs.

- **[The New Job](piece_the_new_job.md)**: carries the skill gap between roles, the manager-versus-maker
  change of trade the ladder hides.
- **[The Level of Incompetence](piece_the_level_of_incompetence.md)**: carries the Peter Principle proper
  (Peter & Hull), the rung a person reaches by succeeding at every rung below.
- **[The One-Way Ladder](piece_the_one_way_ladder.md)**: carries the promote-up-never-demote norm, the
  structure that makes a bad fit permanent.
- **[The Stranded Master](piece_the_stranded_master.md)**: carries the double loss, a bad manager made and
  a good maker unmade by one promotion.
- **[The Rise](piece_the_rise.md)**: carries the trajectory and regression to the mean (Lazear; Galton),
  the climb that carries a person past their fit rather than to it.
- **[The Peter Principle](piece_the_peter_principle.md)**: carries the phenomenon whole (Peter & Hull),
  every post tending to be held by someone risen just past their fit, held whole by nobody.
