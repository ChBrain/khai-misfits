# Play: No One Gains by Telling You

## Name

No One Gains by Telling You.
A thing is sold in two parts. There is the base, the part on the sticker, the price you compare and choose on: the cheap printer, the free checking account, the low room rate, the monthly gym fee that looks like nothing a day. And there is the add-on, the part that comes later and is not on the sticker: the ink, the overdraft fee, the minibar, the months you pay for and never use. The base is priced low, sometimes below what it costs to make, and the add-on is priced high, and the high add-on pays for the low base. This is not a trick played by a bad firm. It is what every firm in a competitive market does, because it is what wins customers: put the low number where the buyer looks, and make the money back where the buyer does not.
And the buyer does not look, because the buyer is human. Not stupid, not careless, just built to weigh the price in front of them more heavily than the cost that comes later, to choose on the salient number and underweight the deferred one, a present bias so ordinary and so documented that firms can design around it the way an engineer designs around gravity. So the buyer picks the cheap printer and meets the dear ink, takes the free account and pays the overdraft, and pays, across the whole arrangement, more than they would have under an honest single price, and none of it required a lie, only a low number where they were looking and a high one where they were not.
And here is the turn that makes it a trap and not just a bad habit. Somewhere there is a firm that could tell the truth: advertise the real total, warn the buyer that the printer is cheap because the ink is dear, unshroud the add-on and compete on honesty. Surely that firm wins the market. It does not. Because a buyer who is warned does not reward the honest firm with their custom, they simply learn to avoid the add-on: they buy the cheap printer and refill it with someone else's cheaper ink, take the free account and never overdraw, join the gym and actually go, or do not join at all. The truth turns a myope into a sophisticate, and a sophisticate does not need the honest firm, they need only the cheap base that every firm already offers, and then they dodge the add-on wherever they are. So the honest firm pays the whole cost of educating the market and captures none of the reward, and telling the truth, which should have been the winning move, is the losing one. This is the curse: no one gains by telling you, so no one does.
And so the shroud holds, not because anyone conspires to hold it, but because unshrouding does not pay, and the market settles into a stable arrangement in which the add-on stays hidden, the base stays cheap, and the money flows from the people who underweight the add-on to the people who avoid it. The sophisticated ride the cheap base and dodge the dear add-on, and they are subsidized, exactly, by the myopic who take the same cheap base and meet the add-on head on. The myope pays a tax, and the tax funds the discount the sophisticate enjoys, and neither the firm nor the sophisticate has any reason to end it: the firm because shrouding is what competition rewards, the sophisticate because the arrangement pays them. Only the myope loses, and the myope is not a fool, only present-biased, which is to say only ordinary.
And nothing in it is a crime. The firm competes and shrouds because a firm that unshrouds loses; the buyer chooses on the salient price because that is how attention and time work; the honest rival would fix it and cannot, because the fix educates buyers into avoiding the add-on rather than into rewarding honesty. The exploitation is real and the loss is real and there is no one to catch, because the one move that would clear it, information, is exactly the move the market is built to make unprofitable, so the truth sits there, cheap to tell and worth nothing to the teller, and the shroud stays down because no one gains by lifting it.

## Arc

The bend is this: a documented consumer bias is exploited not by a cheat but by an ordinary competitive equilibrium in which transparency does not pay, so a low base subsidized by a shrouded add-on stands as a stable arrangement, no firm defecting to honesty and no informed shopper disciplining it.
It runs on a base and a shrouded add-on.
A good is sold as a low, salient base and a high, deferred add-on, and the high add-on subsidizes the low base, because the buyer compares on the sticker and the firm that puts the lowest number there wins the sale (Gabaix and Laibson). This is not a fringe tactic but the equilibrium: in a market of competing firms, every one shrouds, because the low visible price is what custom follows.
And the bias is the opening. The buyer underweights the add-on, not from stupidity but from a real, measured present bias, weighing the salient price now more than the cost that lands later (DellaVigna and Malmendier). So the buyer chooses the cheap base and meets the dear add-on, and pays more, across the whole, than an honest single price would have cost, with no lie told, only a number placed where attention was and a number placed where it was not.
And the cure is cursed. A firm that unshrouds, that advertises the true total and educates the buyer, does not win the market, because a debiased buyer with a close substitute for the add-on avoids the add-on rather than rewarding the honest firm: the truth turns myopes into sophisticates, and sophisticates need only the cheap base, not the truth-teller (Gabaix and Laibson). So the honest firm bears the cost of education and captures none of the gain, and telling the truth is a losing move.
And the arrangement is stable and it transfers. The shroud holds because unshrouding does not pay, and the money flows from the myopic, who take the cheap base and meet the add-on, to the sophisticated, who take the same cheap base and dodge the add-on, a cross-subsidy the myope funds and the sophisticate and the firm both profit from (Gabaix and Laibson). Only the myope loses, and the myope is only present-biased, which is only ordinary.
None of it is a swindle. The firm shrouds because a firm that does not, loses; the buyer chooses on the salient price because that is how attention works; the honest rival cannot fix it because the fix creates sophisticates, not customers: the exploitation emerges from the structure, a real bias meeting a competitive market in which information does not pay. No cheat is required and no dupe, so there is no one to catch and no one to blame.
Regulate the disclosure, mandate the true total on the sticker, and the base-and-add-on split can be made plain: but a buyer shown the total still meets the same close substitute and still merely avoids the add-on rather than rewarding disclosure, so mandated transparency creates sophisticates who dodge the tax and leaves the arrangement's logic intact, and the escape is bought only where the add-on has no substitute to dodge into, against a market that will shroud again wherever one appears, so the cure is real only where the curse has no room to run, and worth nothing to any single firm anywhere else.
