# Accidental Adversaries: Reference

The research warrant for [Accidental Adversaries](play_accidental_adversaries.md). The title is
Kemeny's systems archetype; the play runs on the account of cooperation breaking down through defensive
moves, from the archetype's crossing loops through tit-for-tat and unilateral de-escalation, all cited
below.

## Line of Work

Staging accidental adversaries as a **systemic system**: two parties in a symbiotic partnership, each
of whom makes a locally rational move to protect its own results, the move harming the partner across
a boundary neither can see past, so the harm is dealt without intent and received as intent, answered
in kind, and compounded until allies become adversaries no one chose. The play models the documented
mechanics, the symbiosis whose mutual benefit builds the dependence it exposes, the local move that is
sensible on its own side, the blindspot at the boundary where the harm crosses unseen, the
misattribution that reads an accident as an attack, the spiral that compounds move and counter-move,
and the true adversary indistinguishable from the accidental one, as the load-bearing vectors of the
structure, not as a tale of villainy. The governing finding of the source literature is the play's
governing law: two parties whose success depends on each other can, through actions each takes to
improve its own results, inadvertently undermine each other, so that each experiences the other's
defensive response as aggression and a partnership degrades into a reinforcing rivalry. It is a
two-party archetype, distinct from the house's single-party loops, Fixes that Fail, Shifting the
Burden, and Limits to Growth: here the harm crosses a boundary between two optimisers, each blind to
its effect on the other.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast either
  party as the aggressor, because the source finding is precisely that neither intends the harm: each
  move is a locally rational self-protection, each party sincerely experiences itself as the victim of
  the other, and the harm each deals is invisible to it because it lands across the boundary where only
  the partner feels it. The misfit is that two reasonable parties, each defending itself, destroy an
  alliance that lifted them both, with no malice anywhere in the loop.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a costless reconciliation. The one move that
  works is to see the whole loop, own your half, and stop your own undermining first, and it means
  de-escalating undefended while the other may still be striking, trusting again across a seam thick
  with felt betrayals, and it cannot be told, in advance, from surrender to a true adversary who would
  take the dropped guard as an opening. The reckoning is an undefended step that a real predator is
  built to exploit, not a safe correction.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here,
  never claimed as the house's. The staging is the house's; the archetype, its crossing-loop structure,
  the attribution error, and the de-escalation strategy are theirs, and the condition the misfit
  actually requires, a real symbiosis and a harm that crosses a boundary each party is blind past, is
  carried as carefully as the finding itself.
