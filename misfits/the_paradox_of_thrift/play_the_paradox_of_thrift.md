# Play: The Paradox of Thrift

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

The Paradox of Thrift.
Hard times are coming, so you do the sensible thing: you spend less and put more by, building a buffer against the lean months ahead ... and so does everyone else, because they are prudent too. But your spending was someone's wage and theirs was yours, so when all spend less, all earn less, and the incomes you meant to save from shrink to meet the saving. The nation reaches to save more and saves less, because the thrift that would make one household safer makes every household poorer, and the buffer each grasped for is smaller in the grasping.

## Arc

The bend is this: saving is the right move for a household and the wrong move for all households at once, and the very act of all reaching for it is what takes it away.
It runs on a circle.
One person's spending is another's income: the coin you lay down is a wage, a sale, a takings, and it comes round in time as someone's spending on you. So the flow that feeds every income is the sum of everyone's spending, and to save is to lift a coin out of that flow, to earn without spending back. For one household this is pure gain: it earns as before and spends less, and the buffer grows. For all households together it cannot be, because the spending one holds back is the income another does not receive, so a general thrift does not lift coins out of a flow that stays full, it shrinks the flow.
And the shrinking feeds itself. Less spending is less income, so firms sell less and cut wages and hours and jobs, and the households whose incomes fall now have more reason than ever to save and less to save from, so they tighten again, and the demand falls again, and the circle turns downward. The saving that each meant to do out of a steady income is thwarted by the fall in income the saving itself caused: the country saves a larger share of a smaller whole, and the sum put by need not rise at all.
None of it is folly. Saving against uncertainty is prudent, cutting spending when your income falls is necessary, and no household can be asked to spend a nation out of a slump on its own. The saving would even be harmless, or good, if it were borrowed and spent by someone else, put to work as investment: but in a slump confidence is low and the saved coin finds no borrower, so it sits, a leak and not a loan, draining the flow instead of feeding it.
Let someone spend against the tide, an investor with nerve or a government with a deficit, and the injected demand refills the flow and the incomes return, and the thrift becomes safe again because something now offsets it: but the offset means spending freely exactly when every private instinct says to hoard, running a debt when debt feels most reckless, and it asks one actor to do the opposite of what looks prudent to each, so the cure is the hardest thing to reach for at the moment it is most needed.

## Stakes

Whether a nation's prudence ever leaves it richer, or only ever poorer in the same reaching for safety.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the saving, which each household is right to want and all are impoverished by seeking at once, and away from the spending, which would refill the flow and which every private instinct forbids in a slump. The production cannot make a general thrift add to the nation's wealth, because one household's spending is another's income, so the coins all withhold are the incomes all lose, and saving more of a shrinking whole need not be saving more at all. It can only show that no one erred, that saving against hard times is prudent and cutting to a fallen income is necessary and no household can spend the slump away alone, and that the one move that refills the flow, a spender who spends against the tide, asks the opposite of what looks prudent to each, at the moment it is hardest to dare, so the thrift is sound and the sum of it is loss and the cure is the thing no private hand will reach for first.
