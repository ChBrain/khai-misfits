# The Paradox of Thrift: Reference

The research warrant for [The Paradox of Thrift](play_the_paradox_of_thrift.md).
The term is Keynes's and Samuelson's; the play runs on the theory of aggregate demand and the fallacy of
composition, from Mandeville's antecedent through Keynes's General Theory and the multiplier, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the paradox of thrift as a **circulatory system**: an economy in which one agent's spending is
another's income, so that each household's prudent decision to save more and spend less, when made by all at
once, lowers aggregate demand, lowers incomes, and leaves the nation saving no more, and perhaps less, than
before. The play models the documented mechanics, the prudent saving that is sound for one household, the
circular flow in which spending withheld is income not received, the fallacy of composition by which what is
true of one saver is false of all savers together, the contraction that feeds itself as fallen incomes prompt
more saving still, the saving that leaks from the flow when no investment borrows it back, and the offsetting
injection that alone refills the flow at the cost of spending against every private instinct, as the
load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of hoarders or fools. The governing finding of the
source literature is the play's governing law: saving is a withdrawal from the circular flow of income, so an
increase in the desire to save, unmatched by investment, reduces income until realised saving falls back to
meet investment, and the attempt to save more can lower the saving actually done. It is distinct from the
house's The Bank Run, where a solvent institution is felled by a self-fulfilling panic and a first-come queue:
here there is no institution and no run, only the ordinary demand externality of prudent saving. And it is
distinct from the house's The Tyranny of Small Decisions, where many small choices sum to an outcome no one
chose: here each does choose to save, the choice is sound in itself, and the aggregate defeats the choice's
own purpose through the fall in income the saving causes.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the saver as a
  hoarder or a fool, because the source finding is precisely that each move is right: saving against
  uncertainty is prudent, cutting spending to a fallen income is necessary, and no household can be asked to
  spend a nation out of a slump alone. The loss is authored by no error and emerges from the structure, a
  circular flow in which one's spending is another's income, so the misfit is that a nation of households each
  doing the prudent thing tightens itself poorer and saves no more for all the saving.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a costless correction. The one move that refills
  the flow is a spender who spends against the tide, an investor with nerve or a government with a deficit,
  and it means spending freely exactly when every private instinct says to hoard, running a debt when debt
  feels most reckless, and doing the opposite of what looks prudent to each household. The reckoning is demand
  restored by someone willing to act against the grain at the worst moment, not a free correction, so the cure
  for a nation saving itself poor is an offset no private hand will reach for first.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never
  claimed as the house's. The staging is the house's; the paradox of thrift, aggregate demand, the circular
  flow, the multiplier, and the fallacy of composition are theirs, and the condition the trap actually
  requires, a general increase in the desire to save unmatched by investment, is carried as carefully as the
  finding itself.

  a nation impoverished by its own prudence, and its remedy in a demand no private saver will supply.
