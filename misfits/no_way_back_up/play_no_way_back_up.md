# Play: No Way Back Up

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

No Way Back Up.
A population of living things reproduces by copying. Each genome carries some number of small mutations, most of them mildly harmful and none of them fatal, and every generation the parents copy themselves to their offspring, passing on the mutations they have. In such a population there is always a cleanest class, the individuals carrying the fewest mutations of anyone alive, and they are the best the population has and also, precisely because they are the best, the rarest. Now let the population be finite, as every real one is, so that which individuals happen to reproduce is partly a matter of chance. The cleanest class is small, and a small class is easily missed by chance: in some generation, simply by the luck of who leaves offspring, not one of the fewest-mutations individuals is copied forward, and the class is gone. And here is the turn: once the cleanest class is gone, nothing in an asexual population can bring it back. To rebuild a genome cleaner than any now alive, you would need a mutation to reverse itself, which happens far too rarely to count on, or you would need to take the good parts of two different genomes and combine them into one, which is what sex does and what copying cannot. So the fewest-mutations number in the whole population, having lost its lowest class, steps up by one, and it can only ever step up, never down, because every route back down has been closed. Nobody selected for the mutations. No individual was punished for being clean; the cleanest were lost to the plain arithmetic of chance in a finite crowd, and the copying that carried the rest forward was faithful and efficient and asked for no mate. The load climbs one irreversible notch at a time, the best the population ever had recedes out of reach, and the only thing that could reassemble it, the mixing of two lineages into one, is exactly the thing a faithful copier never does.

## Arc

The bend is this: in a finite population that reproduces without recombination, the least-loaded class, the genomes carrying the fewest deleterious mutations, is lost by chance and cannot be reconstituted, so the minimum mutational load climbs irreversibly, one click at a time (Muller).
It runs on a cleanest class that is also the rarest.
Because mutations accumulate and the fewest-mutations individuals are the ones fewest have matched, the best class in the population is by its nature the smallest, so the leading edge of the whole population's fitness rests on its rarest members, the least-loaded and the least numerous being the same individuals (Haigh). The rarity is not a flaw but a fact of the distribution, and it is what makes the edge fragile.
And a small class is lost by chance.
Because the population is finite and which individuals reproduce is partly luck, a class small enough can simply fail to be copied in a given generation, dropped not by any disadvantage but by the sampling of a finite crowd, so the least-loaded class goes not because it was unfit but because it was few (Haigh). The loss is blind, a matter of who happened to leave offspring, and it falls on the best precisely because the best are fewest.
And the lost class cannot be remade.
Because reversing a mutation is vanishingly rare and copying never combines two genomes into one, an asexual population has no way to reassemble a genome cleaner than its current best, so once the least-loaded class is gone the floor beneath the load has risen and cannot be lowered, the one number that could only have held or climbed having climbed (Muller). The irreversibility is structural, built into inheritance without mixing, and it is what turns a loss into a ratcheting.
And the floor rises again, and again.
Because each new least-loaded class is again the rarest and again finite and again exposed to the same chance, the same loss recurs, the minimum load stepping up notch by notch with nothing to arrest it, a decline assembled entirely from faithful copying and blind luck (Muller; Haigh). In a small population the steps come fast and can accelerate toward collapse, the load mounting until the lineage cannot carry it (Lynch, Bürger, Butcher and Gabriel). The climb is one-way, and it feeds on the very finiteness that every real population has.
And only mixing brings it back.
Because recombination takes the good stretches of two mutated genomes and combines them into one that carries fewer mutations than either parent, sex can reconstitute the least-loaded class the ratchet destroyed, so recombination is the one force that halts and reverses the climb (Felsenstein). But sex is dear: it pays the two-fold cost of producing males who bear no young, it breaks up good combinations as readily as bad, and it demands a partner where copying demands none, so the remedy that alone can lower the floor is the one an efficient copier is always tempted to abandon (Maynard Smith). The cure exists and it is expensive, and dropping it is exactly what starts the climb.
None of it is a fix. The copier reproduces faithfully because faithful copying is what reproduction is, the cleanest are lost because chance in a finite crowd drops the rarest, and no individual is selected against for being clean, so the copying and the losing are each only what they are, and the rising floor emerges from the sum, no selection for decay and no agent of it required. Halt it, and the halt is dear: it means recombination, the mixing of lineages that alone can rebuild a clean genome, bought at the two-fold cost of sex and the breaking of good combinations and the need for a mate, and even then the mixing prevents the next click more than it undoes the last, since the mutations already fixed in the whole population are beyond any reassembly to reach. So the load climbs under faithful copying and blind chance, the best the population had recedes and does not return, and the one force that could bring it back is the costly mixing a copier does without, every clean class lost for good and the floor with no way back up.

## Triggers

**[The Copy](plot_the_copy.md)**
A generation reproduces. The Copier passes its genome to its offspring faithfully, mutations and all, adding whatever new ones arise and shedding none, since asexual copying transmits what it has and has no means to remove a mutation once carried. Closes when the load is handed on intact or heavier, never lighter, the lineage carrying its mutations forward because copying is a transmission and not a repair.

**[The Drop](plot_the_drop.md)**
Cue: the cleanest class is small. In a finite generation, by the luck of which individuals leave offspring, not one of the fewest-mutations class is copied forward, and it is gone. Closes when the least-loaded class is lost, not for any disadvantage but for its rarity, the best the population had removed by the blind sampling of a finite crowd.

**[The Rise](plot_the_rise.md)**
Cue: the least-loaded class is gone and cannot be remade. With no back-mutation to reverse the loss and no recombination to reassemble a clean genome, the fewest-mutations number in the population steps up by one. Closes when the floor beneath the load has risen and can only hold or climb from here, one irreversible notch added, the same exposure waiting for the class that is now the cleanest.

**[The Mending](plot_the_mending.md)**
Cue: two lineages could mix. The Recombiner takes the good stretches of two mutated genomes and combines them into one cleaner than either, reconstituting a least-loaded class the ratchet had destroyed, and pays for it in the cost of sex. Closes when recombination halts or reverses the climb at the price of a mate and a broken combination and the two-fold cost, or the mixing forgone and the floor left to rise.

## Stakes

Whether a lineage that copies itself faithfully holds its ground, or loses its best by chance and climbs a floor it cannot lower without the costly mixing it does without.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the rising floor, which the Copier is right to reproduce faithfully because faithful copying is reproduction, and chance is right to drop the rarest because that is what a finite draw does, and away from the recombination that alone could rebuild the clean class at the price of the cost of sex. The production cannot make the decline a selection for decay, because no individual is punished for being clean and the cleanest are lost to blind luck and the copying is faithful, so reproducing and losing and carrying the load forward are each only what they are. It can only show that a finite population reproducing without mixing loses its least-loaded class by chance and cannot rebuild it, so the minimum load tends to climb one irreversible notch at a time and in a small population can accelerate toward collapse, and that the one force which could lower the floor, the mixing of two lineages into a cleaner one, is the costly recombination a faithful copier abandons, so the load rises not by anyone's selection for it but by the ordinary faithful copying of a lineage that keeps its best only until chance happens to drop it.
