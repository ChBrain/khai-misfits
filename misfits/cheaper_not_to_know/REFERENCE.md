# Cheaper Not to Know: Reference

The research warrant for **Cheaper Not to Know** (play and company not yet staged;
this warrant is drafted first, to prove the science and the distinction before authoring).
The dynamic is the rational-ignorance account of the electorate: Downs's economic theory of the
near-zero return to becoming informed, with Somin's field evidence of its depth and its cost to
democratic competence, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the rational ignorance of the voter as a **negligible-return structure**: a citizen deciding
how much to learn before voting, whose single ballot has a vanishingly small probability of deciding
the election, so the expected benefit of becoming informed, the value of a better outcome multiplied by
the chance one's own vote brings it about, is near zero, while the cost of acquiring and understanding
political information is real, so the rational citizen invests little or nothing in political knowledge
and decides instead on cheap cues, and the electorate governs itself on cues rather than on the
information it could in principle have held. The governing law is not that voters are lazy or incurious,
but that **because a single vote almost never decides an election, the expected return to becoming
informed is near zero against a real cost, so staying ignorant is individually rational, and an
electorate of individually rational citizens decides on cues rather than knowledge, an uninformed
collective choice that no voter is at fault for**. The play models the documented mechanics: the
near-zero probability that a single vote is pivotal and the expected-value calculus that makes informing
a losing investment for the individual (Downs), the cheap cues, party, ideology, endorsement, that a
rational citizen decides on in place of costly knowledge (Downs), and the measured depth of political
ignorance across the electorate and its cost to the competence of democratic choice (Somin), as the
load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of a foolish or apathetic public.

It is distinct from the house's Nobody Reads It, where a buyer skips the fine print because reading it
costs more than the term is worth to the one contract, an informed-minority and search-cost mechanism
set in a market with a drafter and a product: there a market exists, a drafter writes the term, and a
larger informed readership would discipline it, here there is no market, no drafter, and no product, the
object is the electorate's own basis for democratic choice, and the driver is not the cost of reading
but the near-zero chance that the reader's vote decides anything, so that even a fully informed citizen's
ballot is as unlikely to be pivotal as an ignorant one and disciplines nothing. It is distinct from
Everybody's Business, the house's volunteer's dilemma, where a public good needs only one actor and each
holds back hoping another will step in, so a single volunteer would suffice and the failure is one of
diffusion: there one person's action would secure the good, here one person's becoming informed secures
nothing, because a single informed vote changes the outcome as rarely as a single ignorant one, so the
citizen withholds not in the hope that another will inform themselves but because informing oneself does
not, on its own, produce the good at all.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the voter
  as foolish, apathetic, or a bad citizen, because the source finding it stages is precisely that the
  ignorance is rational: with a single vote almost never decisive, the expected return to mastering the
  issues is near zero, and declining a real cost for a near-zero return is sound economics, not a
  character flaw (Downs). Deciding on party, ideology, or endorsement is not laziness but the reasonable
  use of a cheap cue in place of costly knowledge (Downs). The uninformed collective choice is authored
  by no one and emerges from the structure, a real cost of learning meeting a near-zero chance of being
  pivotal, so the misfit is that an electorate of citizens each rationally declining to inform themselves
  governs itself on cues, a public that could know and reasonably does not.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a costless correction. The one citizen who
  masters the issues, at real cost in time and effort, does not fix it, because their informed ballot is
  as unlikely to be pivotal as any other and changes no outcome, so their diligence is a private expense
  that secures no public good (Downs; Somin). Nor does exhorting the electorate to be informed clear it,
  since the exhortation asks each citizen to bear a real cost for a return their single vote cannot make
  positive, so the reckoning is that becoming informed is a genuine good the individual has no
  instrumental reason to buy, and no amount of civic duty changes the arithmetic of one vote among
  millions.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never
  claimed as the house's. The staging is the house's; the near-zero pivotality and the rational-ignorance
  calculus and the cue-voting shortcut are Downs's, and the measured depth of political ignorance and its
  cost to democratic competence are Somin's, and the condition the trap actually requires, a real cost of
  information meeting a near-zero chance of deciding the outcome, is carried as carefully as the finding
  itself. The scope is held to the decision of how much to learn before voting, the rational not-acquiring
  of information, and not stretched into the separate puzzles of why anyone votes at all, the turnout
  paradox, or of voting to express an identity rather than to affect an outcome, expressive voting, or of
  the biased processing of information one already holds, rational irrationality, which the misfit does not
  stage.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per element. The play and its company are not yet authored; these are the
proposed load-bearing pieces the warrant commits the staging to carry, one per vector, no filler.

- **The Vote**: carries the single ballot (Downs), one among millions, the unit whose weight in deciding
  the outcome is the whole of the calculus.
- **The Pivot**: carries the near-zero probability that the single vote is decisive (Downs), the
  vanishingly small chance of being the ballot that tips the election.
- **The Cost**: carries the real price of becoming informed (Downs), the time and effort of learning the
  candidates, the records, the measures, a cost that is positive and felt.
- **The Return**: carries the expected benefit of informing (Downs), the value of a better outcome
  multiplied by the near-zero chance one's own vote brings it about, so small it never covers the cost.
- **The Cue**: carries the cheap shortcut decided on instead (Downs), the party label, the ideology, the
  endorsement, a low-cost proxy standing in for the knowledge not bought.
- **The Verdict**: carries the collective outcome (Somin), an electorate deciding on cues rather than
  knowledge, an uninformed democratic choice that is the sum of many rational refusals and the fault of
  none.
