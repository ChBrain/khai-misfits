# Play: Cheaper Not to Know

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

Cheaper Not to Know.
A citizen sits down to decide how they will vote, and before that, a prior question: how much should they learn first. To vote well they would need to know the candidates' records, read the ballot measures, follow what the offices actually do, weigh the claims against the evidence, and this is real work, hours of it, genuinely costly in a busy life. Against that cost sits the benefit, and the benefit is where the trap opens. The benefit of being informed is only realized if their better-informed vote changes the outcome, and in an election of millions the chance that any single vote decides anything at all is vanishingly small, one in millions, near enough to zero that it does not move the calculation. So the sum is plain and it is not a mistake: a real cost to learn, a near-zero chance the learning matters, and the rational thing to do with a real cost that buys a near-zero return is not to pay it.
So the citizen does not master the issues, and this is not laziness, it is arithmetic. They are not incurious, they are not bad, they have simply run, without writing it down, the same calculation any reasonable person runs on any costly thing that returns almost nothing, and come to the answer it gives. And having decided not to know, they still must vote, so they vote on what is cheap to hand: the party, the name they recognize, the endorsement of someone they trust, the ideology that roughly sorts their side from the other. These cues are not folly either. They are the reasonable shortcuts of a person who has correctly judged that the deep knowledge is not worth its price, standing in for the information that was rationally never bought.
And here is the turn that makes it a trap and not merely a private economy. Every citizen faces the same arithmetic, so nearly every one makes the same rational choice, and the electorate that results is one that decides on cues rather than on knowledge, not because its members are foolish but because each of them is sensible, and the sum of a hundred million sensible refusals to study is a public that governs itself on party and name and slogan. The knowledge that would have made the choice a good one is a genuine good, and it is precisely the good that no single member of the public has any reason to buy, because no single member's purchase would change the result. What is individually rational is collectively a democracy deciding half-blind, and no one chose that, and no one is at fault for it.
And the diligent do not save it. The rare citizen who does the whole homework, who reads every measure and checks every record at real cost to their evenings, casts in the end one vote, exactly as pivotal as the most ignorant one, which is to say almost not at all, so their diligence buys them the private satisfaction of knowing and buys the outcome nothing. Nor does exhortation save it: telling the electorate to inform itself, running the civic campaigns, printing the voter guides, asks each citizen to pay a real cost for a return their one vote cannot make positive, so the earnest call to be informed runs headlong into the same arithmetic it means to overcome. The knowing is worth having and no one is wrong to skip it.
And none of it is a failure of character. The citizen who stays ignorant is being rational, the citizen who votes on party is using a sound shortcut, the candidate who campaigns on cues is meeting the electorate where the arithmetic has put it, and the diligent one who changes nothing did nothing wrong by trying. The half-blind verdict emerges from the structure, a real cost of knowing set against a near-zero chance of its mattering, summed across everyone at once, so the trap is that a public made entirely of reasonable people, each correctly judging that the knowledge is not worth its price to them, governs itself on cues, and the one thing that would fix it, an informed electorate, is the one thing no member of it has a reason to buy.

## Arc

The bend is this: because a single vote almost never decides an election, the expected return to becoming informed is near zero against a real cost, so staying ignorant is individually rational, and an electorate of individually rational citizens decides on cues rather than knowledge, an uninformed collective choice at no voter's fault.
It runs on a near-zero chance of mattering.
To vote well a citizen must learn the records, the measures, the offices, and this costs real time and effort. The benefit of that learning is realized only if the better-informed vote changes the outcome, and in a mass election the probability that a single vote is decisive is vanishingly small (Downs), so the expected return to informing, the value of a better result times the near-zero chance one's own vote brings it about, is near zero.
And so ignorance is rational. A real cost that buys a near-zero return is a cost a reasonable person does not pay, so the citizen rationally invests little or nothing in political knowledge, not from apathy but from sound economics (Downs). This is the rational ignorance the whole structure turns on: not a failure to be able to know, but a correct judgment that the knowing is not worth its price.
And the vote still gets cast, on cues. Having declined the costly knowledge, the citizen decides on what is cheap: party, ideology, name, endorsement, low-cost proxies that roughly sort the choice (Downs). The cue is not folly but the reasonable shortcut of someone who has priced the deep knowledge correctly and found it too dear.
And the sum is a half-blind electorate. Every citizen faces the same arithmetic and nearly every one makes the same rational choice, so the electorate decides on cues rather than knowledge, and the measured depth of that ignorance and its cost to the competence of democratic choice is real (Somin). The good that would have made the choice sound, an informed public, is exactly the good no single member has reason to buy, because no single purchase would change the result.
None of it is apathy or folly. Declining a costly investment that returns almost nothing is rational, voting on a sound cue is reasonable, and the diligent citizen who masters it all casts one vote as unpivotal as any other and changes no outcome: the half-blind verdict emerges from the structure, a real cost of knowing meeting a near-zero chance of mattering, summed across everyone, authored by no one.
Make knowing cheaper, or make one vote matter more, and the calculation could change: shorten the ballot, simplify the offices, sharpen the cues so they carry more true signal, raise the stakes or the closeness so the chance of deciding is felt. But each of these is its own costly reform against the same arithmetic, none abolishes that one vote among millions almost never decides, and the diligent citizen still changes nothing and the exhortation still asks a real cost for a return one vote cannot make positive, so the escape is bought only by lowering the price of knowing or raising the weight of the single vote, never by faulting a public that priced the knowledge correctly and reasonably declined it.

## Stakes

Whether a democracy can choose well, when knowing enough to choose well costs each citizen real effort and returns, to each, almost nothing.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the cheap cue that a reasonable person votes on and away from the costly knowledge that no reasonable person buys, because the benefit of that knowledge is gated behind a chance of mattering that is, for any single vote, vanishingly small. The production cannot make the knowing pay, because its return is the value of a better outcome times the near-zero chance one's own vote brings it about, and near-zero times anything is near-zero, so the cost stays real and the return stays negligible however worthy the knowledge. It can only show that no citizen is foolish and none apathetic, that declining a costly investment that returns almost nothing is rational and voting on a sound cue is reasonable, and that the one move which would make the choice sound, an informed electorate, is exactly the good no member of it has an instrumental reason to buy, because no single purchase would change the result, so the diligence of the few changes nothing and the verdict is half-blind and no one, anywhere in it, is at fault.
