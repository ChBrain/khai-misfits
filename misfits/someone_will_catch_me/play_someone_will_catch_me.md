# Play: Someone Will Catch Me

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

Someone Will Catch Me.
A person is in genuine trouble, and a good soul steps in to help, because no decent person can stand by and watch another suffer when they have the means to relieve it. The help is right: the need is real, the rescue lands, and the sufferer is spared. The trouble is that the good soul cannot commit to not helping. Faced with someone actually in need, they will always help, and everyone knows it, so the recipient, who is no fool, learns that the catch is certain and builds less of their own footing than they would if the ground were the only floor beneath them. Why lay by savings, why brace for the storm, why keep the guard up, when the rescue is sure to come? The under-provision is not fraud or sloth: it is the sound response to a reliable guarantee, exactly what a rational person does when a safety net hangs where they were going to stand. And the Samaritan would dearly like to say next time you are on your own, but they cannot make the threat stick, because when next time comes and the need is real again, they will help again, and both sides know it. So the help arrives, the footing goes unbuilt, the need recurs, and the rescue that answered it was the thing that shaped it. Nobody is a villain. The Samaritan's compassion is genuine and each rescue is correct in the moment it is given, and the recipient's reliance is the rational reading of a guarantee they did not ask for and cannot un-know. The kindness that would never let them fall is the kindness under which they never learn to stand, the help manufacturing, out of pure good will, the dependence it exists to relieve.

## Arc

The bend is this: a benefactor who cannot credibly commit to withholding aid induces the recipient to provide less for themselves, so a guaranteed rescue manufactures the very dependence it means to relieve, each act of help right in the moment and the pattern a trap (Buchanan).
It runs on a compassion that cannot commit.
Because a good person faced with real suffering will relieve it, the help is certain ex post, and a certain help cannot be conditioned on the recipient's effort, since the threat to withhold is not one the benefactor will keep when the moment comes (Buchanan; Kydland and Prescott). The compassion is genuine, and its very reliability is what disarms it.
And a certain rescue is rationally leaned on.
Because the recipient knows the catch will come, they build less of their own footing than they would if they had to bear the fall, under-saving, under-bracing, under-guarding, not from vice but from the plain sense of not provisioning against a risk someone else has agreed to carry (Kornai; Bruce and Waldman). The reliance is the correct response, and it is the response that makes the need recur.
And the threat to withhold is an empty one.
Because the benefactor will help when the time comes whatever they said beforehand, their warning that next time will be different carries no weight, and the recipient, reading the ex-post incentive correctly, discounts it entirely, so the words change nothing while the deeds stay soft (Kydland and Prescott; Schelling). The threat is understood by both sides to be a bluff, so it does not bind.
And the rescue shapes the need it answers.
Because the help is certain and the footing therefore unbuilt, the recipient arrives at the next brink less prepared than they would have been unaided, so the rescue that relieves the need is the same reliability that produced it, the compassion authoring, over cycles, the dependence it keeps relieving (Buchanan). The need is real each time, and each time it is partly the making of the help.
None of it is a fault. The Samaritan relieving present suffering they have the means to end, the recipient provisioning rationally against a rescue they can count on, the empty threat that neither side can make binding: each is acting soundly, and the manufactured dependence emerges from the sum, no cruelty and no fraud required. Commit credibly to withholding, let the recipient bear a fall they could have been spared, and the incentive to build their own footing returns (the binding vow, the hard no): but that means binding yourself against your own compassion and watching someone suffer whom you could relieve, inflicting a real and present harm to teach a lesson that may not take, so the one move that frees the recipient to stand is a cruelty the Samaritan must choose against their own heart, and the reckoning is a kindness that keeps its object dependent, or a hardness that must let them fall.

**[The Cry for Help](plot_the_cry_for_help.md)**

Closes when the help is delivered and the pattern set, the rescue certain and its ex-post correctness beyond dispute.

**[The Lean](plot_the_lean.md)**
Cue: the recipient knows the catch is sure. Reading a guarantee they can count on, they build less of their own footing, under-saving and under-bracing against a risk another has agreed to carry. Closes when the provision is quietly withheld, the reliance the sound response to a reliable rescue and the making of the next need.

**[The Warning](plot_the_warning.md)**
Cue: the Samaritan resolves that next time will be different. They warn the recipient that the help will not come, and the warning changes nothing, because both sides know that when the need is real again the hand will open again. Closes when the threat is seen through and discounted, the words firm and the deeds still soft.

**[The Hard No](plot_the_hard_no.md)**
Cue: someone would break the cycle. The Hard Hand binds themselves to withhold and lets the recipient meet the brink alone, bearing the sight of a suffering they could relieve. Closes when the rescue is refused at the cost of the recipient's fall and the Samaritan's heart, or the vow breaks and the hand opens once more.

## Stakes

Whether help can be given without manufacturing the need for it, or a certain rescue must always breed the reliance that makes it certain to be needed again.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the manufactured dependence, which the Samaritan is right to relieve the suffering in front of them and the recipient right to lean on a rescue they can count on and neither able to make the threat to withhold believed, and away from the hard no that would free the recipient to stand at the cost of a fall. The production cannot make the dependence a fault, because each rescue is correct in the moment and the recipient's under-provision is the rational reading of a real guarantee, so stepping in to end present suffering and provisioning against a risk another carries are each only the sensible act. It can only show that a compassion which cannot commit to refusing is leaned on until it must carry what it meant only to catch, that the threat to withhold is a bluff both sides see through, and that the one move which frees the recipient, a credible and binding no, means inflicting a present harm on the very person it would help and binding the Samaritan against their own good heart, so the dependence grows not by anyone's vice but by the ordinary good will of a helper who cannot stand to watch and the ordinary good sense of a recipient who need not.
