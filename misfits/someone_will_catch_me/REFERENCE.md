# Someone Will Catch Me: Reference

The research warrant for [Someone Will Catch Me](play_someone_will_catch_me.md).
The dynamic is the Samaritan's Dilemma: Buchanan's account of the utility-maximising Samaritan whose ex-post help
induces the recipient's under-provision, Kydland and Prescott's time inconsistency and Schelling's credible commitment
behind the empty threat, Kornai's soft budget constraint and Bruce and Waldman's formalisation of the recipient's
strategic response, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the Samaritan's Dilemma as a **commitment-failure-manufactures-dependence structure**: a benefactor who cannot
credibly commit to withholding aid makes the rescue certain, and a rescue that is certain is rationally leaned on, so
the recipient builds less of their own footing than they would unaided, and the reliability of the help manufactures the
dependence it means to relieve. The play models the documented mechanics, the ex-post rescue that is right in the
moment, the recipient's rational under-provision against a covered risk, the threat to withhold that neither side can
make binding, and the precommitment that alone restores the incentive, as the load-bearing vectors of the structure,
not as a tale of a lazy dependent or a foolish giver. The governing finding of the source is the play's governing law:
the Samaritan's own utility-maximising choice to relieve present suffering, taken again at each brink, is precisely what
leads the recipient to provide less for themselves, so charity offered without the ability to commit to conditions
breeds the need it answers, a dependence authored by good will and correct in each of its parts.

It is distinct from the house's Moral Hazard, where a party takes hidden, unobserved risk because a cost has been shifted
onto a third party such as an insurer: there the action is concealed and the cost externalised, while here the
recipient's under-provision is fully observed, the Samaritan can see it plainly, and no cost is transferred to anyone
else, the engine being the helper's own inability to commit to withholding rather than concealment or a shifted burden.
It is distinct from Time Inconsistency, whose staged form is a single planner's own optimal plan going stale, corrected
by tying one's hands: the Samaritan's Dilemma is a time inconsistency, but the two-sided altruistic case, where it is
compassion that cannot commit and a second party rationally under-provides in anticipation, so the trap lives in the
interaction of the soft heart and the recipient's best response, not in one actor's inconsistency alone. And it is
distinct from Shifting the Burden, where a capability that existed atrophies from disuse while a symptomatic fix is
leaned on: here nothing withers, the footing is never built in the first place, a rational ex-ante under-investment
against an assured rescue, and the driver is the giver's commitment problem, not the erosion of a used capacity.

## Origin

The phenomenon in theory, and the research spine beneath it.

| **James M. Buchanan**                | "The Samaritan's Dilemma" (in _Altruism, Morality, and Economic Theory_, ed. Edmund Phelps, 1975)         | **The naming and the dilemma**: the utility-maximising Samaritan who relieves present suffering ex post thereby induces the recipient to under-provide for themselves, so the aid manufactures the dependence, a time-inconsistent compassion. | **Thomas Schelling**                 | "The Strategy of Conflict" (1960)                                                                         | **The credible commitment**: a threat moves behaviour only if it will be kept, and credibility often requires binding away one's own freedom to be reasonable in the moment, the precommitment that makes a refusal real. ## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the Samaritan as a fool or
  the recipient as a shirker, because the source finding is precisely that no vice is needed: each rescue is correct in
  the moment, a real suffering really relieved, and the compassion that gives it is genuine (Buchanan). The recipient's
  under-provision is not fraud or sloth but the rational response to a rescue they can count on, the sound economy of not
  building a footing against a fall another has agreed to break (Kornai; Bruce and Waldman), and the threat to withhold
  goes unbelieved not from the recipient's stubbornness but from a correct reading of an incentive the helper cannot
  change (Kydland and Prescott). So the misfit is that a goodness reliable enough to be counted on manufactures the
  dependence it relieves, no cruelty and no con required, the kindness doing right in each moment and wrong across them,
  and the helper who cannot stand by is as reasonable as the recipient who need not stand alone.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a dependence dissolved by firmer giving alone. The one move
  that frees the recipient is a credible, binding refusal, a precommitment against one's own compassion so the threat to
  withhold will hold (Schelling), but that means letting a present, certain, relievable suffering stand, inflicting a
  real harm now for a capacity that is future and unprovable, and warring against a virtue one is right to keep. The
  Samaritan who could break the cycle is the one who would have to watch a loved one fall and be called cruel for it,
  with the standing they buy invisible and the fall they allow plain, so the reckoning is a kindness that keeps its
  object dependent, or a hardness that must let them suffer, not a way to help that does not teach reliance nor a
  firmness that costs the helper nothing.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never claimed as the
  house's, and its reach is not overstated. The staging is the house's; the dilemma of the utility-maximising Samaritan
  is Buchanan's, the time inconsistency that empties the threat is Kydland and Prescott's, the credible-commitment
  remedy is Schelling's, the soft budget constraint that shows the same engine among firms and states is Kornai's, and
  the formalisation of the recipient's strategic under-provision is Bruce and Waldman's, each carried as carefully as the
  finding itself: the misfit stages a compassion that cannot commit and credits that no one was a villain and the
  dependence is the good heart's own making.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per element.

- **[The Soft Heart](piece_the_soft_heart.md)**: carries the phenomenon whole (Buchanan), the compassion that cannot
  commit to refusing, so its help is certain, its threat is empty, and its object is left dependent, the virtue whose
  reliability is the trap.
- **[The Rescue](piece_the_rescue.md)**: carries the ex-post aid that is right each time (Buchanan), the certain catch
  whose very correctness in the moment is what the recipient plans around.
- **[The Empty Threat](piece_the_empty_threat.md)**: carries the commitment problem (Kydland and Prescott), the warning
  to withhold that both sides price as a bluff because the helper will not keep it when the need is real.
- **[The Unbuilt](piece_the_unbuilt.md)**: carries the recipient's rational under-provision (Kornai; Bruce and Waldman),
  the footing foregone against an assured rescue, a strength never built rather than a capacity worn away.
