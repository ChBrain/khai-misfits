# First Do Harm: Reference

The research warrant for [First Do Harm](play_first_do_harm.md).
The dynamic is iatrogenesis: Illich's clinical, social, and cultural iatrogenesis and his medical nemesis, and
Rochon and Gurwitz's prescribing cascade, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging iatrogenesis as a **cure-inflicts-harm structure**: a genuine remedy, applied in good faith to a real
complaint and truly working on it, reaches past the presenting condition and inflicts a new and separate harm on a
sound part the complaint had left whole, and because that wound presents as its own fresh condition and is never
traced to the cure, the remedy is never blamed and is given again, the interventions chaining under a standing
demand that something be done. The play models the documented mechanics, the treatment that genuinely works and by
the same power reaches past its target, the harm that is a different pathology in a different place rather than the
complaint worsened, the missing causal trace by which the wound wears a fresh face and the cure keeps its alibi,
the cascade of interventions each treating the last one's harm, the interventionist pressure that makes
forbearance read as negligence, and the costly restraint that alone breaks the chain, as the load-bearing vectors
of the structure, not as a tale of quackery or of a cure that fails. The governing finding of the source is the
play's governing law: the healing enterprise itself produces injury, so a real remedy competently given can harm
what it was not aimed at, and the harms of the cures, compounded and untraced, can grow past the conditions they
were brought to treat.

It is distinct from the house's Fixes that Fail, and the two are close enough that the cut must be exact: there the
engine is a fix that eases a symptom but sets off delayed consequences that worsen the very problem it treated, a
balancing loop whose side effect loops back onto the original variable, so the fix ultimately fails at its own
target; here the remedy may fully succeed at its target and still inflict a separate harm elsewhere, a new
pathology in a new place that the original condition never carried, so the failure is not of the cure but the
creation of a distinct wound beside it. Fixes that Fail worsens the same problem; First Do Harm makes a different
one. It is distinct from The Cobra Effect, where a perverse incentive breeds more of the very thing it was meant
to reduce: here there is no incentive and nothing is bred, only a treatment causing collateral injury. It is
distinct from Shifting the Burden, where reliance on a symptomatic fix lets the fundamental capacity atrophy from
disuse: the social form of iatrogenesis actively disables the capacity to cope rather than merely letting it waste,
an injury done and not a muscle unused. And it is distinct from The Balloon Effect, where one problem is displaced
intact to a new location: here the original complaint is genuinely eased, and what appears elsewhere is a new harm
the cure created, not the old problem relocated.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the healer as a
  quack or the petitioner as reckless, because the source finding is precisely that the harm comes of genuine
  healing: the complaint is real, the remedy works, the healer means only to help, and the demand that something be
  done is fair on its face. The wound is authored by no ill will and emerges from a true cure reaching past a real
  complaint, so the misfit is that a remedy powerful enough to treat the condition is powerful enough to injure
  what the condition had spared, and a harm hidden inside a good treatment is one no one will trace to it.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a costless correction. The one move that keeps the first
  rule is to stay the hand where the cure would cost more than the complaint, trace the wound to the remedy that
  made it, and tolerate the condition rather than trade it for a new harm, and it means allowing a visible
  complaint to stand rather than causing a hidden one, being read as negligent for the harm not caused while the
  treating healer is thanked for the harm they did, and proving a wound to a cure that has an alibi. The reckoning
  is the first rule kept only by paying, continuously, the price of looking like you failed to act, not a free
  correction.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never claimed as
  the house's. The staging is the house's; the iatrogenesis frame, its clinical, social, and cultural levels, and
  medical nemesis are Illich's, and the prescribing cascade is Rochon and Gurwitz's. The honest boundary, that the
  remedy genuinely works and the complaint is real, so the harm is the shadow of a true cure and not the failure
  of a false one, is carried as carefully as the finding itself, and the medical source is credited while the
  structure is staged to run wherever a genuine cure can wound what it was not aimed at.
