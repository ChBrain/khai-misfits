# Play: First Do Harm

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

First Do Harm.
There is a complaint, and it is real: a condition that presents, a problem that hurts, something wrong that asks to be put right. And there is a remedy, and it is genuine: a treatment that works on the complaint, applied by a competent healer in good faith, meaning nothing but to help. This is not a story of quackery or of a cure that fails. The cure works. It treats the thing it was for. It is only that the treatment, doing exactly what it does, reaches past the complaint and harms something the complaint had left whole: a different tissue, a separate function, a healthy part that the original condition never touched, wounded now by the very intervention that eased the presenting problem. The drug that settles the heart unsettles the kidney. The reorganisation that fixes the reporting lines severs the tacit knowledge that was carried in the old ones. The control that stops the fraud kills the initiative the fraud was a rounding error against. The harm is not the complaint worsened, it is a new complaint, in a new place, that was not there before the cure. And here is the turn that makes it a trap and not a mistake: the new wound presents as its own condition, a fresh complaint arriving after the treatment and looking nothing like it, so no one traces it back to the remedy. The remedy has an alibi. It was applied to the heart, and it is the kidney that now fails, and the kidney's failure is treated as a kidney problem, with a second remedy, which reaches past the kidney and harms a third thing, and so the interventions chain, each one genuine, each one treating the wound the last one made, none of them ever blamed because the harm never looks like the hand that caused it. And behind all of it stands a pressure that makes stopping unthinkable: something must be done. A complaint that presents demands a response, and the healer who stays their hand, who says this condition is better tolerated than treated, that the cure would cost more than the disease, is not read as wise but as negligent, as having failed to act, because a harm you allow is visible and a harm you cause by treating is hidden inside a treatment that meant well. So the hand is not stayed. The remedy is applied, and the wound is made, and the wound is treated, and the system fills with harms that no single intervention will own, each healer having done the competent, caring, demanded thing. Nobody meant harm. The complaint was real, the remedy worked, the healer acted in good faith and under a genuine call to act, and the wound was the shadow of a true cure and not the failure of a false one. It is only that a remedy real enough to treat the complaint is a remedy powerful enough to harm what the complaint had spared, and a harm hidden inside a good treatment is a harm no one will trace to it, so the cure is given again, and the first rule, do no harm, is broken first and always by the act of curing.

## Arc

The bend is this: a genuine remedy applied in good faith to a real complaint inflicts a new and separate harm the condition never carried, and because the harm never looks like the cure that caused it, the cure is never blamed and is given again.
It runs on a remedy that truly works.
The treatment is not a failure or a fraud: it works on the complaint it was brought for, applied competently by a healer meaning only to help (Illich, on iatrogenesis, the injury done by the healing itself). So the cure is real, and this is what makes it a trap rather than a fault, because there is nothing wrong with the remedy as a treatment of the thing it treats: the harm is the shadow of a genuine cure, not the mark of a false one.
And the harm is new and separate, not the complaint worsened. The remedy, doing exactly what it does, reaches past the presenting condition and injures something it had left whole, a different tissue, a separate function, a healthy part the original problem never touched, so the damage is a fresh wound in a new place and not the old problem returning (Illich, on clinical iatrogenesis, disease produced by medical intervention). The cure eases the complaint and wounds the sound.
And the wound has an alibi. Because the new harm presents as its own condition, arriving after the treatment and looking nothing like it, no one traces it to the remedy, so the cure is exonerated and the wound is taken for a fresh complaint in its own right (Rochon and Gurwitz, on the prescribing cascade, each drug's effect treated as a new disease). The remedy is never in the dock, because the harm never wears its face.
And the interventions chain. The wound, taken for a new complaint, is treated with a second remedy, which reaches past it and harms a third thing, which is treated in turn, so the cures cascade, each genuine, each treating the harm the last one made, the system filling with injuries no single intervention will own (the iatrogenic cascade, harm compounding through good treatment). Each link is competent and caring, and the chain is made of them.
And a demand to act keeps the hand from staying. Behind it all stands the pressure that something must be done: a complaint that presents demands a response, and the healer who forbears, who judges the condition better tolerated than treated, is read not as wise but as negligent, because a harm allowed is visible and a harm caused by a well-meant cure is hidden (Illich, on social and cultural iatrogenesis, the expropriation of the capacity to suffer and cope). Restraint looks like failure, so the remedy is given.
None of it is malice or quackery. The complaint was real, the remedy worked, the healer acted in good faith and under a genuine call to act, and the wound was the shadow of a true cure. What makes the remedy worth giving, that it is real enough to treat the complaint, is exactly what makes it powerful enough to harm what the complaint had spared, and a harm hidden in a good treatment is one no one will trace to it.
Stay the hand where the cure would cost more than the complaint, trace the wound back to the remedy that made it, and tolerate the condition rather than trade it for a new harm, and the cascade can be stopped: but this means allowing a visible complaint to stand rather than causing a hidden one, being read as negligent for the harm you did not cause while the healer who treats is thanked for the harm they did, and proving a wound to a cure that has an alibi, against the demand that something be done, so the first rule is kept only by paying, continuously, the price of looking like you failed to act.

## Triggers

**[The Presenting](plot_the_presenting.md)**
A real complaint presents and demands a response. The Petitioner calls for action, so that to treat is expected and to forbear reads as negligence, and the Healer is set to apply the remedy the condition calls for. Closes when the complaint is on the chart and the demand to act stands, restraint already looking like failure and the cure already the responsible move.

**[The Cure](plot_the_cure.md)**
Cue: the remedy is applied to the complaint. The Healer treats the presenting condition competently and in good faith, and the remedy works, easing the thing it was brought for. Closes when the complaint is genuinely treated and the cure has done exactly what it does, sound as a treatment of the problem it was for, and reaching, unseen, past it.

**[The Harm](plot_the_harm.md)**
Cue: the remedy reaches past the complaint. The cure injures something the condition had left whole, a separate function or a healthy part, so a new wound is made in a new place that was not there before the treatment. Closes when the cure has eased the complaint and wounded the sound, the harm real and separate and the intent to heal intact.

**[The Spiral](plot_the_spiral.md)**
Cue: the new wound presents as its own condition. Because it arrives after the cure and looks nothing like it, the Healer treats it as a fresh complaint with a second remedy, which reaches past it and harms a third thing, the remedy never traced and never blamed. Closes when the interventions have chained, each genuine and each treating the last one's wound, the system filling with harms no single cure will own.

**[The Stayed Hand](plot_the_stayed_hand.md)**
Cue: someone would forbear. The Steward would judge the condition better tolerated than treated, trace the wound to the remedy, and let the complaint stand rather than trade it for a new harm, against the demand that something be done. Closes when the hand is stayed at the price of looking negligent and the cascade is broken ... or restraint is read as failure to act, the cure is given, and the wound is made.

## Stakes

Whether a real complaint can be left untreated, when the remedy works and the demand to act is genuine and the harm the cure does is hidden inside it.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the remedy applied, which the healer is right to give and the complaint calls for, and away from the hand stayed, which would allow a visible condition to stand rather than cause a hidden harm and be read as negligence for it. The production cannot make the cure spare what it treats, because a remedy real enough to ease the complaint is powerful enough to wound what the complaint had left whole, and the wound it makes is hidden inside a good treatment and never traced to it, so the harm is the shadow of a true cure and not the failure of a false one: no one means it and the complaint was real and the remedy worked. It can only show that the healer acted in good faith and under a genuine call to act, that the wound wore a fresh face and the cure kept its alibi, and that the one move that keeps the first rule, staying the hand where the cure costs more than the complaint and tracing the wound to the remedy and tolerating the condition, must be made by being read as negligent for the harm not caused while the treating healer is thanked for the harm they did, so the intent is to heal and the remedy is real and the rule, do no harm, is broken first and always by the curing.
