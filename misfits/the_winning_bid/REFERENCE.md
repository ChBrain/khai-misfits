# The Winning Bid: Reference

The research warrant for [The Winning Bid](play_the_winning_bid.md). The title names the
outcome; the play runs on the winner's curse and common-value auction theory, from the
original oil-lease observation through the experimental and theoretical accounts, all cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the winner's curse as a **systemic system**: when many bidders compete for a prize of
one common but unknown value, each on an honest but noisy estimate, the auction awards the prize
to the highest bid, which is the highest estimate, which is systematically above the truth, so
the winner overpays not by error but by being selected for optimism. The play models the
documented mechanics, the prize of common value, the dispersed estimates, the bids near them,
the selection of the maximum, the overpayment revealed, and the bid shading that alone corrects
it, as the load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of foolish bidders. The
governing finding of the source literature is the play's governing law: to win a common-value
auction is to learn your estimate was the highest in the field, which is bad news about your
estimate, so a rational bidder must bid as if winning already told them they were too high.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast any bidder
  as reckless or the winner as a fool, because the source finding is precisely that each estimate is honest
  and each bid is reasonable near it: the winner is not the greediest but the most optimistic, and optimism
  is only revealed as error by the winning. The misfit is that the auction selects the top of the spread,
  and the top of the spread is where the overestimate lives.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a clever bidder simply beating the field. The one
  move that works is a retreat, shading the bid below the honest estimate or walking away, which means winning
  less often and looking timid in a room that came to win. The curse is not beaten by a bolder bid; it is
  priced, at the cost of the prizes not taken.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never
  claimed as the house's. The staging is the house's; the observation, the auction theory, and the behavioural
  evidence are theirs, and the condition the winner's curse actually requires, a common value and dispersed
  estimates, is carried as carefully as the finding itself.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per element.

- **[The Prize](piece_the_prize.md)**: carries the item of common but unknown value (Capen, Clapp & Campbell),
  the thing worth the same to everyone and known to none.
- **[The Estimate](piece_the_estimate.md)**: carries the honest noisy signal (Wilson), each bidder's guess
  scattered around the truth.
- **[The Spread](piece_the_spread.md)**: carries the dispersion of estimates (Milgrom & Weber), the field of
  guesses whose top the auction will reward.
- **[The Bid](piece_the_bid.md)**: carries the offer near the estimate (Thaler), the number a bidder is willing
  to pay on their own read.
- **[The Selection](piece_the_selection.md)**: carries adverse selection by the auction (Bazerman & Samuelson),
  the mechanism handing the prize to the maximum estimate.
- **[The Overpay](piece_the_overpay.md)**: carries the realized overpayment (Capen, Clapp & Campbell), the price
  above the value that winning locked in.
- **[The Shade](piece_the_shade.md)**: carries the equilibrium correction (Wilson; Milgrom & Weber), bidding
  below the estimate to account for winning being bad news.
- **[The Curse](piece_the_curse.md)**: carries the phenomenon whole (Capen, Clapp & Campbell; Thaler), winning as
  adverse selection, and its remedy in shading.
