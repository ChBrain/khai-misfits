# Play: The Iron Triangle

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

The Iron Triangle.
Three corners, and each holds something the other two cannot do without. The committee writes the law and the budget, and wants, above all, to be re-elected. The agency runs the programme, and wants its funding kept and its mission defended. The lobby speaks for the industry, and wants the policy to go its way. And each has exactly what the others need: the lobby has campaign money and organised votes for the committee, and a loyal clientele that will testify for the agency; the committee has appropriations and friendly oversight for the agency, and favourable legislation for the lobby; the agency has contracts and rulings and programmes for the lobby, and benefits to steer into the committee's district. So the three settle into a trade, each supplying the other two, and the trade closes into a ring: the lobby funds the committee that funds the agency that favours the lobby, round and round, and the ring governs its slice of policy in the shared interest of the three who sit at its corners. And nothing in it need be corrupt. The legislator is serving constituents and seeking re-election, which is the job. The administrator is defending a programme they believe in and the budget it needs, which is the job. The lobbyist is advocating for members who pay them to, which is the job. Each corner is doing exactly what its role rightly asks, and the sum of three roles played well is a closed circuit that runs the domain for its insiders and holds against anyone outside it: the president who would cut the programme, the rival interest, the general public who pays for it all and has no seat at any corner. And it is iron because it is a triangle and not a line: a single captured agency a president can lean on, but a committee, an agency, and a lobby braced against each other, each defending the ring that feeds it, is a structure that outlasts elections and administrations, because to break it you must overpower all three at once, and all three need it to stand.

## Arc

The bend is this: three actors who each control a resource the other two need settle into a closed, mutually reinforcing exchange that governs their policy domain in their shared interest, stable because each corner rationally supplies the other two, so a ring of legitimate roles runs its slice of government for the insiders and shuts the outside out.
It runs on three resources, each held by one corner and needed by the others.
A legislative committee, an executive agency, and an organised interest each command something the other two require: the committee, authorisation and appropriation and oversight; the agency, programmes and rulings and implementation; the interest, electoral support and organised testimony and a mobilised clientele (Cater; Freeman; Ripley and Franklin). None can get what it wants from within itself, and each can get it from the other two, so the resources make a market.
And the market closes into a ring. Each corner supplies the two it needs to keep supplying it: the lobby funds the committee and backs the agency, the committee funds the agency and legislates for the lobby, the agency serves the lobby and steers benefits to the committee, so the three exchanges lock into a closed circuit in which every corner both gives and receives, and the giving is what keeps the receiving coming (Gordon Adams). The ring is self-reinforcing, because each corner's payoff depends on the other two staying in it.
And the closure holds the domain. Sitting astride the committee's gate to legislation, the agency's control of implementation, and the lobby's command of the affected public, the ring governs its policy slice from the inside and shuts out the president, the rival interest, and the general public who fund it and hold no corner (Lowi). What reads as three constituencies well served is a subsystem that has captured its own domain.
And the triangle is iron because it is three-cornered. A single agency captured by a single interest can be leaned on by a president or a court; but three actors braced against each other, each defending the ring that feeds it and each holding a resource the reformer needs, make a structure that survives elections and administrations, because breaking it means overpowering all three at once, and all three are committed to its standing.
None of it is conspiracy or corruption. The legislator serving the district and seeking re-election, the administrator defending a programme and its budget, the lobbyist advocating for paying members: each is playing a legitimate role exactly as the role is meant to be played, and the ring emerges from the sum of three roles played well, no one needing to intend a closed circuit for one to form. What is meant to be responsive representation becomes a self-dealing ring.
Open the ring to the outside, subject the domain to the president and the budget office and the rival interests and the press, so the three corners must answer beyond each other, and the circuit can be broken: but opening it means overpowering three braced actors at once, each defending the ring that feeds it and each holding a resource the reformer needs, spending real political capital against an entrenched, information-rich, mutually supporting alliance, and risking, in breaking the ring, the genuine expertise and constituency service tangled up in it, so the escape is bought by beating all three corners together, against every incentive each has to hold, and can seldom be held open for long.

## Triggers

**[The Constituencies](plot_the_constituencies.md)**
Three corners each set to a legitimate goal. The Chairman wants re-election, the Administrator wants the programme's budget and mission, the Lobbyist wants favourable policy, and each holds a resource the other two need. Closes when the three are in place, each pursuing its own mandate and each depending on what the others control.

**[The Exchange](plot_the_exchange.md)**
Cue: each corner has what the others lack. The Lobbyist supplies the Chairman with support and the Administrator with backing, the Chairman supplies the Administrator with budget and the Lobbyist with law, the Administrator supplies the Lobbyist with favour and the Chairman with benefits, and the three trades lock into a circuit. Closes when the ring is formed, every corner both giving and receiving, the giving keeping the receiving coming.

**[The Closing](plot_the_closing.md)**
Cue: the ring sits astride the domain's gates. The three, braced together at the committee's gate, the agency's implementation, and the lobby's clientele, govern their policy slice from the inside and shut the outside out. Closes when the subsystem controls its domain, the president and the rival and the public held beyond the corners.

**[The Delivery](plot_the_delivery.md)**
Cue: the ring produces its output. A favourable programme, a protected budget, a friendly rule passes through the circuit, serving the three corners and charged to the outside that funds it and holds no seat. Closes when the ring has delivered for its insiders, the general interest paying the bill it never voted.

**[The Reform](plot_the_reform.md)**
Cue: someone outside would break the ring. The Reformer would open the domain to the president, the budget office, the rival interests, and the press, against three corners each defending the ring that feeds it and each holding a resource the reform needs. Closes when the ring is opened and the circuit broken ... or all three corners hold together once more, the reformer is spent, and the triangle stands.

## Stakes

Whether a policy domain can be governed by responsive representation without closing into a ring that serves only its corners.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: toward the closed circuit, which each corner is right to supply and all is sustained by supplying, and away from the opened domain, which would answer the outside and which asks a reformer to overpower three braced actors at once. The production cannot make the ring answer beyond itself, because each corner rationally supplies the other two and each holds a resource the outsider needs, so the closure is three legitimate roles played well, not a plot: the legislator seeking re-election, the administrator defending a mission, and the lobbyist advocating for members are each only doing the job. It can only show that no one is corrupt, that each corner is playing its proper role and the ring emerges from the sum, and that the one move that opens the domain, subjecting it to the president and the budget office and the rival interests and the press, must be made by beating all three corners together, spending real capital against an entrenched, mutually supporting alliance, and risking the genuine expertise and service tangled in the ring, against every incentive each corner has to hold, so the roles are legitimate and the ring is closed and the structure that runs the domain for its insiders is the one that no single corner built and all three keep.
