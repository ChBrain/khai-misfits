# The Iron Triangle: Reference

The research warrant for [The Iron Triangle](play_the_iron_triangle.md).
The dynamic is the political iron triangle: the subgovernment triad of Cater and Freeman, the defence-contracting
case of Gordon Adams, and Lowi's interest-group liberalism, with Heclo's issue-networks critique credited, all
cited below.

## Line of Work

Staging the iron triangle as a **closed-triad structure**: a legislative committee, an executive agency, and an
organised interest, each commanding a resource the other two need, settling into a closed, mutually reinforcing
exchange that governs their policy domain in their shared interest and resists outside intervention, stable
because each corner rationally supplies the other two. The play models the documented mechanics, the three
resources, authorisation and budget and oversight, programmes and rulings and implementation, electoral support
and organised clientele, the three-way exchange that locks into a self-reinforcing circuit in which each corner's
payoff depends on the others staying in, the closure that holds the domain against the president, the rival, and
the general public, the three-cornered stability that makes the ring iron where a single captured agency would be
breakable, and the outside reform that alone opens it, at the cost of beating all three corners at once, as the
load-bearing vectors of the structure, not as a tale of bribery or conspiracy. The governing finding of the
source is the play's governing law: where a committee, an agency, and an interest each control what the other two
need, they form a stable, closed subsystem that governs its domain in their shared interest and excludes
outsiders, as the equilibrium of three actors each rationally pursuing its own legitimate mandate.

It is distinct from the house's Regulatory Capture, and the distinction is the whole point of the cut: capture is
dyadic, a single regulator coming over time to serve the single industry it was meant to police, one arrow from
watchdog to captor, whereas the iron triangle is triadic, a closed three-cornered circuit whose load-bearing
element is the third corner, the legislature, and the mutual exchange among all three. The triangle's stability
does not come from one body being defanged but from three braced against each other, each holding a resource the
others need and the reformer needs, so it survives the executive pressure that could break a mere capture, and it
is a positive alliance actively producing policy, not only a watchdog that fails to bark. It is also distinct from
the house's Vetocracy, where too many veto points leave a system unable to act: the iron triangle is the opposite
failure, a subsystem that acts decisively and efficiently for its insiders while shutting the outside out, closure
and control rather than gridlock.

## Restrictions

What the house refuses to model, and how it credits what it stages.

- **No villains**: the elements are reduced to load-bearing vectors. The play refuses to cast the corners as
  corrupt or conspiring, because the source finding is precisely that each is playing a legitimate role: the
  legislator seeking re-election and serving the district, the administrator defending a mandated programme and
  its budget, and the lobbyist advocating for paying members are each doing exactly the job the role is meant to
  do. The ring is authored by no one and emerges from three resources each held by one corner and needed by the
  others, so the misfit is that three legitimate roles played well sum to a closed circuit that captures a policy
  domain, no bribery and no conspiracy required for a self-dealing ring to form and hold.
- **No hero**: the play refuses to resolve the misfit into a costless correction. The one move that opens the
  domain is to subject it to the president, the budget office, the rivals, and the press, so the corners must
  answer beyond each other, and it means overpowering three braced actors at once, each defending the ring that
  feeds it and each holding a resource the reform needs, spending real political capital against an entrenched,
  information-rich, mutually supporting alliance, and risking, in the breaking, the genuine expertise and
  constituency service tangled in the ring. The reckoning is a domain opened by beating all three corners
  together, seldom held for long, not a free correction, so the cure for a closed subsystem is a fight against
  three parties only doing their jobs.
- **Credit, not claim**: the research above is other people's work, packaged and attributed here, never claimed
  as the house's, and its reach is not overstated. The staging is the house's; the subgovernment triangle is
  Cater's and Freeman's, the defence-contracting iron triangle and the three-cornered stability are Gordon
  Adams's, the interest-group capture of policy and its public cost are Lowi's, and the honest limit, that in
  many domains the closed triangle has loosened into a more open issue network, is Heclo's, carried as carefully
  as the finding itself: the misfit stages the closed-triad case and credits that it is not the only shape a
  policy subsystem takes.
