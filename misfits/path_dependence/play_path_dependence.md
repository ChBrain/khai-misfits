# Play: Path Dependence

## Estate

[khai-misfits](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-misfits): the house that holds this production and answers for the whole run.

## Name

Path Dependence.
Two ways to do a thing, and a small event, no accounting for it, gives one of them an early lead ... and because the more who use a thing the more worth using it becomes, each new adopter picks the one already ahead, and the lead compounds into a standard, and the standard hardens into a rut, so that when the better way finally arrives it finds a world already built around the first, priced to stay, and unwilling to move alone, and the option a coin-toss chose is the one everyone is stuck with.

## Arc

The bend is this: the best does not win, the first-with-momentum does.
It runs on increasing returns.
The value of a choice rises with the number who share it: a bigger network, more parts and repairs, more people trained, a cheaper product made at scale, so the leader is not just ahead but self-reinforcing, and each rational adopter, picking the option with the most support, is picking the option because it was picked. A small old event, a jammed typewriter, a studio's deal, a wartime rush, decides which option gets the early edge, and merit has little say once the compounding starts.
None of it is error. The adopter is right to bet on the base, the incumbent need only be first and grow, and the better challenger, arriving to a world already standardised, is stranded, because its value needs the users and the users need its value, and it has neither. So the inferior standard locks in, held not by anyone's choice to keep it but by everyone's cost to leave it, and switching would take all of them moving at once.
Coordinate the jump, a sponsor big enough to move the whole crowd, a bridge between the old and the new, and the rut can be escaped: but the move must be paid for, the coordinator may pick the wrong winner on purpose, and often the switch is not worth its cost, or the standard the parable calls inferior was never truly the worse.

## Triggers

**[The Fluke](plot_the_fluke.md)**
Two ways to do a thing stand roughly even, and a small event with no bearing on merit gives one of them the early edge. The Adopter picks it, and the Chronicler notes that nothing about the choice was about which was better. Closes when one option leads for a reason that had nothing to do with being best.

**[The Bandwagon](plot_the_bandwagon.md)**
Cue: the value of a choice rises with the number who share it. The Adopter picks the leader because it leads, and the Incumbent grows cheaper and better simply by being made and used the most, so the edge compounds. Closes when the lead is self-reinforcing and pulling away.

**[The Lock-In](plot_the_lock_in.md)**
Cue: the standard is now built into everything, the training, the tools, the habits. The Captive would take the better option but cannot move to it alone, because its worth waits on everyone moving at once. Closes when the standard is held not by choice but by the cost of leaving it.

**[The Challenger](plot_the_challenger.md)**
Cue: a plainly better way arrives, late. The Challenger wins on the merits and loses on the base, because its value needs adopters and adopters need its value, and the Adopter still bets on the incumbent's crowd. Closes when the superior option is stranded and the inferior one holds.

**[The Switch](plot_the_switch.md)**
Cue: someone moves to coordinate the jump, a sponsor big enough to shift the crowd, a bridge from old to new. The Sponsor tries to move everyone at once, and the Chronicler asks whether the standard was ever really the worse. Closes when the rut is escaped at a price ... or the switch costs more than it saves, and the path holds.

## Stakes

Whether a standard is ever judged on its merits, or only on how many already share it.
Every plot but the last moves the same way: not toward the better option, toward the bigger base, which each new adopter rationally joins and thereby enlarges. The production cannot make a lone user switch to a better standard the rest have not, because a standard is worth only what its crowd is worth. It can only show that no adopter was foolish, that betting on the base is the right bet and the better challenger really was stranded, and that the one thing that would free the crowd, all of it moving at once to the better way, is the very coordination that a scattered market, each rationally staying put, cannot perform, and may not even be right to.
